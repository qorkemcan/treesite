# Do You Need an Arborist Report Before Cutting Large Tree Roots in Florida?

Florida homeowners often notice root problems only after something else goes wrong.

A driveway lifts. A patio starts to crack. A fence post shifts. A plumber, irrigation contractor, or hardscape crew says, “We can just cut those roots out of the way.”

That may sound simple. It is not always simple.

Large roots are not just underground clutter. They help anchor the tree, move water, store energy, and support the trunk during heavy rain and wind. Cutting the wrong roots can turn a landscape repair into a tree-risk question.

## Short Answer

You may not need a formal arborist report for every small root issue, but you should strongly consider one before cutting large roots near the trunk, foundation, driveway, sidewalk, pool deck, septic area, or utility line.

In Florida, root cutting can affect tree stability, storm safety, property damage risk, and sometimes local documentation requirements. An arborist report can help explain whether roots can be pruned safely, whether the work should be avoided, or whether the tree has become a removal candidate.

A casual opinion is not the same as a documented tree-risk assessment.

## Why Large Root Cutting Is Different From Minor Root Cleanup

Small surface roots in a planting bed are one thing. Large structural roots are another.

A homeowner may see a root lifting a paver and think the problem is only cosmetic. But that same root may be part of the tree’s support system, especially if it is close to the trunk or part of the root flare.

Cutting large roots can create several problems:

- the tree may lose some anchoring strength
- decay can start at the cut and move inward
- water and nutrient movement can be reduced
- the canopy may decline months later
- the tree may become less stable in saturated soil or high winds

Florida makes this more serious because trees often face wet-season soil movement, shallow root zones, storm gusts, tight side yards, and hardscape conflicts. A tree that looked stable before root damage may not behave the same way after a major cut.

## When an Arborist Report Is Worth Considering

A full report is not needed for every landscape question. But it becomes more valuable when the decision could affect safety, money, or future responsibility.

Consider getting an arborist report before root cutting if:

- the root is large or close to the trunk
- the tree leans toward a house, driveway, pool cage, road, or neighbor’s property
- roots are being cut for a driveway, sidewalk, patio, pool project, trench, fence, or utility repair
- the tree is mature, storm-exposed, hollow, cracked, or already declining
- the property is in an HOA, historic area, coastal zone, wetland-adjacent lot, or municipality with tree rules
- insurance, permitting, or a neighbor dispute may become part of the situation
- a contractor wants to cut roots quickly without discussing tree stability

The report gives the homeowner something more useful than a verbal “it should be fine.” It creates a record of what was inspected, what risks were visible, and what options were considered.

## What an Arborist Report Should Actually Explain

A helpful arborist report should not be a vague letter that says, “Tree is healthy” or “Tree is hazardous.” It should connect the root problem to the actual site conditions.

A useful report may include:

- tree species and approximate size
- visible condition of the trunk, canopy, root flare, and surrounding soil
- location of the proposed root cutting
- size and number of roots involved
- distance from the trunk or root flare
- nearby targets such as house, driveway, fence, pool cage, utility line, sidewalk, or road
- signs of decay, root plate movement, soil cracking, cavities, conks, or previous damage
- whether root pruning, hardscape redesign, monitoring, or removal is more appropriate
- whether local rules, HOA requirements, or permit review should be checked before work begins

For a homeowner, the most important part is the decision logic. The report should help answer: “Can this root be cut without creating a bigger problem?”

## Root Cutting Near Driveways, Pavers, and Pool Decks

Hardscape conflicts are common in Florida neighborhoods. Roots lift pavers, push against edging, shift pool decks, or make older driveways uneven.

The mistake is treating every hardscape problem like a construction-only issue.

A paver contractor may be focused on resetting the surface. A fence contractor may be focused on getting a post in the ground. An irrigation or plumbing contractor may be focused on access to a line. Those are valid jobs, but the tree may need its own evaluation.

Sometimes the better option is not to cut the root. It may be to adjust the hardscape, bridge over the root zone, change the project layout, use a different surface, or remove the tree before repeated root cutting creates a worse risk.

An arborist report can help separate repair work from tree-risk work.

## Root Cutting Near Utilities or Septic Areas

Roots near utilities create a different kind of problem. Homeowners should not guess where underground lines are. Before digging, trenching, grinding, or cutting around utilities, the location of lines should be confirmed through the proper utility locate process and any current local requirements.

This matters because root cutting and utility work can overlap in risky ways:

- trenching can remove important support roots
- irrigation repairs can disturb the root zone
- septic or drain-field work may require extra planning
- stump grinding near utilities can require careful depth and access control
- buried lines may change what equipment can safely be used

An arborist report does not replace utility locating. It helps explain the tree side of the problem.

## When Root Damage Becomes a Removal Question

Root cutting does not always mean the tree must come down. But sometimes the risk changes enough that removal should be discussed.

That is more likely when:

- multiple large roots have already been cut
- soil is cracking or lifting near a leaning tree
- the root flare is buried, decayed, or girdled
- conks, cavities, carpenter ants, termites, or soft wood are visible near the base
- the tree has heavy lean toward a target
- the canopy is declining after construction or trenching
- the tree is large and growing in a tight space with no room to protect the root zone

The key is not panic. The key is not guessing. A mature Florida tree with root damage deserves a careful look before a homeowner approves more cutting.

## Does Florida Law Require an Arborist Report?

This is where homeowners need to be careful. Requirements can depend on the city, county, property type, HOA rules, tree species, tree size, risk situation, and whether the tree is being pruned, trimmed, removed, or affected by construction.

Florida also has statewide language related to certain residential tree-risk situations, but that does not mean every root-cutting project is automatically exempt from local review. It also does not replace careful documentation when a tree may pose an unacceptable risk.

The safest wording is this: check current local requirements before cutting major roots or removing a tree based on root damage.

If a tree risk decision may affect permits, insurance, HOA approval, or a neighbor dispute, written professional documentation is usually more useful than a text message, a photo, or a quick opinion.

## Questions to Ask Before Approving Root Cutting

Before you let anyone cut large tree roots, ask a few direct questions.

What roots are being cut, and how large are they?

How close are they to the trunk?

Could the work affect the tree’s stability?

Is there a way to repair the hardscape without cutting that root?

Should the tree be inspected before the work starts?

Will photos be taken before and after?

Does the contractor carry the right insurance for the work being performed?

Are utility lines, irrigation, septic components, or drainage features nearby?

Should the city, county, or HOA be checked before the work?

A good contractor should not be offended by these questions. They help clarify the scope before damage happens.

## What Homeowners Should Photograph First

If root cutting is being considered, take photos before the work begins.

Capture:

- the entire tree from multiple angles
- the trunk base and root flare
- the root or roots being discussed
- nearby driveway, pavers, fence, sidewalk, pool cage, or structure
- any cracks in soil or pavement
- existing cavities, conks, ants, termites, sawdust, or bark loss
- the proposed work area before equipment enters the yard

Photos are useful for arborists, contractors, insurance discussions, HOA questions, and future comparison if the tree changes after the work.

## When Professional Help Is Worth It

Professional help is worth it when the root decision could change the safety of the tree or the cost of the property repair.

That does not always mean removal. Sometimes the best outcome is a better hardscape plan, a smaller root-pruning scope, a monitoring plan, or a written explanation that the tree is still reasonably stable.

Other times, the report may confirm that root damage, decay, lean, or structural defects have made the tree a poor candidate for preservation.

Large tree roots should not be treated like weeds in the way of a project.
