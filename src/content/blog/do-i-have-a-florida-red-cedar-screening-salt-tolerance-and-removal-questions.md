# Do I Have a Florida Red Cedar? Screening, Salt Tolerance, and Removal Questions

A Florida red cedar is usually a dense evergreen tree with fine, scale-like foliage, reddish-brown peeling bark, and sometimes blue berry-like cones on female trees. It can be a useful native screening tree, especially in sunny, coastal, or salt-exposed yards.

The real question is not just “is this a red cedar?” It is also: is it planted in the right place?

A red cedar with enough room, good drainage, and full sun can be low-maintenance. A red cedar squeezed against a house, fence, driveway, pool cage, utility area, or narrow side yard can become a different conversation. In that case, homeowners may need selective pruning, clearance, root-zone care, or [tree removal services](/services/tree-removal/) if the tree is declining or badly placed.

## Why Florida homeowners notice red cedars

Red cedars stay green when many other trees thin out, drop leaves, or look seasonal. In Florida yards, they are often used as privacy screens, windbreaks, wildlife cover, or background trees along property lines.

They can also be easy to overlook until they start crowding a fence, shading a narrow side yard, dropping debris into a pool area, or leaning after a storm.

Identification matters because it helps you ask better questions about pruning, spacing, irrigation, storm exposure, and whether the tree is in a good long-term location.

## How to recognize a Florida red cedar

Red cedar is a juniper, even though the common name says cedar. In Florida landscapes, homeowners may hear names like red cedar, eastern red cedar, southern red cedar, or pencil cedar.

Look for:

| Feature | Common clue |
|---|---|
| Foliage | Dense evergreen foliage, often fine or scale-like. |
| Bark | Reddish-brown, fibrous, sometimes peeling in strips. |
| Shape | Pyramidal, oval, columnar, or windswept depending on site. |
| Cones | Small blue or purple-blue berry-like cones on female trees. |
| Site | Often used for screening, windbreaks, or wildlife habitat. |

Peeling bark can be normal on red cedar. It becomes more concerning when it appears with large dead sections, soft wood, trunk cracks, conks, or a worsening lean.

## Screening is useful only when the tree has room

Red cedars can make good screening trees, but a screen still needs space.

A red cedar planted too close to a structure may create problems such as:

- branches rubbing fences or walls,
- crowding a driveway or gate,
- shade and debris near a pool area,
- root-zone stress in narrow beds,
- pruning pressure that ruins natural form,
- storm exposure in a tight target area.

For right-plant-right-place context, see [Florida trees with less aggressive roots](/blog/florida-trees-with-less-aggressive-roots-for-homes-patios-and-driveways/).

## Pruning questions

[Tree trimming services](/services/tree-trimming/) may help when the issue is clearance, dead branches, fence-line growth, driveway visibility, or storm-damaged material.

Avoid shearing a mature red cedar repeatedly into a shape that no longer fits the site. If the tree needs constant heavy cutting to stay out of a roof, pool cage, or driveway, the planting location may be the real problem.

## When removal may be considered

Removal may become more realistic when:

- the tree is severely declining,
- roots or trunk are compromised,
- the tree is too close to a structure,
- it has storm damage,
- it is leaning toward a target,
- repeated pruning cannot solve the space conflict,
- it blocks safe access.

If the tree has moved after a storm, split, or created an urgent hazard, [emergency response services](/services/emergency-response/) may be appropriate. If removal is chosen, ask whether [stump grinding services](/services/stump-grinding/) are included before replanting a new screen.

## Questions to ask

Ask:

- Is this definitely a red cedar or another juniper?
- Is the peeling bark normal for this tree?
- Is the tree in enough sun and drainage?
- Is it planted too close to a structure or fence?
- Would pruning solve the problem without overcutting?
- Is the tree declining or just messy?
- If removed, what should replace it?
