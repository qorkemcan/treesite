# Debris Cleanup After a Major Storm: Hiring Professionals

Storm debris is not one category.

A pile may be ordinary vegetative waste, the lower end of a still-attached limb, utility-related material, building debris, flood-contaminated material, or wood resting under stored tension.

Before hiring anyone, identify **what must be made safe, who is responsible, what will be hauled, and what can go to municipal collection**.

## Use this debris-classification table

| Debris or condition | Likely work type | Who may need to be involved |
|---|---|---|
| Small settled branches fully on ground | Routine yard cleanup or municipal vegetative pickup | Owner, landscaper, hauler, or local collection program |
| Large detached logs in open area | Cutting, loading, hauling, equipment access | Tree service or debris contractor |
| Partly attached or hanging wood | Active tree hazard | Qualified tree crew or emergency response |
| Tree or limb on roof or structure | Tree stabilization plus building assessment | Tree crew, roofer, insurer, or structural professional |
| Material touching power line | Electrical hazard | Utility or emergency services first |
| Debris in public right-of-way | Public access and ownership issue | Local government, utility, or responsible owner |
| Flood-soaked or contaminated debris | Health and disposal issue | Local emergency or waste guidance |
| Mixed building and vegetative debris | Separate disposal streams may apply | Municipality, insurer, restoration contractor, hauler |
| Stump and root plate | Grinding, excavation, or removal scope | Stump service, utility locate, site contractor |

## First decide whether the debris is stable

Do not begin dragging or cutting when debris is:

- still attached
- hanging overhead
- resting in another tree
- supporting a larger piece
- loaded against a roof or fence
- near a power line
- covering a root-plate failure
- blocking access beneath unstable wood

Use [Caring for Trees After a Storm](/blog/caring-for-trees-after-a-storm-what-to-inspect-first/) before cleanup begins.

## Public pickup and private hauling are different

Florida cities and counties may issue storm-specific rules for:

- vegetative debris
- construction and demolition debris
- household hazardous waste
- curb placement
- pile separation
- right-of-way access
- collection dates
- gated or private roads
- contractor-generated debris

Confirm the current local instructions before paying to move material to the curb.

A private crew’s quote should state whether debris will be:

- cut and left
- stacked for public pickup
- chipped
- hauled off-site
- moved to another part of the property
- loaded into a container
- separated by material type

“Cleanup included” is too vague.

## Utility responsibility comes first

Do not hire a tree crew to move material that may be energized.

Keep away from:

- downed conductors
- service drops
- damaged meters
- tree contact with wires
- wet fences or vehicles near electrical damage
- arcing or smoking equipment

Use [What to Do If a Tree Is Touching Power Lines](/blog/what-to-do-if-a-tree-is-touching-power-lines-after-a-storm/) for the utility-first process.

## Structure-loaded wood needs a joint plan

When a tree rests on a roof, carport, pool cage, shed, vehicle, or fence, removing one piece may transfer load elsewhere.

The written plan should identify:

- who controls the tree
- who evaluates the structure
- where pieces will be lowered
- whether temporary stabilization is required
- what roofing or restoration work is excluded
- how interior exposure will be protected
- who documents pre-existing and storm damage
- whether the insurer has requested photographs or inspection

Tree work and building repair are separate scopes.

## Document before the pile changes

Photograph:

- entire scene
- source tree
- attachment points
- structure contact
- utility proximity
- access blockage
- property damage
- pile size
- equipment route
- condition before cutting or hauling

Do not delay emergency work solely to take photographs.

## Questions to ask the cleanup provider

1. Is all material fully detached and stable?
2. Does the job include active tree work?
3. Are utility or structure hazards excluded until cleared?
4. Is cutting included?
5. Is chipping included?
6. Is hauling included?
7. Where will debris go?
8. Are municipal pickup piles allowed?
9. Is stump work separate?
10. What equipment and access are required?
11. How will lawns, drives, septic, irrigation, and gates be protected?
12. What restoration is excluded?

Use [Tree Removal Cleanup: What Should Be Included in the Quote?](/blog/tree-removal-cleanup-what-should-be-included-in-the-quote/) for scope comparison.

## Access restoration is not always full cleanup

An emergency visit may only reopen:

- driveway
- entrance
- road
- utility access
- emergency route

The remaining scope may still include:

- complete takedown
- hazardous canopy work
- hauling
- stump grinding
- root-plate cleanup
- fence or roof coordination
- grading and landscape repair

Ask for both the immediate and final scopes in writing.
