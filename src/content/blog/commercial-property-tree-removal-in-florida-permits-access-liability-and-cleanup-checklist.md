# Commercial Property Tree Removal in Florida: Permits, Access, Liability, and Cleanup Checklist

A commercial tree-removal plan should protect **people, access, operations, and the final site**.

The core questions are:

1. Who has authority to approve the work?
2. What local or site-plan requirements apply?
3. How will customers, tenants, employees, vehicles, and emergency routes be protected?
4. What equipment and utility constraints shape the method?
5. What must be complete before the property reopens?

## Use this commercial operations table

| Operating issue | What the work plan should state |
|---|---|
| Customer or tenant entrance | Closure, alternate route, and reopening time |
| ADA route | Accessible detour or work scheduled when closure is permitted |
| Fire lane | Must remain available or be coordinated under an approved plan |
| Parking | Spaces blocked, towing or move notice, equipment staging |
| Deliveries | Time window and alternate loading access |
| Public sidewalk | Local approval, barriers, and pedestrian detour |
| Utility area | Utility coordination and qualified line-clearance work |
| Landscape island | Curb, irrigation, lighting, and underground-line protection |
| Debris | Same-day hauling or approved temporary staging |
| Stump area | Grinding, private utilities, fill, barricade, and final surface |
| Business reopening | Walkthrough, clear routes, no unstable wood or trip hazards |

## Confirm authority and site status

Before quoting, identify whether the tree is:

- on the commercial parcel
- in a public right-of-way
- within a shared easement
- part of a master association
- in a tenant-controlled patio
- inside a required landscape buffer
- beside a stormwater facility
- within a preserve or wetland area
- shown on an approved site plan
- maintained under a lease or property-management agreement

The person approving the work should have written authority under the ownership, lease, management, or association documents.

## Commercial permits and landscape obligations

Florida tree rules vary by jurisdiction.

A commercial property may need review for:

- tree-removal permit
- protected or native tree
- replacement or mitigation
- landscape-island requirements
- required buffer
- site-plan compliance
- public sidewalk or lane closure
- right-of-way work
- stormwater or preserve area
- redevelopment conditions

Florida Statute 163.045 defines residential property through a qualifying single-family detached use. It should not be assumed to apply to a shopping center, office, hotel, warehouse, mixed-use site, or ordinary commercial parcel.

## Plan the work zone before the equipment arrives

Map:

- tree and drop zone
- entrances
- fire lane
- ADA route
- sidewalks
- customer parking
- tenant parking
- loading area
- drive-through lane
- outdoor seating
- dumpsters and service yard
- signage
- overhead lines
- underground facilities
- crane or lift position
- chipper and log truck
- debris staging
- alternate pedestrian and vehicle routes

A tree in a small parking island can require more control than a larger tree in an open rear landscape area.

## Utilities

Tree work near energized lines is specialized and potentially fatal.

When a tree or equipment could enter an electrical hazard area:

- contact the utility
- identify whether qualified line-clearance personnel are required
- keep customers and staff away
- do not rely on visual clearance alone

Before stump grinding or excavation, address:

- public/member utilities through the appropriate 811 process
- private irrigation
- parking-lot lighting
- sign power
- security and communications
- private electric
- water and sewer
- stormwater pipes
- fountain or pool systems

## Contractor documents

Request and verify:

- legal business name
- written scope
- general liability insurance
- workers’ compensation coverage or exemption status applicable to the business and work
- subcontractor coverage
- commercial auto where required
- equipment operators
- crane or lift documents where relevant
- additional-insured or certificate-holder wording required by contract
- local registration where applicable
- safety and work-zone plan
- change-order process

Florida workers’ compensation requirements vary by industry, employee count, business organization, and exemptions. Verify the provider’s actual status rather than assuming one threshold fits every tree company.

## Define the commercial tree scope

| Scope category | Required detail |
|---|---|
| Tree work | Exact tree, condition, pruning or removal |
| Method | Climbing, rigging, lift, crane, controlled drop |
| Schedule | Before hours, overnight, phased, or emergency |
| Access | Parking closures, gates, loading, traffic control |
| Protection | Buildings, signs, lights, curbs, pavers, vehicles |
| Approvals | Permit, site-plan, association, right-of-way |
| Debris | Hauling, temporary staging, logs, chips |
| Stump | Depth/result, roots, utilities, fill, barricade |
| Restoration | Mulch, sod, irrigation, replacement planting |
| Reopening | Final inspection and routes returned to service |

## Emergency commercial response

After storm failure, prioritize:

1. life safety
2. electrical hazards
3. fire and emergency access
4. occupied entrances
5. tree on a building or vehicle
6. hanging wood over customer routes
7. documentation
8. stabilization
9. complete removal and hauling
10. stump and landscape restoration

Emergency stabilization may reopen one entrance while another part of the job remains pending.

Do not describe a site as fully restored when unstable wood, open pits, debris, or blocked accessible routes remain.

## Debris and reopening

Confirm:

- whether all logs leave
- whether debris remains overnight
- where storm debris can be staged
- whether contractor debris is accepted by municipal collection
- whether parking reopens the same day
- whether sidewalks are swept or blown
- whether sawdust is removed from drains
- whether outdoor dining and customer areas are cleaned
- whether a manager signs off before barriers are removed

## Stump restoration

A commercial stump area may intersect:

- ADA route
- curb
- irrigation
- sign bed
- lighting circuit
- drainage
- customer walkway
- parking island
- required replacement planting

Define the final result. A pile of grindings and a cone may not satisfy the operating-site need.
