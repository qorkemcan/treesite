# Bagworms on Florida Trees: What Homeowners Should Do First

Bagworms live inside individual hanging cases made from silk and pieces of the host plant.

UF/IFAS describes common bagworm as an occasional Florida pest. A few reachable bags on a healthy shrub usually call for identification and hand removal—not tree removal.

The response changes when a small evergreen is heavily defoliated, bags are high and unsafe to reach, or dead branches create a separate pruning problem.

## Use this bagworm table

| What you see | Practical response |
|---|---|
| A few bags on a small reachable shrub | Hand-pick and dispose of the bags |
| Old empty-looking bags but no new damage | Monitor for active larvae or new small bags |
| Many small active bags and fresh feeding | Seek timely pest-management guidance |
| Evergreen has heavy browning or repeated defoliation | Evaluate recovery versus replacement |
| Bags are high in a large tree | Do not climb; treatment may be unnecessary or professional access may be needed |
| Branch is already dead or broken over a target | Address the branch condition through pruning |

## Bagworms versus webworms

| Bagworms | Webworms |
|---|---|
| Individual hanging cases | Larger webbed nests |
| Case is covered with leaf or needle pieces | Web encloses leaves and caterpillars |
| Caterpillar remains inside its case | Caterpillars feed inside the expanding web |
| Often noticed on fine-textured evergreens | Often noticed at deciduous branch tips |

Use [Webs in Trees in Florida](/blog/webs-in-trees-in-florida-webworms-tent-caterpillars-or-spider-webs/) when the symptom is a large web rather than separate hanging bags.

## Check whether the infestation is active

Old bags can remain attached after the feeding period.

Look for:

- new small bags
- caterpillar movement
- fresh chewing
- expanding browning
- bags increasing in number
- current damage on branch tips

Take close photographs before applying a product.

## Hand removal

Hand-picking can work well when:

- plant is small
- bags are reachable from the ground
- only a limited number are present
- no electrical or branch hazard exists
- inspections can be repeated

Remove the entire bag and dispose of it. Do not leave it attached to the plant.

Do not use a ladder or climb a tree for bagworm removal.

## Why timing matters

Young larvae are generally easier to manage than mature larvae inside large cases.

Before treatment:

- confirm the insect
- determine whether it is active
- identify the host plant
- check the product label
- protect water, people, pets, pollinators, and beneficial insects
- avoid treating a large canopy with homeowner equipment

A product recommendation should be based on current life stage and label directions.

## Which plants can be damaged more severely?

Heavy feeding can be more consequential on:

- small ornamentals
- newly planted plants
- stressed plants
- fine-textured evergreens
- plants with repeated defoliation

A mature deciduous tree may tolerate limited feeding better than a small evergreen that loses a large share of its foliage.

## When trimming may help

Pruning may make sense when:

- branch is already dead
- damage is confined to a small branch tip
- clearance is needed
- deadwood is over a roof, driveway, or walkway
- the plant requires limited shaping after the pest is managed

Do not remove large healthy limbs merely to eliminate a few bags.

Visit [tree trimming services](/services/tree-trimming/) when the affected branch is high, dead, broken, or over a target.

## When replacement may be more practical

For a small ornamental, replacement can be considered when:

- most foliage is gone
- dead sections will not refill
- infestation is repeated
- plant is poorly suited to the site
- restoration cost exceeds plant value

A few bagworms on a large structurally sound tree are not a removal reason.

## What not to do

Avoid:

- burning bags or branches
- random pesticide use
- treating old inactive cases
- over-pruning
- unsafe ladder work
- assuming every brown branch is bagworm damage
- removing a healthy tree because of a minor infestation

## What to photograph

Send:

1. full plant
2. close-up of the cases
3. new versus old-looking bags
4. feeding damage
5. branch browning
6. neighboring plants
7. irrigation and site stress
8. any dead branch over a target
