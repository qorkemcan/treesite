# Do You Need a Permit to Remove a Tree in Florida? What Property Owners Need to Know

There is no single yes-or-no answer for every Florida property.

A permit may be required when a city or county regulates the tree, the tree is protected, the work is tied to construction, the tree is in a right-of-way, or the property falls outside the hazardous-tree protection in Florida Statute 163.045. A local permit may not be required when a qualifying residential property owner possesses the professional documentation described by that statute and the tree poses an unacceptable risk to persons or property.

HOA approval, mangrove rules, utility control, wetland restrictions, and ownership questions can still exist even when an ordinary local tree permit does not. The safest first step is to identify the property jurisdiction and the reason for removal before scheduling [tree removal services](/services/tree-removal/).

## Use this permit decision path

### 1. Is anyone in immediate danger?

If a tree is on a home, blocking emergency access, actively splitting, or touching a power line, keep people away. Call 911 or the electric utility as appropriate before treating the situation like an ordinary permit question.

Emergency stabilization and routine removal are not always the same job. [Emergency response services](/services/emergency-response/) may first make the area safer, while final removal, hauling, and [stump grinding services](/services/stump-grinding/) may be handled later.

### 2. Is the tree on your parcel?

Check whether the trunk is fully inside your property, on a boundary, in an HOA common area, in a public right-of-way, in a utility easement, or beside a canal, shoreline, wetland, or preserve. Ownership and control may determine who can authorize the work.

### 3. Is the tree hazardous, or is the removal for preference?

Hazardous-tree documentation is different from a preference-based removal, landscaping project, view opening, construction plan, or lot-clearing project. If you plan to rely on Florida Statute 163.045, read [Florida Statute 163.045: What Homeowners Should Understand Before Tree Removal](/blog/florida-statute-163-045-what-homeowners-should-understand-before-tree-removal/) and [Do You Need an Arborist Report to Remove a Hazardous Tree in Florida?](/blog/do-you-need-an-arborist-report-to-remove-a-hazardous-tree-in-florida/).

### 4. Which local rules apply?

Florida has statewide statutes, but cities and counties still manage many tree, land-development, right-of-way, environmental, and mitigation rules.

| Rule area | What to confirm |
|---|---|
| City or county tree permit | Whether the species, size, or location is regulated. |
| Protected tree | Whether special review applies. |
| Right-of-way | Whether the tree is controlled by a public authority. |
| HOA or association | Whether written approval is required. |
| Mangroves or wetlands | Whether environmental rules apply. |
| Construction | Whether removal is tied to site work or mitigation. |

Verify current requirements directly with the city, county, HOA, or property authority before work begins.

## Commercial and land-clearing projects

Commercial sites, associations, apartment properties, development parcels, and larger clearing projects may have different rules from a single-family homeowner removing one hazardous tree. For businesses, rental properties, or managed communities, [commercial tree services](/services/commercial-services/) coordination may help with access, notices, documentation, and scheduling. For multiple trees, brush, or lot-prep work, [land clearing services](/services/land-clearing/) may be the better category.
