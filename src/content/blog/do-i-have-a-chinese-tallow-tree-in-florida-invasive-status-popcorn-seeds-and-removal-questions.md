# Do I Have a Chinese Tallow Tree in Florida? Invasive Status, Popcorn Seeds, and Removal Questions

Chinese tallow, currently accepted as *Triadica sebifera* and also known by the older name *Sapium sebiferum*, is listed as **Prohibited** throughout Florida.

The white wax-coated seeds explain the common name “popcorn tree,” but identification should also use the leaves, petiole glands, fruit capsules, bark, and whole-tree form.

Removal planning must address three different sources of return: the living stump and roots, seedlings from existing seed, and new seed-bearing debris.

## Use a complete identification set

| Feature | Chinese tallow clue |
|---|---|
| Leaves | Alternate, broadly diamond- to heart-shaped leaves with a long pointed tip. |
| Petiole | Small glands may be visible near the leaf-blade attachment. |
| Color | Yellow, orange, red, or purple color may appear. |
| Fruit | Three-lobed capsules. |
| Seed | White wax-coated seeds exposed after capsules open. |
| Bark | Gray-brown, becoming furrowed with age. |
| Site | Disturbed or wet areas are common. |

A red leaf or white seed alone is not proof. Take photographs of the whole tree, complete leaf and petiole, both leaf surfaces, fruit, seed, bark, base, and nearby seedlings. Use [Florida Tree Identification for Homeowners](/blog/florida-tree-identification-for-homeowners-common-yard-trees-and-what-they-can-tell-you/) when uncertain.

## Removal planning

| Return source | What to plan for |
|---|---|
| Stump and roots | Cut-stump control and follow-up may be needed. |
| Seedlings | Monitor after removal. |
| Seed-bearing debris | Contain and dispose of material responsibly. |
| Wet or disturbed site | Verify site authority and access. |
| Replacement planting | Choose a better species for the site. |
