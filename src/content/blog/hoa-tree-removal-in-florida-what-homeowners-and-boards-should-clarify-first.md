# HOA Tree Removal in Florida: What Homeowners and Boards Should Clarify First

The first HOA tree question is not “How much will removal cost?”

It is:

> Who controls the tree and who has authority to approve the work?

The trunk may be on:

- a private parcel
- HOA common area
- an easement
- a preserve or buffer
- a lake or drainage edge
- a public right-of-way
- neighboring property
- a landscape area required by a development approval

The answer should be documented before a homeowner or board signs a tree-service estimate.

## Use this HOA control table

| Tree location | First authority check |
|---|---|
| Fully on a private parcel | Declaration, architectural rules, owner responsibility |
| HOA common area | Board or authorized association representative |
| Preserve, conservation, or buffer | Governing documents and applicable agency/local review |
| Drainage or utility easement | Easement rights and responsible entity |
| Public right-of-way | City or county |
| Required entrance or street landscaping | Approved plan and replacement requirements |
| Neighbor property | Neighbor authorization and property-line facts |
| Location unclear | Survey, plat, association records, and legal guidance |

## Identify the trunk and the recorded property area

Collect:

- survey or plat
- association map
- declaration
- easement documents
- preserve documents
- approved landscape plans
- right-of-way information
- photographs showing trunk and nearby targets

Overhanging branches do not by themselves establish ownership or authority to remove the entire tree.

## Read the governing documents

Look for:

- common-area maintenance
- private-lot landscaping
- architectural approval
- tree-removal restrictions
- replacement planting
- entry and street-tree standards
- preserves and buffers
- contractor requirements
- emergency authority
- owner notice
- board approval
- restoration responsibility

Florida Statutes Chapter 720 provides the statutory framework for homeowners’ associations. It does not replace the specific declaration, plats, easements, rules, and property facts.

## Separate preference from maintenance or risk

A request may be based on:

- leaf litter
- shade
- blocked view
- roots
- roof clearance
- deadwood
- documented decay
- storm damage
- active failure
- required landscape redesign

The board should record the actual reason.

Leaves, shade, or a view concern do not automatically justify removal of a common-area tree. A new lean, opening crack, root movement, or falling major wood requires a different response.

## Approval workflow

| Step | HOA record |
|---|---|
| 1. Locate and classify tree | Map, survey, or marked photo |
| 2. Identify request | Owner submission or manager inspection |
| 3. Determine authority | Declaration, rules, board or owner responsibility |
| 4. Evaluate condition | Photos and professional assessment if appropriate |
| 5. Check local rules | Permit, protected tree, replacement, right-of-way |
| 6. Approve work | Written owner or association authorization |
| 7. Select provider | Scope and insurance documents |
| 8. Notify affected owners | Access, parking, noise, closure |
| 9. Complete work | Removal, hauling, stump, restoration |
| 10. Close records | Invoice, permit, photos, replacement documentation |

## Florida Statute 163.045

Florida Statute 163.045 creates a specific pathway for qualifying residential property when proper risk documentation and statutory conditions exist.

The statute defines residential property as a qualifying single-family detached building used for single-family residential purposes.

In an HOA community, that does not answer:

- whether the tree is on the homeowner’s parcel
- whether the association controls the tree
- whether governing documents require approval
- whether the tree lies in a preserve or right-of-way
- whether common-area property qualifies
- whether another environmental rule applies

Do not use the statute as a substitute for establishing control and location.

## Local government requirements

Check for:

- city or county permit
- protected or specimen tree
- replacement or mitigation
- right-of-way approval
- wetland or mangrove rules
- preserve restrictions
- development-order landscaping
- emergency documentation

HOA approval and municipal approval are separate questions.

## Emergency HOA tree work

For a tree on a home, blocked community road, hanging major limb, active split, electrical contact, or root-plate movement:

1. keep people away
2. contact 911 and the utility as appropriate
3. identify whether the area is private or association controlled
4. authorize the minimum work needed to stabilize the hazard
5. photograph before and after when safe
6. preserve insurer and contractor records
7. create a separate full-removal and restoration scope

Emergency access does not automatically settle who ultimately pays.

## Contractor and scope review

Request:

- exact tree and location
- approved work type
- general liability insurance
- applicable workers’ compensation documentation
- subcontractor information
- equipment route
- resident and traffic controls
- utility coordination
- debris and hauling
- stump and roots
- fill and landscape restoration
- replacement tree
- change orders
- final photos

Use [What Should Be in a Written Tree Removal Estimate?](/blog/what-should-be-in-a-written-tree-removal-estimate/) for scope language.

## Final-site responsibilities

Define who handles:

- logs
- branches
- curb staging
- stump grinding
- chip removal
- clean fill
- irrigation and lighting
- sod or mulch
- pavers and curbs
- replacement planting
- watering and establishment
- final inspection

A board should not approve “remove tree” without knowing how the shared area will be left.
