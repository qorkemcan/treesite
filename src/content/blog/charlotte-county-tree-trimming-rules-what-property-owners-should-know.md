# Charlotte County Tree Trimming Rules: What Property Owners Should Know

A lot of homeowners assume tree trimming is simple.

If the tree is on their property, they assume they can cut it however they want. If the canopy feels too low, too wide, or too close to the roof, they call someone to “take it back hard” and move on.

In Charlotte County, that mindset can create two problems. The first is a code or compliance problem if the work becomes more than ordinary trimming or affects a specially protected tree. The second is a tree-health and safety problem if the cutting is so aggressive that it damages the structure of the tree and creates bigger storm risk later.

Tree trimming should be treated as both a maintenance issue and a standards issue.

## The key rule: no permit does not mean anything goes

For many ordinary trees on private property, routine pruning may not require the same process as removal. But “permit not required” does not mean “cut however you want.”

Routine trimming is different from:

- topping,
- hat-racking,
- removing too much live canopy,
- cutting a protected tree improperly,
- creating a future storm hazard,
- trimming near power lines,
- trimming so aggressively that the tree is functionally destroyed.

[Tree trimming services](/services/tree-trimming/) should follow proper pruning practice, not just remove as much canopy as possible.

## What to verify before trimming

| Situation | Safer first step |
|---|---|
| Routine deadwood or light clearance | Confirm proper pruning scope. |
| Major live-canopy reduction | Ask whether the work may become regulated or damaging. |
| Protected or specimen tree | Verify current local rule first. |
| Storm-damaged limb | Document and consider urgency. |
| Limb near utility lines | Contact the utility or appropriate authority. |
| HOA or common-area tree | Confirm authority before work. |
