# Condo Association Tree Removal in Florida: Common Elements, Board Approval, and Storm Cleanup

A condominium tree decision should begin with **property classification and association authority**.

Before requesting removal, identify whether the trunk is located within:

- a common element
- a limited common element
- a unit-controlled area
- association property
- an easement
- a right-of-way
- a preserve or regulated buffer
- neighboring property

Florida Statute 718.113 states that maintenance of common elements is the association’s responsibility, except for limited-common-element maintenance assigned to a unit owner by the declaration. That rule is an important starting point, but the declaration, plats, site plan, and specific facts still control the practical workflow.

## Use this condo decision table

| Tree location or condition | First document or action |
|---|---|
| Common lawn, parking area, courtyard, or entrance | Declaration, site plan, board authority |
| Limited common element near one unit | Declaration’s assigned maintenance responsibility |
| Tree appears inside a unit boundary | Recorded documents and association counsel if unclear |
| Right-of-way or utility area | Local government or utility approval |
| Preserve, wetland, mangrove, or buffer | Applicable environmental and local review |
| Stable planned removal | Board approval, permit review, written scope |
| Tree on building or blocking fire access | Emergency closure and stabilization |
| Resident request based on leaves, shade, or view | Separate preference from documented maintenance or risk need |

## Classify the tree before obtaining a final quote

The trunk location should be mapped against:

- declaration
- condominium plat
- survey or site plan
- limited-common-element assignments
- easements
- landscape plans
- preserve documents
- right-of-way maps
- utility plans

Branches and roots can extend beyond the controlling property area. Their location does not automatically transfer authority over the trunk.

When documents conflict or the proposed work may affect legal rights, association counsel should guide the authority question.

## Maintenance, removal, and alteration are not identical

Routine trimming may qualify as ordinary maintenance.

Complete removal may raise additional questions when the tree:

- is part of an approved landscape plan
- provides required screening
- affects common-area appearance
- is tied to replacement obligations
- lies within a protected area
- creates a material landscape change
- serves multiple units
- is disputed by owners

The board should document whether the action is:

- routine maintenance
- risk reduction
- emergency mitigation
- complete removal
- landscape alteration
- restoration after storm damage

A trimming approval should not be treated as blanket approval for removal.

## Board approval checklist

| Decision item | Record to keep |
|---|---|
| Tree identity and location | Map, marked photo, or site-plan reference |
| Reason for work | Maintenance, risk, damage, access, or replacement |
| Professional input | Arborist or landscape-architect documentation if used |
| Local approval | Permit, written response, or exemption basis |
| Board authority | Minutes, resolution, or authorized-manager approval |
| Resident impact | Parking, walkway, balcony, pool, and noise notice |
| Contractor review | Estimate, insurance, coverage documents, subcontractors |
| Final site | Debris, stump, fill, turf, irrigation, replacement planting |

## Local permits and Florida Statute 163.045

Association approval does not replace local government requirements.

Check for:

- protected-tree rules
- replacement planting
- right-of-way permits
- commercial or multifamily landscape-plan compliance
- wetland or coastal review
- storm-debris rules
- hazardous-tree documentation

Florida Statute 163.045 uses a narrow single-family detached definition of residential property. A condominium association should not assume that common-element trees fall within that pathway.

When the statute, local ordinance, or ownership classification is unclear, obtain professional legal or municipal guidance before removal.

## Resident and common-area safety

Tree work may affect:

- sidewalks
- parking spaces
- balconies
- pool decks
- courtyards
- carports
- entrances
- roads
- fire lanes
- mail areas
- utility rooms

Before planned work, communicate:

- date and work window
- spaces to clear
- walkways and amenities closed
- balcony restrictions
- pet and child precautions
- equipment route
- debris staging area
- expected reopening time
- rain-delay plan
- association contact

A serious drop zone needs physical control and active management, not only a small sign.

## Emergency storm response

When a tree is on a building, loading a carport, blocking the only entrance, or threatening an occupied area:

1. keep residents away
2. call 911 and the utility as appropriate
3. close affected parking and walkways
4. photograph the condition when safe
5. authorize the minimum work needed to stabilize the hazard
6. preserve the removed sections or photographs when an insurer requests documentation
7. create a separate scope for complete removal and restoration

Emergency mitigation may not include:

- all hauling
- complete stump work
- pool-deck cleanup
- sod
- irrigation repair
- replacement planting

## Contractor and work-zone requirements

Request:

- written estimate
- general liability certificate
- applicable workers’ compensation documentation
- subcontractor information
- equipment and access plan
- utility coordination
- resident-control plan
- debris and hauling plan
- stump and restoration plan
- change-order process

Verify coverage and contract requirements for the specific association and provider rather than relying on a generic statement that every business has the same insurance obligation.

## Cleanup, stump, and restoration

The association should define:

- branches and logs hauled
- temporary storm staging
- contractor debris responsibility
- sidewalk and parking cleanup
- sawdust and chips
- stump depth or result
- surface roots
- private irrigation and lighting
- clean fill
- sod or mulch
- paver or curb damage
- replacement planting
- final walkthrough

A stump in a common walkway, dog area, lawn, or courtyard is a different final condition from a stump in a closed landscape buffer.
