# What Are Conks on a Tree Trunk, and When Are They a Serious Warning Sign?

A conk is a hard, shelf-like or mushroom-like growth that some decay fungi produce on a tree trunk, root flare, or old wound. Seeing one does not automatically mean the tree will fall today. But in Florida, where wind, saturated soil, palms, oaks, and older shade trees all complicate risk, a conk deserves attention.

The most concerning conks are near the base of the tree, on the lower trunk, around the root flare, or on a tree that is already leaning, hollow, cracking, shedding large limbs, or thinning in the canopy.

A conk can be a visible sign of hidden internal decay. The outside of the tree may still look green. That is what makes it easy to underestimate.

## What a conk is

A conk is the fruiting body of a fungus. It is the part you can see.

The more important activity may be happening inside the wood, where fungi can break down decayed or weakened tissue.

Conks can look like:

- a hard shelf attached to the trunk,
- a bracket-shaped growth near the root flare,
- a flat, woody mushroom,
- a layered growth on old wounds or pruning cuts,
- a reddish, brown, tan, white, gray, or varnished-looking surface,
- a rounded knob or hoof-like mass on older wood.

A conk is not surface dirt or random decoration. It is connected to fungal activity in or around the tree.

## Why conks matter in Florida yards

Florida trees already deal with a hard mix of stressors: heavy summer rain, saturated soil, hurricane-season wind, heat, humidity, construction pressure, irrigation changes, and shallow root conflicts near driveways, pool decks, pavers, and sidewalks.

A tree with internal decay may stand quietly for years. Then a storm, saturated root zone, or heavy limb can expose the weakness.

That is why conks near targets matter more.

A conk on a tree in a back corner of a large open lot is different from a conk on a tree over a bedroom, driveway, sidewalk, pool cage, or neighbor’s fence.

## When conks are more concerning

Conks deserve faster attention when they appear with:

| Sign | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Leaning | Decay plus lean can change the risk picture. |
| Trunk cracks | Structural wood may already be compromised. |
| Cavities | Internal wood may be missing or weakened. |
| Large dead limbs | The tree may be declining more broadly. |
| Soil movement | Root stability may be involved. |
| Carpenter ants or termites | Insects may be using already damaged wood. |
| Storm damage | Wind may have worsened an existing weakness. |

## What not to do

Avoid:

- knocking conks off to “fix” the tree,
- sealing the area with paint, tar, foam, or concrete,
- cutting into the trunk to see how deep decay goes,
- assuming the tree is safe because the canopy is green,
- standing under large limbs to inspect the fungus,
- ignoring a conk near the base of a tree over a target.

Removing the conk does not remove the internal decay.

## Does a conk always mean removal?

No. A conk does not automatically mean the tree must be removed.

The decision depends on:

- tree species,
- conk location,
- trunk condition,
- root condition,
- canopy health,
- lean,
- nearby targets,
- storm exposure,
- whether decay has compromised load-bearing wood.

Some trees may be monitored or pruned. Others may need [tree removal services](/services/tree-removal/), especially if decay is near the base and the tree can hit a structure or high-use area.

If the tree is leaning, cracking, dropping limbs, or moving after a storm, [emergency response services](/services/emergency-response/) may be appropriate.

## Questions to ask

Ask:

- What type of conk or fungal growth is visible?
- Is it near the base, trunk, root flare, or a limb?
- Is there evidence of internal decay?
- Is the tree leaning or cracking?
- Are major limbs over a target?
- Would [tree trimming services](/services/tree-trimming/) reduce risk, or is removal more realistic?
- Should the area be kept clear until the tree is evaluated?
