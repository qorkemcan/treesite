# Florida Statute 163.045: What Homeowners Should Understand Before Tree Removal

Florida Statute 163.045 is **not** blanket permission to remove any tree on private property without a permit.

The law limits local-government requirements in a narrower situation: a qualifying residential property owner possesses the professional documentation described by the statute, and that documentation shows the tree poses an unacceptable risk to persons or property.

When those conditions are not met—or the tree, property, or work falls into another regulated category—the ordinary local permit and review questions may still apply.

## The four conditions homeowners should remember

A useful plain-English reading of the law begins with four questions.

### 1. Is this the kind of residential property covered by the statute?

The statute defines residential property as a single-family detached building on a lot actively used for single-family residential purposes, with a conforming or legally recognized nonconforming use under local land-development rules.

Do not automatically extend that definition to:

- commercial property
- apartment or condominium property
- HOA common areas
- vacant development parcels
- institutional property
- every mixed-use or multifamily site

Property type should be confirmed before relying on the law.

### 2. Does the owner possess the required documentation?

The documentation must be an onsite assessment completed under the tree-risk procedures referenced in the law and signed by either:

- an ISA Certified Arborist, or
- a Florida-licensed landscape architect

A tree company’s verbal opinion, an unsigned note, a remote photo review, or a homeowner’s own description is not the same thing.

See [Do You Need an Arborist Report to Remove a Hazardous Tree in Florida?](/blog/do-you-need-an-arborist-report-to-remove-a-hazardous-tree-in-florida/) for a document checklist.

### 3. Does the tree pose an unacceptable risk?

The law is about risk to persons or property—not general dislike, shade, leaf drop, appearance, or a future landscaping plan.

The statute defines unacceptable risk in relation to recognized tree-risk assessment procedures and whether removal is the only practical way to reduce the risk below a moderate level.

That is more demanding than saying a tree “could fall someday.” Every tree carries some level of uncertainty. The documentation should explain why this tree, in this location, presents the relevant level of risk.

### 4. Is the work outside a separately controlled category?

Section 163.045 does not erase every other layer of regulation or private control.

A homeowner may still need to examine:

- mangrove rules
- right-of-way or utility control
- construction and site-plan approvals
- wetlands or conservation restrictions
- HOA covenants and architectural review
- ownership and boundary issues
- rules for public or shared trees

The state statute and those separate issues should not be blended together.

## What the law prevents a local government from requiring

When the statutory conditions are satisfied, the law says a local government may not require notice, application, approval, a permit, a fee, or mitigation for the pruning, trimming, or removal of the covered tree.

That is the part often summarized online as “no permit required.”

The missing half of that sentence is important: **the homeowner must first be within the covered statutory situation and possess the required documentation**.

A social-media post, contractor statement, or neighborhood example does not establish that your property qualifies.

## What the law does not say

The statute does not say:

- every tree on private property may be removed freely
- every leaning tree is an unacceptable risk
- a tree-service estimate is a qualifying risk assessment
- local tree rules have disappeared
- commercial and multifamily property are treated the same as covered residential property
- HOAs must ignore their recorded covenants
- mangroves are covered by the same process
- documentation can be created after an unverified removal and automatically cure the problem

The safest sequence is documentation and verification **before** non-emergency removal.

## Why the phrase “unacceptable risk” matters

A visible defect does not automatically lead to removal.

For example, a cavity, lean, dead branch, root injury, or included bark may warrant:

- monitoring
- pruning
- restricting access beneath the target
- cabling or bracing in a suitable case
- further testing
- removal

The professional assessment should connect three things:

1. the likelihood of failure
2. the consequence if failure occurs
3. the practical options for reducing the risk

A large defect over an occupied bedroom presents a different decision from the same defect over a fenced, unused area.

## How this statute interacts with local permits

Think of the statute as one possible pathway, not the entire Florida permit system.

### Path A: qualifying hazardous-tree documentation

The owner has the documentation required by section 163.045, the property fits the definition, and the tree meets the risk standard. The statute limits the local requirements described in the law.

### Path B: ordinary local tree-removal review

The tree is healthy, the removal is elective, the property does not fit the statutory definition, or the homeowner is not relying on the statutory pathway. City or county permit rules may apply.

### Path C: separate environmental, utility, development, or private-property control

Mangroves, wetlands, rights-of-way, utility areas, construction sites, HOA common property, and boundary disputes can trigger different questions.

The general permit guide explains how to identify the correct path: [Do You Need a Permit to Remove a Tree in Florida?](/blog/florida-tree-removal-permit-guide/).

## Mangroves are a separate issue

Florida mangroves are regulated under the Mangrove Trimming and Preservation Act and related state or delegated-local processes.

Do not use section 163.045 as a shortcut for mangrove trimming or alteration. Even small shoreline plants may fall within mangrove rules, and removal can be treated differently from limited homeowner trimming.

When a coastal tree might be a red, black, or white mangrove, stop and verify before cutting.

## Does an HOA still matter?

State restrictions on local government are not automatically the same as restrictions on a private homeowners association.

An HOA may have recorded covenants, architectural-review procedures, common-area ownership rules, or replacement requirements. Whether a particular HOA restriction is enforceable is a property-specific legal question, but homeowners should not assume that a city-permit exemption automatically equals HOA approval.

Confirm:

- who owns the tree
- whether the tree is on a private lot or common area
- whether written HOA approval is required
- whether replacement planting or design review applies
- whether an emergency procedure exists

## A practical five-step process

### Step 1: Identify the governing property and tree location

Confirm city versus unincorporated county, parcel ownership, right-of-way status, easements, and HOA control.

### Step 2: Decide whether the reason is risk or preference

A risk-based removal and an elective removal may travel through different paths.

### Step 3: Obtain qualified onsite documentation when relying on the statute

The assessment should be signed, tree-specific, and completed by a professional named in the law.

### Step 4: Check for separate rules

Screen for mangroves, wetlands, public rights-of-way, utility involvement, construction approvals, and private covenants.

### Step 5: Keep the full record

Save the report, credential information, photographs, jurisdiction correspondence, written estimate, and final invoice.

## Examples that show why the details matter

### Mature oak with a documented split over a home

If an onsite risk assessment concludes that removal is the only practical way to reduce an unacceptable risk and the property meets the statutory definition, section 163.045 may be directly relevant.

### Healthy tree removed for a pool project

This is an elective property-improvement decision, not automatically a statutory hazardous-tree case. Local permit and replacement rules should be checked.

### Tree in an HOA common area

The single-family homeowner may not own or control the tree. Association governance, ownership, and local requirements need separate review.

### Mangrove beside a seawall

Use Florida’s mangrove rules and the appropriate state or delegated-local process—not the ordinary residential hazardous-tree framework.

### Storm-damaged tree touching power lines

Stay away. Contact 911 or the electric utility as appropriate. Electrical emergency response comes before permit interpretation or ordinary tree-service scheduling.

## Common mistakes to avoid

- removing first and seeking documentation afterward
- relying on a report that does not identify the exact tree
- assuming any person who works with trees is an ISA Certified Arborist
- confusing “high concern” with the statute’s unacceptable-risk standard
- ignoring property type
- ignoring HOA or ownership questions
- treating mangroves like ordinary landscape trees
- assuming a tree-service quote is legal documentation
- discarding the report after removal

## What a tree-service estimate should do—and should not do

The arborist or landscape-architect documentation addresses risk. The tree-service estimate addresses the work.

A complete estimate should identify:

- the exact tree
- removal method
- access and equipment
- property protection
- debris hauling
- stump handling
- exclusions
- permit or documentation assumptions
- payment and change-order terms

It should not claim to replace professional risk documentation unless the estimator separately holds the required credential and completes the required assessment.
