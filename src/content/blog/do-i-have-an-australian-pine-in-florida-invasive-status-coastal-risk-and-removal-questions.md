# Do I Have an Australian Pine in Florida? Invasive Status, Coastal Risk, and Removal Questions

Australian pine is not a true pine.

In Florida, the name commonly refers to several *Casuarina* species. Current UF/IFAS Assessment pages list *Casuarina equisetifolia*, *C. cunninghamiana*, and *C. glauca* as **Prohibited**.

That status matters, but it does not prove that a particular tree is about to fail. A coastal Australian pine decision should combine species identification, site authority, dunes or wetlands, erosion concerns, utilities, storm exposure, tree condition, and the replacement plan.

## Identify the genus before applying pine rules

The apparent “needles” are jointed green branchlets. The true leaves are tiny teeth arranged around the joints.

| Feature | Australian-pine clue |
|---|---|
| Foliage | Thin, jointed, drooping branchlets. |
| Leaf teeth | Tiny scale-like teeth in rings at branchlet joints. |
| Fruit | Small woody cone-like structures. |
| Bark | Rough, fissured gray-brown bark. |
| Litter | Dense mat of brown branchlets and cones. |
| Site | Coastal, roadside, canal, disturbed, or windbreak setting. |
| Roots | Some species may produce root suckers. |

A true Florida pine has needles in bundles and larger pine cones. Use [Florida Tree Identification for Homeowners](/blog/florida-tree-identification-for-homeowners-common-yard-trees-and-what-they-can-tell-you/) before applying pine-care guidance to *Casuarina*.

## Coastal authority changes the removal question

Before cutting or grinding, check whether the tree is near:

- dunes,
- beaches,
- seawalls,
- wetlands,
- canals,
- rights-of-way,
- conservation areas,
- public land,
- utility corridors,
- erosion-sensitive slopes.

Coastal work may involve authority beyond the property owner. Prohibited plant status does not erase dune, wetland, access, wildlife, or public-land rules.
