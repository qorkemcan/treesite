# Preparing Your Landscape for Tropical Storms

Most homeowners prepare for tropical storms by thinking about the house first.

That makes sense. People think about shutters, rooflines, outdoor furniture, and generators. But the landscape matters more than many property owners realize. Trees, overhanging limbs, unstable palms, poor drainage areas, loose yard materials, and overgrown canopy sections can all become part of the storm problem before the winds feel severe.

The best time to deal with those issues is before the weather system forces the decision.

## Start with what can hit something important

Do not scan the whole yard evenly. Start with trees and limbs that could damage:

- the house,
- roof edges,
- screened enclosures,
- parked vehicles,
- fences,
- driveways,
- pool equipment,
- utility lines,
- road access.

The question is not only “What looks messy?” It is “What has a target if it fails?”

## Practical pre-storm landscape checklist

| Area | What to look for |
|---|---|
| Roofline | Limbs rubbing or hanging over shingles and gutters |
| Palms | Dead fronds, seed pods, crown decline, loose debris |
| Mature trees | Deadwood, cracks, heavy overextended limbs |
| Leaning trees | New lean, soil lifting, root plate movement |
| Drainage | Clogged swales, blocked drains, saturated low areas |
| Loose items | Pots, branches, furniture, tools, yard waste |
| Access | Trees or limbs that could block driveway or entry |

For pruning timing, see [The Right Way to Prune Trees Before Storm Season](/blog/the-right-way-to-prune-trees-before-storm-season/).

## What not to do at the last minute

Do not start aggressive cutting when a storm is already close. Last-minute work can create fresh wounds, leave debris loose, and put people under unstable trees during deteriorating weather.

If a branch is already broken or a tree has shifted, stay clear and treat it as a safety problem. Do not work near power lines or unstable limbs.
