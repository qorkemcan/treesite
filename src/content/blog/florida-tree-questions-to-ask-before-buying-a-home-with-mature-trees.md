# Buying a Florida Home With Mature Trees: Questions to Ask Before Closing

Mature trees can be one of the strongest reasons to buy a property. They can also create costs and constraints that a standard showing does not reveal.

The useful question is not, “Are these trees good or bad?” It is:

**Which trees matter to the house, people, access, utilities, hardscape, insurance, and future plans—and what evidence is available before the inspection period ends?**

## Organize tree due diligence by transaction stage

| Stage | Tree task | Desired record |
|---|---|---|
| Before or just after offer | Identify major trees, obvious targets, and visible conflicts | Initial photographs and concern list |
| Inspection period | Obtain the appropriate tree assessment and cost estimates | Written scope, findings, limitations, and estimates |
| Negotiation | Separate urgent safety, near-term work, maintenance, and optional improvements | Prioritized cost and responsibility list |
| Before closing | Confirm permits, HOA records, completed work, and remaining conditions | Receipts, approvals, final photos, and written disclosures |
| After closing | Establish maintenance and storm-monitoring plan | Tree inventory and first-year schedule |

The contract, inspection deadline, and disclosure obligations should be handled with licensed real-estate and legal professionals.

## Identify the major trees correctly

Start with species and approximate size.

Ask:

- What are the dominant shade trees and palms?
- Are any invasive, short-lived, or poorly suited to the site?
- Which trees are close to the home, driveway, pool, septic area, or utility lines?
- Are any trees in an HOA common area or public right-of-way?
- Are protected species, mangroves, conservation areas, or local tree rules involved?
- Which trees create most of the property’s shade, privacy, and curb appeal?

Species alone does not determine safety. It helps explain mature size, known growth habits, maintenance needs, and site fit.

## Look at trees and targets together

Tree risk depends on what could be struck as well as the tree’s condition.

Map major targets:

- occupied rooms
- roof and garage
- driveway and parking
- sidewalks and entrances
- pool cage and deck
- neighboring structures
- play areas
- sheds
- utility equipment
- septic and irrigation infrastructure

A defect in a remote tree may require a different response from the same defect over a bedroom or main entrance.

## Ask whether the requested inspection matches the decision

“Tree inspection” can describe very different services.

| Need | Appropriate scope |
|---|---|
| Identify species and obvious maintenance needs | General tree inventory or property walk-through |
| Evaluate visible defects and targets | Tree-risk-oriented assessment |
| Investigate possible internal decay or roots | Advanced assessment with appropriate tools |
| Establish property boundaries | Licensed survey |
| Determine permit or protected-tree requirements | Current jurisdiction and HOA research |
| Estimate pruning, removal, or stump work | Itemized contractor estimate |
| Determine insurance acceptability or coverage | Insurer or licensed insurance professional |

Ask for the scope and limitations in writing. A quick contractor walk-through is not automatically a formal tree-risk assessment, survey, appraisal, insurance inspection, or legal opinion.

## Read the structure, not just the leaves

A full green canopy does not prove that every major attachment, trunk section, or root is sound.

Look for:

- large dead limbs
- hanging branches
- fresh or expanding lean
- root-plate movement
- soil cracks near the base
- trunk cracks or splits
- cavities
- fungal fruiting bodies
- codominant stems
- weak attachments
- old topping or severe overpruning
- recent construction damage
- repeated branch failure
- one-sided canopy loading

UF/IFAS emphasizes that there are no universal rules separating every safe and unsafe tree. Condition, species, site, defects, and targets must be evaluated together.

## Ask what changed during recent storms

Request records of:

- fallen trees or large limbs
- insurance claims
- roof or fence repairs
- emergency cleanup
- lightning strikes
- flooding or saturated soil
- root movement
- tree removal
- heavy pruning
- neighboring tree failures

Compare older real-estate photographs with current conditions when available. A recently cleaned yard may still have unresolved root, trunk, or canopy issues.

## Walk the root zones and hardscape

Check:

- lifted sidewalks or pavers
- driveway displacement
- pool-deck movement
- damaged irrigation
- roots near septic or drain fields
- trenching
- grade changes
- compacted parking areas
- fill soil over roots
- buried root flare
- mulch against trunks
- erosion
- long-standing ponding

Surface roots do not automatically mean foundation damage or tree removal. Root conflicts should be evaluated by location, severity, tree stability, hardscape design, and the owner’s future plans.

Use [How Close Is Too Close to Cut Tree Roots Near a Florida Home?](/blog/how-close-is-too-close-to-cut-tree-roots-near-a-florida-home/) before approving root cutting.

## Review roof, solar, pool, and access conflicts

Ask whether branches:

- touch or scrape the roof
- block roof or solar maintenance
- overhang a pool cage
- drop heavy fruit or deadwood over parking
- restrict emergency or equipment access
- crowd a narrow side yard
- interfere with gutters
- sit near service drops or overhead conductors

A pruning estimate should identify what can be corrected without topping, over-thinning, or destabilizing the tree.

## Confirm utilities and easements

Check the survey and seller records for:

- utility easements
- overhead lines
- service drops
- underground electric
- water and sewer
- septic tank and drain field
- irrigation mains
- gas lines
- communication lines
- drainage easements

Do not assume the future owner has authority to prune or remove vegetation in an easement. Utility coordination and current local rules may control the work.

## Request permit and HOA history

Ask for:

- prior tree-removal permits
- denied applications
- replacement-tree obligations
- code notices
- protected-tree records
- HOA approvals
- landscape-plan requirements
- common-area responsibility
- pending violations
- right-of-way or street-tree communication
- conservation or waterfront restrictions

A state statute does not replace local fact checking for every property and tree.

## Build a first-year cost plan

Separate likely work into four groups:

### Immediate safety

- active split
- hanging limb
- root-plate movement
- tree on or against a structure
- line involvement
- blocked emergency access

### Near-term correction

- deadwood over a target
- roof clearance
- poor past cuts needing management
- declining palm
- hardscape conflict
- drainage or root-zone problem

### Routine maintenance

- inspection interval
- selective pruning
- palm care
- mulch correction
- irrigation adjustment
- debris management

### Optional improvement

- removal for a pool or addition
- privacy planting
- stump grinding
- landscape redesign
- replacement trees

Request itemized estimates rather than one broad “tree allowance.”

## Use findings in negotiation carefully

Possible transaction responses include:

- seller completes agreed work
- buyer receives a credit
- price reflects near-term work
- funds are held under an agreement
- buyer accepts maintenance with documentation
- a specialist resolves an uncertainty
- the parties revise timing

The best response depends on the contract, inspection rights, lender, insurer, and advice from the transaction professionals. A tree provider should not recommend a legal negotiation strategy.

## Records to obtain before closing

Keep:

- tree assessment
- photographs
- tree-work estimates
- completed-work invoices
- permit and HOA approvals
- proof of replacement planting
- insurer communication
- survey
- seller disclosures
- storm and repair records
- warranties
- remaining-work list
