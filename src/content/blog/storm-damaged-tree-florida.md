# Storm-Damaged Tree in Florida: What to Do First

A storm-damaged tree is not ordinary yard debris. A trunk can keep shifting after the wind stops, a split limb can remain suspended under load, and a root plate can continue to lift as saturated soil moves.

The safest response is a sequence:

1. protect people,
2. identify hazards that belong to emergency services or the utility,
3. document the loss from a safe location,
4. authorize only the work needed to stabilize the scene,
5. decide later what can be restored, pruned, removed, or cleaned up.

That order matters. Starting with a chainsaw, moving branches off a line, or walking under a damaged canopy can turn property damage into a medical emergency.

## Use the right first call

| What you see | First call or action | Tree work status |
|---|---|---|
| Injury, fire, gas odor, partial building collapse, or a person trapped | Call 911 and leave the affected area | Wait for emergency responders |
| Tree or limb touching a power line, service drop, pole, transformer, or electrical equipment | Stay far away; call 911 and the electric utility | No private tree work until the utility controls the electrical hazard |
| Tree resting on an occupied home with signs of structural movement | Move people out of the affected rooms; call 911 when collapse or injury is possible | Coordinate with emergency responders, insurer, and a qualified tree crew |
| Hanging limb, split trunk, new lean, or lifting root plate over an active area | Establish an exclusion zone and keep people, pets, and vehicles out | Request prompt professional assessment |
| Tree blocks a driveway but is clear of lines and structures | Use another exit when possible; do not crawl under or cut through the tree | An [emergency tree response](/services/emergency-response/) may be appropriate |
| Small loose twigs and leaves with no damaged tree overhead | Wear basic protective gear and inspect the area before cleanup | Routine cleanup may be reasonable |

Floodwater, darkness, and piles of branches can conceal electrical equipment. Florida Power & Light advises people to stay away from downed lines and from flooded or debris-covered areas that may hide them. Its post-storm guidance also directs customers to report downed lines or unsafe electrical equipment through 911 or the utility. See [FPL’s after-storm safety guidance](https://www.fpl.com/storm/restoration/safety.html).

## Build an exclusion zone before taking photographs

Keep everyone out of the possible fall and movement area. The boundary should account for more than the piece already on the ground.

Include space around:

- hanging or partly attached limbs,
- a trunk leaning against another tree,
- a root plate that has lifted,
- broken branches resting on a roof, fence, pool cage, or vehicle,
- cracked leaders or codominant stems,
- the downhill side of a leaning tree,
- any area near electrical lines or equipment.

Do not park a vehicle under the damaged canopy to “protect it from the street.” Do not let children or pets back into the yard because the rain has stopped. Our guide to [making a storm-damaged yard safer for children and pets](/blog/how-to-make-a-storm-damaged-yard-safer-for-kids-and-pets/) covers the practical reopening steps.

## Document the loss without entering the hazard area

Photographs are useful for the tree professional and the insurer, but no photograph is worth stepping beneath unstable wood.

From a safe position, capture:

- the whole tree and its relationship to the house or other targets,
- the point where the trunk or limb failed,
- lifted soil or exposed roots,
- cracks, splits, cavities, or fungal growth,
- contact with the roof, fence, driveway, vehicle, or pool enclosure,
- nearby utility equipment,
- the route equipment may need to use,
- visible damage before anything is moved.

Save the original files. Record the date and approximate time, weather conditions, and what changed after the storm. When an insurance claim may be involved, notify the insurer promptly and ask what documentation it wants before non-emergency debris is removed. Florida’s Department of Financial Services provides a [homeowners insurance overview](https://www.myfloridacfo.com/division/consumers/understanding-insurance/homeownersinsuranceoverview) and policyholder claim resources.

## Separate emergency stabilization from final tree work

The first crew visit may not settle every question. A safe response can occur in stages.

### Stage 1: electrical and life safety

Emergency responders and the utility address hazards within their authority. A private tree company should not be used as a substitute for the utility when electrical contact is possible.

Read [what to do when a tree is touching power lines after a storm](/blog/what-to-do-if-a-tree-is-touching-power-lines-after-a-storm/) before approaching the site.

### Stage 2: temporary make-safe work

A qualified crew may remove an immediately threatening hanging limb, relieve a load from a damaged structure, or open essential access. This work should have a written scope that explains what is being stabilized now and what remains.

### Stage 3: standing-tree assessment

The rest of the tree may need inspection after the urgent load is removed. Questions include:

- Did the root plate move?
- Is the trunk split below the visible failure?
- How much live crown remains?
- Can the tree be restored without creating a worse structure?
- Does the tree still threaten an occupied target?
- Is monitoring realistic, or would removal be the safer long-term decision?

UF/IFAS explains that the decision to remove or restore a hurricane-damaged tree depends on the tree’s condition, species, size, damage, and targets. See [Assessing Hurricane-Damaged Trees and Deciding What to Do](https://edis.ifas.ufl.edu/publication/FR172/pdf).

### Stage 4: removal, pruning, and cleanup

Only after the site is controlled should the work move into restoration, [tree removal](/services/tree-removal/), [tree trimming](/services/tree-trimming/), hauling, stump work, and landscape repair.

Our comparison of [storm cleanup versus full tree removal](/blog/storm-cleanup-vs-full-tree-removal-what-happens-first/) explains why these may be separate scopes and separate prices.

## Signs the tree may remain unstable

A tree does not become safe merely because it stayed upright overnight.

Prompt assessment is warranted when you see:

- a new lean or a lean that increased,
- fresh soil cracks or mounding at the base,
- torn or exposed roots,
- a trunk split that extends below the broken limb,
- a large suspended branch,
- a tree lodged in another tree,
- a remaining canopy heavily weighted to one side,
- a cracked union over a roof, driveway, street, or play area,
- repeated movement, popping sounds, or falling debris,
- a tree that changed again after additional rain.

Some damaged trees fail hours or days later. The warning signs are reviewed in [Can Storm-Damaged Trees Fail Days Later?](/blog/can-storm-damaged-trees-fail-days-later/).

## What not to do

Do not:

- cut a bent or pinned limb to see how it reacts,
- pull a leaning tree with a truck, rope, or winch,
- climb onto a damaged roof beneath the tree,
- walk through floodwater near electrical equipment,
- use a ladder or pole saw near a broken canopy,
- enter a neighbor’s property without permission except as directed by emergency responders,
- agree to a vague cash-only cleanup before the work and insurance responsibilities are clear,
- assume the stump, logs, chips, hauling, and final grading are included.

Storm-loaded wood can release suddenly. UF/IFAS notes that bent and tensioned material can spring back when moved or cut. That is one reason compromised trees require different handling from routine pruning.

## What a written emergency scope should say

Before authorizing work, ask for a written description of:

- the immediate hazard being addressed,
- whether electrical clearance has been confirmed,
- which limbs or trunk sections will be removed,
- whether the structure will be protected or accessed,
- what debris will be moved now,
- what debris will remain,
- whether the standing tree will need a separate assessment,
- whether full removal and stump grinding are separate,
- how property damage will be documented,
- the company’s insurance information and payment terms.

Emergency pricing can be higher because of timing, instability, limited access, specialized equipment, and the need to protect structures. The scope should still be understandable.

## When the yard can reopen

Do not reopen the area simply because the visible debris is gone.

Confirm that:

- no hanging or suspended wood remains over the area,
- the utility has released any electrical hazard,
- the damaged structure is safe to enter,
- the remaining tree has been assessed when structural damage is present,
- unstable piles and logs are secured,
- sharp stubs and trip hazards are removed,
- access routes are clear,
- children and pets cannot reach unfinished work.

The site may be safe for limited access before every stump chip and branch is hauled away. Those are different milestones.
