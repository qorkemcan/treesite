# Can You Be Sued for a Falling Tree in Florida? What to Do Before Assigning Fault

A person can file a claim or lawsuit after a tree causes damage. That does not mean the tree owner is automatically legally responsible.

Responsibility may depend on facts such as:

- ownership or control of the tree
- boundary location
- prior condition
- what the owner knew or reasonably should have known
- warnings or complaints
- inspections and prior work
- the weather event
- causation
- insurance policy terms
- damage and injury
- current Florida law

This page is an incident-response guide, not a prediction of liability.

## Use this response table

| Situation | First priority | Keep or document |
|---|---|---|
| Person is injured or trapped | Call 911 | Do not move evidence unless needed for rescue |
| Tree contacts power lines | Stay away and contact utility or emergency services | Photos only from a safe location |
| Tree rests on occupied structure | Evacuate unsafe area and obtain emergency guidance | Full scene, points of support, weather, time |
| Water or gas line may be affected | Contact appropriate utility or emergency authority | Odor, sound, flooding, visible damage |
| No immediate life hazard | Notify insurer and preserve scene | Wide and close photos, video, records |
| Tree came from neighboring property | Avoid admissions or accusations | Boundary information, tree base, impact path |
| Cleanup must begin | Use minimum make-safe scope | Before-and-after photos, removed sections, invoices |
| Dispute is developing | Contact insurer and attorney | Notices, prior complaints, assessments, messages |
| Public or HOA tree may be involved | Identify controlling entity | Maps, governing documents, maintenance records |

## Safety comes before evidence

Keep people away from:

- suspended wood
- cracked trunks
- root plates under tension
- trees resting on roofs or fences
- energized lines
- unstable structures
- floodwater near electrical damage
- partially blocked roads
- leaning trees that changed during the event

Call 911 and the utility as appropriate.

Use [What to Do If a Tree Is Touching Power Lines](/blog/what-to-do-if-a-tree-is-touching-power-lines-after-a-storm/) for electrical hazards.

## Notify the insurer promptly

Florida’s Department of Financial Services advises policyholders to understand their rights and responsibilities during the claims process.

Report the loss according to the policy and ask:

- claim number
- emergency mitigation instructions
- documentation requirements
- whether inspection is needed before nonemergency removal
- approved communication method
- coverage limits for tree and debris removal
- whether damaged personal property should be preserved
- how to submit estimates and invoices

Coverage depends on the policy and facts.

Do not allow a tree contractor to negotiate or settle the insurance claim unless that person is legally authorized to provide that service.

## Photograph the whole event

Before the scene changes, when safe, document:

- full tree and impact path
- tree base and root plate
- trunk and fracture
- crown
- structure or vehicle damage
- neighboring property
- boundary markers if visible
- power lines and utilities
- weather and site conditions
- prior decay or defects visible in broken wood
- debris before movement

Take wide shots first, then details.

Do not climb, move loaded wood, or enter a dangerous structure for a better photograph.

## Preserve prior records

Collect:

- older photographs
- arborist reports
- pruning and removal estimates
- invoices
- inspection notes
- HOA records
- permit records
- neighbor emails or letters
- storm-damage photographs
- maintenance records
- insurance communications
- survey or boundary records

Prior notice may be disputed. Preserve the original documents and dates.

## Do not assign fault in the field

A tree crew can describe physical conditions such as:

- root plate lifted
- trunk fractured
- decay visible at fracture
- branch was dead
- tree rested on roof
- utility contact prevented access

The crew should not state who is legally liable unless qualified to do so.

Avoid speculative statements in texts, social posts, or recorded conversations.

## Make-safe work versus full cleanup

Emergency work may include:

- opening an exit
- preventing additional movement
- removing a hanging section
- separating tree load from a structure
- cutting power-line-free debris after utility clearance
- temporary stabilization

Full cleanup may include:

- completing removal
- hauling
- stump grinding
- grading
- fence work
- roof repair
- landscape restoration

The estimate should separate these scopes.

## Before a tree fails

When a tree shows a concerning condition, document and address it.

Examples include:

- new lean
- root-plate movement
- trunk crack
- split union
- large dead limb
- repeated branch failure
- basal decay
- major storm change
- written warning

Use [What to Document Before Removing a Hazardous Tree](/blog/what-to-document-before-removing-a-hazardous-tree-in-florida/) to preserve the condition.

A qualified tree-risk assessment may help the owner decide what physical action is reasonable. It is not a legal opinion about future liability.

## Boundary and ownership issues

The tree base may be:

- entirely on one parcel
- on a boundary
- in HOA common area
- in public right-of-way
- on government property
- within an easement

Do not assume ownership from where the canopy or fallen trunk lands.

A surveyor or attorney may be needed when control is disputed.
