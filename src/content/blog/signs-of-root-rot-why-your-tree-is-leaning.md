# Signs of Root Rot: Why Your Tree Is Leaning

A leaning tree does not always mean root rot—but root rot is one of the most important reasons homeowners should take a new lean seriously.

Some trees grow with a stable lean for years. The problem is that a tree leaning because its root system is weakening is a very different situation. In Florida, heavy rain, saturated soil, fungi, and storm pressure can turn a manageable-looking lean into a real structural risk faster than homeowners expect.

The most important question is not simply: **Is this tree leaning?**

It is: **Is this tree leaning differently now because the root system is losing support?**

## Why root rot matters for leaning trees

A tree stays upright because of more than the trunk. The real support system is below ground.

When roots are healthy, they anchor the tree and help it tolerate wind, soil movement, and canopy weight. When roots decay, the tree can lose the structure that holds it in place. That is why root rot is not just a health problem. It is a stability problem.

A tree can still look leafy and alive while its support system is becoming less reliable underneath.

## Warning signs to take seriously

| Sign | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Lean appears suddenly | New movement can indicate root or soil failure. |
| Lean is increasing | Change over time is more concerning than a stable old lean. |
| Soil is lifting on one side | Root plate movement can indicate anchoring failure. |
| Mushrooms or conks near base | Decay fungi may indicate root or buttress issues. |
| Soil stays saturated | Roots can lose oxygen and strength in wet conditions. |
| Canopy thins or drops leaves | Root stress may show above ground later. |
| Trunk cracks or base softening | Structural integrity may be compromised. |

For related symptoms, see [Why Is My Tree Dropping Leaves in Summer?](/blog/why-is-my-tree-dropping-leaves-in-summer/) and [Flooding and Tree Health](/blog/flooding-and-tree-health-managing-saturated-soil/).

## What to do first

Do not try to pull, brace, or cut a leaning tree yourself. First, look for targets: house, driveway, sidewalk, fence, pool, road, power lines, or neighboring property.

If the lean is stable and there are no major warning signs, an evaluation may help determine whether monitoring, pruning, soil correction, or removal makes sense.

If the tree has moved suddenly, the soil is lifting, or the tree is leaning toward a target, [emergency response services](/services/emergency-response/) may be appropriate.

## Trimming versus removal

[Tree trimming services](/services/tree-trimming/) can reduce deadwood or broken limb risk, but trimming does not repair failed roots.

[Tree removal services](/services/tree-removal/) may be the safer option when root support is compromised, decay is advanced, or targets are exposed. If the tree is removed, ask whether [stump grinding services](/services/stump-grinding/) are included.
