# Do I Have a Carrotwood Tree in Florida? Invasive Status, Berries, and Removal Questions

Carrotwood, *Cupaniopsis anacardioides*, is currently listed by UF/IFAS Assessment as **Prohibited** throughout Florida.

That does not mean a homeowner should remove an unidentified mature tree with no site check. Confirm the plant, separate seed control from stump control, verify local authority, and decide what the property should become after removal.

## Fruit is helpful, but the complete leaf matters

Carrotwood is an evergreen tree with compound leaves.

| Feature | Carrotwood clue |
|---|---|
| Leaf | Pinnately compound with several glossy, leathery leaflets. |
| Leaflet | Oval to oblong, generally smooth-edged. |
| Fruit | Yellow-orange capsule that splits into sections. |
| Seed | Dark, shiny seed exposed as the capsule opens. |
| Bark | Gray-brown, becoming rougher with age. |
| Crown | Dense, rounded canopy. |
| Spread | Seedlings beneath the tree, along fences, and near natural edges. |

Do not identify it from orange berries alone. Photograph the full compound leaf and attachment, fruit at several stages, seeds, bark, crown, and site. Use [Florida Tree Identification for Homeowners](/blog/florida-tree-identification-for-homeowners-common-yard-trees-and-what-they-can-tell-you/) when the tree is mature or the ID is uncertain.

## State plant status and removal authority are separate

UF/IFAS shows carrotwood as Prohibited because it appears on federal or Florida prohibited or noxious plant lists. Before mature-tree removal, still verify ownership, local rules, HOA or easement issues, utilities, sensitive sites, and disposal expectations.
