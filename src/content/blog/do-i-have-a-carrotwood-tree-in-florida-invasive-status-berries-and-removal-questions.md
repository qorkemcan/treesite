# Do I Have a Carrotwood Tree in Florida?

Carrotwood, *Cupaniopsis anacardioides*, is currently listed by UF/IFAS Assessment as **Prohibited** throughout Florida.

That does not mean a homeowner should remove an unidentified mature tree with no site check. Confirm the plant, separate seed control from stump control, verify local authority, and decide what the property should become after removal.

## Fruit is helpful, but the complete leaf matters

Carrotwood is an evergreen tree with compound leaves.

| Feature | Carrotwood clue |
|---|---|
| Leaf | Pinnately compound with several glossy, leathery leaflets |
| Leaflet | Oval to oblong, generally smooth-edged |
| Fruit | Yellow-orange capsule that splits into sections |
| Seed | Dark, shiny seed exposed as the capsule opens |
| Bark | Gray-brown, becoming rougher with age |
| Crown | Dense, rounded canopy |
| Spread | Seedlings beneath the tree, along fences, and near natural edges |

Do not identify it from “orange berries” alone. Photograph the full compound leaf and its attachment, fruit at several stages, seeds, bark, crown, and site.

Lookalikes can include tulipwood and other Sapindaceae landscape trees. Use the [Florida identification hub](/blog/florida-tree-identification-for-homeowners-common-yard-trees-and-what-they-can-tell-you/) or county Extension when the tree is mature or the ID is uncertain.

## State plant status and removal authority are separate

UF/IFAS shows carrotwood as Prohibited because it is included on federal or Florida prohibited/noxious plant lists.

That status affects use, planting, movement, possession, and management. Before a mature-tree removal, still verify:

- property ownership,
- city or county process,
- HOA,
- right-of-way or easement,
- natural-area, coastal, wetland, or conservation setting,
- replacement obligations,
- utilities,
- wildlife.

An invasive-plant label should not be used as a shortcut around a survey, permit, environmental requirement, or shared-property issue.

## Separate four sources of return

Carrotwood can remain a property problem through more than one route.

1. **Fruit still on the tree**
2. **Seed already on the ground**
3. **Established seedlings**
4. **Living stump or root tissue**

One operation may not address all four.

| Scope item | Questions to put in writing |
|---|---|
| Fruiting canopy | Will seed-bearing branches be contained before they are moved? |
| Ground seed | Is immediate cleanup included, and what area is covered? |
| Seedlings | Are visible seedlings included or priced separately? |
| Stump | Is it cut low, chemically controlled under label, ground, or left? |
| Roots | Which surface roots are included in grinding? |
| Follow-up | Who returns for sprouts or new seedlings, and for how long? |

## Do not move viable material into a clean area

Avoid:

- dragging fruiting branches through planting beds,
- dumping debris beside a canal or preserve,
- leaving fruit and seed in a neighbor’s yard,
- adding seed-bearing debris to an unmanaged brush pile,
- using chips in a way that spreads viable seed,
- moving soil containing seedlings without a plan.

Follow current local disposal requirements. A tree crew should explain whether material is chipped, hauled, staged, or handled by another provider.

## Stump treatment and grinding do different jobs

A registered cut-stump herbicide may be used to control living tissue under a specific product label. Grinding physically removes specified stump wood.

Do not assume:

- grinding kills every root,
- herbicide removes the trip hazard,
- cutting prevents seedlings,
- one visit controls the seed bank.

Use [the stump-killer comparison](/blog/do-stump-killers-really-work-or-is-grinding-better/) to separate biological control from site restoration.

Pesticide work must follow the exact label and may require a licensed applicator. The label governs target species, method, concentration, site, protective equipment, weather, water restrictions, and follow-up.

## Hardscape conflict needs a design decision

A carrotwood beside pavers, a pool deck, driveway, wall, or fence may produce:

- lifted surfaces,
- restricted equipment access,
- roots exposed during repair,
- drainage changes,
- fruit and leaf cleanup,
- conflict with a future replacement.

Do not repeatedly cut living roots while keeping the tree without evaluating stability. Compare hardscape redesign with removal and restoration.

After removal, pavers or concrete may need unsuitable organic material removed and a proper base rebuilt. Stump grinding alone is not a hardscape warranty.

## Sensitive sites need another authority lane

Pause when the tree is:

- near a preserve,
- beside a canal or retention area,
- at a wetland edge,
- inside coastal habitat,
- in a common area,
- in a road right-of-way,
- on a disputed boundary,
- mixed with desirable native vegetation.

A local government, environmental agency, HOA, surveyor, or restoration professional may need to define the work limits.

## Replacement is part of invasive control

After removal, disturbed soil and increased light can create a new weed problem.

Plan:

- short-term seedling checks,
- soil stabilization,
- drainage,
- mulch or groundcover,
- suitable regional replacement,
- mature size,
- salt and wind exposure,
- utility clearance,
- establishment irrigation.

Use [right tree, right place guidance](/blog/how-to-choose-the-right-tree-for-the-right-place-in-florida/) rather than replacing carrotwood with another fast-growing problem tree.

## Require a complete quote

The proposal should state:

- confirmed or provisional identification,
- tree and stems included,
- permit and property checks,
- seed containment,
- removal method,
- pesticide role,
- hauling,
- stump depth,
- surface roots,
- chips and backfill,
- hardscape protection,
- seedlings,
- monitoring,
- replacement responsibility.
