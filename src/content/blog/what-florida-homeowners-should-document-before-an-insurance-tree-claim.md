# What Florida Homeowners Should Document Before an Insurance Tree Claim

When a tree damages your home, the cleanup feels urgent. The insurance claim feels urgent too.

That is exactly why homeowners often miss the most important first step: documenting the damage before the situation changes.

Once a tree is cut off the roof, once debris is hauled away, once emergency repairs begin, the original condition becomes much harder to prove clearly. In Florida, where tree claims often happen after storms, wind events, or saturated-ground failures, the difference between a messy story and a well-documented claim can come down to what you photographed, what you saved, and what you recorded in the first few hours.

The goal is not to create paperwork for the sake of paperwork. It is to preserve the facts before the weather, cleanup, or emergency repairs erase them.

## Why documentation matters so much

Most homeowners think of a tree claim as one question:

**Will insurance cover this?**

But before that question gets answered, there is another one:

**Can you clearly show what happened, what was damaged, and what you did next?**

That is why documentation matters.

A tree claim usually becomes easier to explain when the homeowner has clear records showing:

- what the tree hit
- what the damage looked like before cleanup
- where the tree came from
- what emergency steps were taken
- what items were damaged
- what expenses were incurred to prevent further loss

In a storm-driven situation, the damage can change quickly. That is exactly why the first documentation pass is so valuable.

## Start with safety, then document immediately

Do not walk into a dangerous area just to take better photos.

If the tree is still unstable, if large limbs are hanging, or if there is any utility-adjacent concern, stay back and document from a safe distance.

Once it is reasonably safe to observe, start as early as possible. The best photos are almost always the ones taken **before** cleanup, cutting, tarping, or debris removal changes the scene.

## What homeowners should photograph first

### 1. The full scene

Take wide shots showing:

- the tree
- the house
- the driveway or garage if involved
- where the tree landed
- where the tree appears to have originated
- the overall relationship between the damage and the property

Wide shots help explain the event in a way close-up damage photos alone cannot.

### 2. Specific damage to the structure

Take closer photos of:

- roof damage
- broken gutters
- fascia damage
- punctures
- crushed screening
- damaged fencing
- damaged siding
- broken windows
- impact areas around doors or entries

These photos help separate “tree fell” from “this is what the tree actually damaged.”

### 3. The tree itself

Photograph:

- the broken trunk or limbs
- the point of failure if visible
- the base or root area if uprooting occurred
- visible storm or failure indicators
- bark loss, splits, or major cracking if relevant

The tree is part of the story, not just the structure.

### 4. Contents damage, if any

If the tree event caused interior or personal property damage, document that too.

Do not stop at the roofline if water intrusion or impact damage moved inside.

## Why video can help too

Photos are essential, but a slow walk-through video can add useful context.

A short video can show:

- the overall scale of the event
- where debris sits relative to the house
- how the damage connects from outside to inside
- the condition before temporary repairs change the scene

Video should support the photo record, not replace it.

## What documents to save besides photos

A strong tree claim file usually includes more than images.

Keep:

- the date and rough time of the event
- weather context if relevant
- notes on what changed first
- emergency service invoices
- tarp or temporary repair receipts
- debris-removal invoices
- estimates related to the tree and the property damage
- communication with contractors or adjusters
- any written explanation of urgent actions taken to prevent further damage

The point is to show a clear timeline, not just a pile of disconnected receipts.

## Why temporary repair receipts matter

This is one of the most overlooked parts of a claim.

If you had to make temporary repairs to protect the property from further damage, keep receipts and document what you did. That might include:

- tarping
- boarding openings
- emergency branch removal needed to stop further roof damage
- materials used to stabilize exposed areas

A tree claim is often not only about the original impact. It is also about what the homeowner did afterward to prevent the damage from spreading.

## Do not throw everything away too fast

This is where homeowners sometimes create avoidable problems.

In the rush to clean up, people may discard damaged materials before the condition is fully documented or inspected. If it is safe and practical, preserve the evidence long enough for the damage to be properly photographed and reviewed.

That does not mean leaving the property unsafe. It means avoiding unnecessary cleanup that erases the claim story too early.

## A common mistake: documenting only the roof and not the tree

Homeowners often focus on the house because that is where the money concern feels largest.

But the tree is part of the claim narrative too.

It helps to document:

- whether the tree uprooted
- whether a major limb failed
- whether the damage appears storm-related
- whether the impact involved one limb, multiple sections, or full-tree failure

Without that, the claim record can feel incomplete.

## Another common mistake: relying on memory instead of a timeline

A storm claim gets harder to explain when the homeowner later tries to reconstruct the sequence from memory.

It helps to note:

- when the damage was discovered
- what the first visible damage was
- when temporary repairs were made
- when cleanup began
- who was called and when

This becomes especially useful if the claim stretches over time or involves multiple contractors.

## What to do if you must discard hazardous material

Sometimes damaged items cannot safely stay in place.

If material must be thrown away because it is unsafe, contaminated, or worsening the risk, document it thoroughly first. Clear photos and video before disposal are much better than trying to explain later what had to be removed quickly.

## Why “before and after” can matter

If you have recent pre-loss photos of the property, those can help too.

This is especially useful when the tree’s location relative to the house, driveway, or other structures becomes part of the claim conversation. A recent exterior photo from a listing, family gathering, or routine property snapshot can sometimes help show what changed.

## What homeowners should have ready when the claim starts

It helps to organize the file into a simple structure:

1. event photos and video  
2. damage close-ups  
3. tree/failure photos  
4. emergency repair receipts  
5. contractor invoices or estimates  
6. timeline notes  
7. communications and claim details  

The easier it is to understand your story, the easier it is to support it.

## A practical mindset for homeowners

Do not think of documentation as something you do after the claim.

Think of it as the foundation of the claim.

The strongest documentation usually answers four basic questions:

- What happened?
- What was damaged?
- What did the homeowner do immediately afterward?
- What records support that sequence?

If your documentation answers those questions clearly, you are already in a stronger position than most people realize.

If the tree is still on the home, blocking access, or creating an active hazard, document only from a safe distance before the site changes. ## Claim documentation bottom line

Before filing an insurance tree claim in Florida, homeowners should document the full scene, the damaged structure, the tree itself, any interior impact, and every emergency step taken to prevent further damage.

Photos, video, receipts, and a simple timeline often matter more than people expect—especially after storms, when cleanup can erase the original condition fast.

The best documentation is not perfect. It is early, clear, organized, and taken before the evidence disappears.
