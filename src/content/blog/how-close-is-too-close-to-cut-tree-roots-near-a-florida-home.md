## Short Answer

Cutting tree roots close to the trunk is rarely a casual yard fix. Small, shallow roots may sometimes be managed with limited impact, but larger roots, roots near the trunk, or roots on the side of a leaning tree can affect stability and long-term health.

In Florida, the risk can be higher because many yards already deal with sandy soil, saturated rainy-season ground, shallow rooting, hurricanes, hardscape conflicts, and tight lots. If the root is large, close to the trunk, supporting a leaning tree, lifting a driveway or patio, or near utilities, do not guess. Get the tree and work area looked at before cutting.

## Why Root Cutting Is Not Just a Landscaping Decision

A tree root is not only a nuisance under grass or pavers. Roots anchor the tree, absorb water, exchange oxygen with the soil, and help the tree respond to stress.

When a root is cut, the tree may lose part of its support system and part of its water-absorbing system at the same time. That does not always mean the tree will fail. It does mean the decision should match the size of the root, the location of the cut, the condition of the tree, and the reason for the work.

A small feeder root near the surface is different from a large structural root running away from the trunk. A root cut in a quiet mulch bed is different from a root cut beside a leaning oak near a roof.

## Why Florida Yards Make Root Decisions More Sensitive

Florida homeowners often see root conflicts around:

- driveways and sidewalks
- paver patios and pool decks
- irrigation lines
- septic or drain field areas
- fences and side-yard gates
- older lots with mature oaks, pines, ficus, sweetgum, or magnolia
- saturated soil after heavy rain
- coastal lots with sandy or compacted soil

In some Florida yards, roots stay relatively shallow because the water table, soil compaction, or drainage pattern limits deeper rooting. That can make root cutting more important than it looks from the surface.

A tree can look green and still lose stability if the wrong roots are cut in the wrong place.

## The Closer the Cut Is to the Trunk, the More Cautious You Should Be

There is no single distance that makes every root cut safe. Tree species, trunk diameter, soil, defects, lean, canopy weight, previous pruning, and root condition all matter.

Still, one practical rule holds up: the closer the cut is to the trunk, the more serious the decision becomes.

Root cuts close to the trunk can affect the main structural roots. These are the roots that help keep the tree upright during wind, especially when the soil is wet. A cut several feet away may still matter, but a cut near the base of the tree is usually a bigger concern.

Be especially careful if the root is:

- more than about an inch across
- coming directly from the root flare
- on the side opposite a lean
- connected to a raised or cracked soil area
- under a tree with decay, cavities, conks, or previous storm damage
- beside a driveway, patio, pool cage, or house foundation

If you have to ask whether it is too close, it probably deserves a second look.

## Root Size Matters, Too

Homeowners often focus on where the root is. Size matters just as much.

Small surface roots may sometimes be removed or adjusted with limited impact, especially on healthy, established trees. But larger roots are different. Once a root gets thick enough to look structural, cutting it can change how the tree responds to wind and soil movement.

A wide root near the trunk is not the same as a small root crossing a planting bed.

If the root is large enough that you would need a saw, grinder, trenching machine, or heavy cutting tool, pause before cutting. That is not routine gardening anymore.

## Signs Root Cutting Could Create a Stability Problem

Root cutting deserves extra caution when the tree already shows risk signs.

Look for:

- a visible lean that has changed recently
- soil cracking or lifting near the base
- roots lifting on one side of the tree
- mushrooms, conks, or decay near the trunk
- dead limbs or thinning canopy on one side
- trunk cracks, cavities, or included bark
- recent trenching, grading, or construction nearby
- standing water around the root zone
- heavy canopy weight over a house, driveway, or pool cage

One warning sign does not automatically mean removal is needed. But root cutting around a tree with multiple warning signs is a much different decision than trimming a small root in a healthy landscape bed.

## Common Places Homeowners Want to Cut Roots

### Driveways and Sidewalks

Roots lifting concrete are frustrating. Cutting them may seem like the cleanest fix.

The problem is that a large root may be contributing to tree stability. Cutting it so the concrete can be reset may solve the surface problem while creating a tree-health or wind-risk problem later.

Better questions to ask are:

- Can the slab or paver layout be adjusted?
- Can the surface be ramped or redesigned?
- Is the tree still a good fit for that location?
- Would removal and replanting be safer long term?

### Paver Patios and Pool Decks

Florida pavers often hide root issues until the surface starts lifting or settling. A pool deck conflict is especially sensitive because work may involve access limits, irrigation, drainage, screen enclosures, and underground lines.

Before cutting roots under pavers, clarify whether the goal is repair, replacement, drainage correction, or tree removal. Each choice changes the risk.

### Fences and Tight Side Yards

Roots near fences can make post repair or gate replacement difficult. In tight side yards, cutting roots can also affect access for equipment and future stump grinding.

If the root belongs to a large tree close to the property line, get clarity before cutting. Neighbor, HOA, and municipal issues can complicate what looks like a simple fence repair.

### Utilities and Irrigation

Never dig, trench, or cut blindly around underground utilities. Even if the visible problem is a root, the real hazard may be a buried utility line, irrigation pipe, or drainage system.

For deeper work, utility locating should happen before digging. A tree service, landscaper, irrigation contractor, and utility process may all need to be coordinated.

## Root Cutting vs Root Pruning: Not the Same Thing

Homeowners often use the phrase “cut the roots” for anything involving a saw or trench. A better term, when done carefully, is root pruning.

Root pruning should be planned, limited, and clean. It should not be done with a trenching machine ripping through roots at random. Ragged, crushed, or torn roots are harder for the tree to respond to than clean cuts.

Good root pruning considers:

- which roots are being cut
- how far the cuts are from the trunk
- how many roots are affected
- whether the tree has room to recover
- whether the tree is already stressed
- what will happen to soil, water, and hardscape afterward

If the project involves a large mature tree, a tree near a structure, or a tree with any lean or decay, root pruning should not be treated as a casual add-on.

## When Root Cutting May Be the Wrong Fix

Sometimes the root is not the real problem. The real problem may be that the tree is too large for the space, the hardscape was built too close, or the grade was changed around the root zone.

Root cutting may be the wrong fix if:

- the tree has outgrown a narrow planting strip
- roots are lifting multiple hardscape areas
- the trunk is very close to a house or pool cage
- the tree has decay or structural defects
- the same roots are likely to cause problems again
- the work would remove several large roots on one side
- the tree is already leaning toward a target

In those cases, the safer conversation may be about removal, replacement, redesign, or a staged plan rather than repeated root cutting.

## What to Ask Before Anyone Cuts Tree Roots

Before approving root cutting, ask practical questions:

- Which roots are being cut, and how large are they?
- How close are the cuts to the trunk?
- Is the tree leaning or showing root plate movement?
- Could this affect storm stability?
- Are utilities, irrigation, or drainage lines nearby?
- Will the cuts be clean, or is a trenching machine involved?
- Is the goal hardscape repair, tree preservation, or eventual removal?
- Should an arborist look at the tree first?
- Will this create a new issue during hurricane season?

A clear answer is a good sign. A vague “we’ll just cut what’s in the way” is not.

Professional help is worth it when the tree is large, close to a house, close to a driveway or pool cage, leaning, damaged, or already showing decline.

## Final Takeaway

Root cutting near a Florida home should be treated as a tree-risk decision, not just a landscaping shortcut.

Small roots may be manageable. Large roots, roots close to the trunk, roots near utilities, or roots connected to a leaning or declining tree deserve caution. In Florida, where storm winds, wet soil, shallow roots, pavers, pool cages, and tight lots often overlap, the wrong cut can matter later.

Before cutting, ask what the root is doing, what the tree is supporting, and whether the fix solves the problem or simply hides it for now.
