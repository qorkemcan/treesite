# How Close Is Too Close to Cut Tree Roots Near a Florida Home?

There is no universal distance that makes root cutting safe for every tree.

Risk increases when the cut is close to the trunk, the root is large, multiple roots are affected, cuts occur on one side, the tree already leans, soil is saturated, or a home, driveway, pool enclosure, road, or power line sits within the potential failure zone.

Do not use a simple “X feet is safe” rule for a mature tree. Evaluate the tree, root, soil, target, and project together.

## Five questions determine the risk

1. How far is the cut from the trunk?
2. How large is the root?
3. How many roots will be cut?
4. Is the tree already compromised?
5. What could the tree hit?

The closer and larger the cut—and the greater the target—the more caution is required.

## Why distance matters

Main structural roots leave the trunk and help anchor the tree.

UF/IFAS notes that damage to main structural roots within roughly five feet of the trunk can result in health decline, while its root-pruning guidance warns that root cutting can contribute to tree failure.

That does not make five feet a universal boundary. A large tree can have important roots farther out, and a small tree has a different scale.

Distance is one factor, not permission.

## Why root diameter matters

### Small fine roots

These absorb water and nutrients. Damage can stress the tree, but one limited cut far from the trunk may not create the same structural issue as cutting a major root.

### Large woody roots

These can be important for:

- anchorage
- transport
- storage
- support during wind
- connection to deeper roots

If a root requires a saw, grinder, or trencher, it deserves more analysis than ordinary garden edging.

## The side of the tree matters

Cutting roots on one side can reduce support asymmetrically.

Risk is especially concerning when:

- tree leans away from cut roots
- prevailing wind loads the weakened side
- canopy is heavy toward target
- roots on opposite side are constrained
- several roots are cut in one trench
- soil is wet
- tree has recent storm movement

UF/IFAS documents cases where root severing contributed to dramatic lean and failure.

## Trees that should not be casually root-pruned

Pause when there is:

- existing lean
- lifted root plate
- trunk crack
- base decay
- cavity
- conks or mushrooms
- dead canopy
- recent storm damage
- poor anchorage
- several previous root cuts
- compacted or waterlogged soil
- high-value target

UF/IFAS root-pruning guidance specifically cautions against root pruning leaning trees.

## Common projects that create root risk

### Driveway or sidewalk repair

Cutting the root may allow the surface to return to the original grade, but can exchange a trip hazard for a tree-stability hazard.

Alternatives may include:

- redesign
- ramping
- bridging
- flexible surface
- paver adjustment
- path relocation
- tree removal and replacement

### Pool deck or patio

Consider:

- screen enclosure
- underground plumbing
- irrigation
- drainage
- narrow access
- tree position
- future settlement

### Fence or gate

A large root beside the property line may involve ownership, neighbor access, and future stump work.

### Foundation excavation

Do not assume the root caused the structural problem. Coordinate tree and building assessment.

### Utility or drainage work

Before digging or grinding, use the appropriate utility-location process. Sunshine 811 instructs Florida excavators and homeowners to contact 811 before digging and allow member utilities time to mark facilities.

Private lines such as irrigation, pool equipment, or customer-owned electrical lines may require separate locating.

## Root cutting versus root pruning

Random cutting removes whatever is in the way.

Planned root pruning considers:

- exact roots
- cut location
- root size
- total root loss
- clean cut
- soil replacement
- irrigation
- canopy response
- monitoring
- future wind exposure

A trencher tearing multiple roots is not equivalent to a planned clean cut.

## A simple risk matrix

| Condition | Lower concern | Higher concern |
|---|---|---|
| Distance | Farther from trunk | Close to trunk |
| Root size | Small | Large woody root |
| Number | One limited root | Multiple roots |
| Tree | Healthy/stable | Leaning/decayed/damaged |
| Soil | Well drained | Saturated/eroded |
| Target | Open space | Home/road/pool/line |
| Project | Minor surface adjustment | Trench or grinder |
| History | No prior damage | Repeated root cutting |

A high-concern result calls for professional review before excavation.

## What to do before cutting

1. Identify tree species and trunk size.
2. Mark proposed excavation.
3. Measure cut distance.
4. expose roots carefully when appropriate
5. identify root diameter and direction
6. inspect tree base and canopy
7. identify targets
8. contact Sunshine 811 before digging
9. locate private utilities
10. consider alternatives
11. obtain qualified tree advice
12. document the decision

Do not expose roots with destructive digging merely to inspect them.

## When removal may be safer than repeated root cutting

Removal can become more reasonable when:

- tree has outgrown narrow strip
- several major roots conflict with hardscape
- root work would be close to trunk
- tree leans toward target
- defects already exist
- repair would require repeated cuts
- preserving tree prevents safe site use
- alternatives are impractical

A removal decision should include permit/HOA review and a written scope for stump and roots.

## After root pruning

Monitor:

- canopy thinning
- leaf size or color
- dieback
- new lean
- soil movement
- fungal growth
- sprouts
- reduced vigor
- storm response

The tree may not show consequences immediately.

Ask the professional whether canopy work, irrigation adjustment, mulching, or follow-up inspection is appropriate. Do not automatically over-prune the canopy to “match” root loss.
