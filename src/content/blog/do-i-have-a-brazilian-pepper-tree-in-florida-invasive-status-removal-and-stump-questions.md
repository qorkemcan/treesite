# Do I Have a Brazilian Pepper Tree in Florida?

Brazilian pepper is a prohibited nonnative plant in Florida, but a legal plant-status label does not by itself answer who owns the tree, whether local approval is needed, or how a specific specimen should be removed.

Start with three separate decisions:

1. Confirm that the plant is Brazilian pepper.
2. Control exposure, seeds, sprouts, and sensitive-site risks.
3. Build a written removal and follow-up scope.

Brazilian pepper is in the cashew family, the same family as poison ivy. Florida agencies warn that its sap and plant material can irritate the skin and respiratory system in sensitive people. Do not burn it.

## Identification should use several features

Brazilian pepper commonly grows as a dense shrub or multi-stemmed tree.

| Feature | Brazilian pepper pattern | Why one clue is not enough |
|---|---|---|
| Leaves | Alternate, odd-pinnately compound leaves with several leaflets | Many landscape plants have glossy compound leaves |
| Leaf rachis | May be reddish or slightly winged | This varies and can be subtle |
| Fruit | Clusters of small red berries on female plants | Fruit is seasonal and absent on some plants |
| Habit | Dense branching, thickets, volunteer seedlings | Other invasive shrubs can form similar stands |
| Sap or odor | Broken tissue may release resinous sap and odor | Do not deliberately crush an unknown plant against bare skin |
| Site | Fence lines, disturbed edges, canals, wetlands, vacant lots | Site supports the identification but does not prove it |

Take photographs of the full plant, both sides of a complete leaf, fruit when present, bark, stems, and the surrounding site. Use the [Florida tree-identification guide](/blog/florida-tree-identification-for-homeowners-common-yard-trees-and-what-they-can-tell-you/) or a county Extension office when the identification is uncertain.

Do not confuse Brazilian pepper with native hollies, sumacs, or other berry-bearing plants.

## What “prohibited” means—and what it does not mean

UF/IFAS Assessment currently lists *Schinus terebinthifolia* as **Prohibited** throughout North, Central, and South Florida because it appears on federal or Florida prohibited/noxious plant lists.

That status is important for planting, use, possession, movement, and invasive-plant management. It does not automatically function as a residential tree-removal permit.

Before cutting a mature plant, confirm:

- trunk and property-line location,
- city or county authority,
- HOA or common-area ownership,
- easement or right-of-way,
- wetland, canal, mangrove, conservation, or stormwater setting,
- overhead and underground utilities,
- wildlife constraints,
- access across neighboring property.

Use [the Florida own-property removal guide](/blog/can-you-cut-down-a-tree-on-your-own-property-in-florida-what-homeowners-should-check-first/) for the authority sequence.

## Exposure control belongs in the work plan

Brazilian pepper can cause contact dermatitis or respiratory irritation in sensitive individuals.

Avoid:

- bare-skin contact with sap, leaves, fruit, and fresh wood,
- touching the face or eyes during handling,
- burning branches, leaves, stumps, or chips,
- sending smoke toward neighbors,
- casual chipping without an exposure and debris plan,
- leaving fruit-covered debris where children or pets can contact it.

FDACS specifically warns that smoke from burning Brazilian pepper can irritate the eyes, skin, and lungs.

A professional scope should address:

- protective clothing and gloves,
- eye and respiratory protection appropriate to the work,
- tool and clothing cleanup,
- exclusion of children, pets, and sensitive occupants,
- debris handling,
- first-aid and exposure response.

People with known severe plant allergies or respiratory sensitivity should not treat removal as a routine yard project.

## Cutting alone commonly leaves unfinished control

Brazilian pepper can resprout from living tissue after stems are cut. Seedlings may also continue emerging from fruit that was already dispersed.

Separate the possible tasks:

| Task | What it does | What it does not guarantee |
|---|---|---|
| Seedling removal | Removes small individual plants before they mature | Does not control nearby mature seed sources |
| Felling or stem cutting | Removes above-ground growth | Does not necessarily kill the root crown |
| Cut-stump treatment | Applies a registered herbicide to specified living tissue under its label | Does not physically remove the stump |
| Basal-bark treatment | Treats standing stems under a label-specific method | Is not appropriate for every size, site, or applicator |
| Stump grinding | Removes specified stump wood below grade | Does not equal biological control of every root or seedling |
| Follow-up monitoring | Finds sprouts and seedlings | Requires an assigned person and schedule |

UF/IFAS Brazilian pepper control guidance discusses methods such as cut-stump and basal-bark applications. The exact product, concentration, timing, treated tissue, site, protective equipment, water restrictions, and applicator qualifications come from the current label.

Do not use salt, bleach, fuel, motor oil, homemade mixtures, or an agricultural product that is not labeled for the site and method.

## Pesticide work is a separate professional role

A tree-removal crew is not automatically a licensed pesticide applicator.

Before chemical control, verify:

- exact species,
- target size,
- label-approved method,
- residential versus natural-area use,
- distance from water,
- desirable plants with connected roots or nearby foliage,
- children and pets,
- weather and runoff,
- applicator license when required,
- who returns for follow-up.

The U.S. EPA states that pesticide labels are legally enforceable. Florida licensing requirements may apply to commercial applications.

When the plant is near a canal, wetland, stormwater pond, shoreline, or drainage path, ordinary yard-product assumptions are not enough. Use the exact label and obtain direction from the responsible environmental authority.

## Seed containment changes cleanup

Fruit-bearing material should not be dragged through clean landscape beds or dumped along a preserve, canal, road edge, vacant lot, or neighboring property.

The written scope should say:

- whether fruiting branches are contained,
- how debris moves through the property,
- whether material is chipped, hauled, or disposed of under local rules,
- whether the work zone is raked,
- whether seedlings are included,
- how tools and machinery are cleaned,
- who monitors the area afterward.

A low removal price that excludes fruit, seedlings, stump control, hauling, and follow-up may leave the homeowner with the same invasive problem.

## Stump grinding is a restoration decision

Grinding may be useful when the stump interferes with mowing, access, sod, a planting bed, or a future landscape project.

Ask:

- diameter and measurement method,
- grinding depth,
- surface roots included,
- utility-locate status,
- irrigation and private-line review,
- chip removal,
- backfill and grade,
- whether chemical treatment occurs before or after grinding,
- what the product label permits,
- who handles returning sprouts.

Read [stump killer versus grinding](/blog/do-stump-killers-really-work-or-is-grinding-better/) before treating those services as interchangeable.

## Use a sensitive-site stop list

Pause the project when the plant is:

- inside or beside a wetland,
- on a canal or stormwater bank,
- in a mangrove or conservation area,
- within an HOA common area,
- in a public right-of-way,
- tangled with utility lines,
- on a disputed property line,
- mixed with desirable native vegetation,
- supporting an active wildlife nest,
- blocking safe emergency access.

The site may need an environmental professional, utility, surveyor, local government, HOA, or restoration specialist before a tree crew is scheduled.

## Replacement should close the opening

Brazilian pepper removal can leave light, disturbed soil, and an open edge where new invasive seedlings establish.

Plan:

- seedling monitoring,
- mulch or groundcover,
- drainage,
- suitable Florida-Friendly or native replacement plants,
- mature height and spread,
- distance from structures and utilities,
- irrigation establishment,
- access for future maintenance.

Do not replant a new fast-growing invasive or prohibited species.

## Build the quote around control and completion

A complete proposal should identify:

- confirmed or provisional species identification,
- stems and area included,
- exposure controls,
- utility and authority checks,
- seed-bearing debris,
- felling method,
- pesticide role and licensed applicator when required,
- stump scope,
- seedlings and sprouts,
- hauling,
- restoration,
- follow-up responsibility.
