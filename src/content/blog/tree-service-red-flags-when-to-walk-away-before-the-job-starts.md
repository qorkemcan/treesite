# Tree Service Red Flags: When to Walk Away Before the Job Starts

Some concerns can be fixed with one more question. Others are reasons to pause the job and choose another provider.

Walk away—or at least stop approval—when a tree service refuses to identify the business, will not provide a written scope, dismisses power-line danger, proposes dropping heavy wood without a safe landing zone, pressures you to pay before explaining the work, or cannot account for insurance, workers, cleanup, permits, and property protection.

The issue is not whether the company uses polished sales language. It is whether the work can proceed without shifting avoidable risk to the homeowner.

## 1. The company will not put the scope in writing

A verbal number is not enough for removal near a home, driveway, fence, pool cage, vehicle, or neighboring property.

The written scope should identify:

- the exact tree,
- removal versus pruning,
- general work method,
- debris and log handling,
- stump-grinding status,
- access limitations,
- property-protection assumptions,
- permit or HOA responsibility,
- exclusions,
- payment terms,
- conditions that could change the price.

For estimate structure, see [what should be included in a tree removal estimate?](/blog/what-should-be-included-in-a-tree-removal-estimate-in-florida/).

## 2. Power-line danger is dismissed

This is a stop sign.

If a tree or branch is touching electrical equipment, the utility or emergency authorities may need to be contacted before [tree removal services](/services/tree-removal/) begin. A provider that treats energized-line risk casually is not a safe fit.

For line-adjacent hazards, see [what to do if a tree is touching power lines after a storm](/blog/what-to-do-if-a-tree-is-touching-power-lines-after-a-storm/).

## 3. The work method sounds reckless

Be cautious when a company casually proposes dropping heavy limbs near:

| Target | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Roof | Impact and water intrusion risk. |
| Pool cage | Screens and framing can be damaged. |
| Pavers | Heavy wood and equipment can crack surfaces. |
| Fence | Sections may need removal or protection. |
| Driveway | Vehicles and access routes may be exposed. |
| Neighbor property | Liability and access issues may arise. |

For method questions, see [red flags before hiring a tree removal crew](/blog/red-flags-before-hiring-a-tree-removal-crew-in-florida/) and [bucket truck, climber, or crane](/blog/bucket-truck-climber-or-crane-why-tree-removal-methods-change-the-quote/).

## 4. Cleanup and stump work are vague

A low number may not include [stump grinding services](/services/stump-grinding/), log hauling, chip cleanup, final raking, or return visits.

Ask what the property will look like when the crew leaves. For cleanup scope, see [tree removal cleanup quote](/blog/tree-removal-cleanup-what-should-be-included-in-the-quote/).

## 5. Pressure replaces explanation

Be careful with pressure tactics:

- “This price is only good if you decide now.”
- “You do not need anything in writing.”
- “Permits are never an issue.”
- “Insurance is not necessary.”
- “We can just drop it.”

A trustworthy provider should be able to explain the plan.

## 6. Emergency claims are unclear

If the tree is actively hazardous, [emergency response services](/services/emergency-response/) may be appropriate. But the provider should still explain what is being done first: stabilization, access clearing, full removal, hauling, or later cleanup.

For routine pruning issues, [tree trimming services](/services/tree-trimming/) may be the better route. For rental, HOA, or business sites, [commercial tree services](/services/commercial-services/) coordination can help with notices and documentation.
