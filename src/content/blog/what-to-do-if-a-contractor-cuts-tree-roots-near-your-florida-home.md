# What to Do If a Contractor Cuts Tree Roots Near Your Florida Home

A contractor may cut tree roots while installing a fence, repairing pavers, trenching for utilities, adding irrigation, replacing a driveway, or grading a yard.

Sometimes the tree looks fine afterward.

That does not always mean the tree is fine.

Roots are not just for feeding the tree. They also help hold the tree upright. In Florida, where sandy soils, saturated ground, storms, and high winds are part of normal yard life, root damage deserves a careful second look.

## Short Answer

If a contractor cuts roots near a Florida tree, do not assume the tree will recover just because the canopy still looks green. Take photos, stop additional digging near the root zone if possible, document where the roots were cut, and ask for a qualified tree-risk or arborist evaluation if the tree is large, close to the home, leaning, recently storm-stressed, or near a driveway, pool cage, fence, sidewalk, or utility area.

Small feeder-root disturbance at the edge of the canopy may be less serious. Large root cuts close to the trunk are different. They can affect health, decay resistance, and stability.

## First, Identify What Kind of Root Damage Happened

Not every root cut means the same thing.

A shallow scrape from edging near a small ornamental tree is not the same as a trench through large structural roots beside a mature oak.

Start by looking at four things:

- how close the cut was to the trunk
- whether large woody roots were severed
- how many roots were cut in one area
- whether the tree is near a structure or high-use space

Large roots close to the trunk are more concerning because they may help anchor the tree. Feeder roots farther from the trunk matter too, but their loss is usually more about water uptake and stress than immediate stability.

## Take Photos Before Anything Is Covered

Once soil, mulch, sod, gravel, or concrete goes back in place, it becomes much harder to understand what happened.

Before the area is covered, photograph:

- the whole tree from several angles
- the trunk and root flare
- the trench, cut, or disturbed soil line
- close-ups of any severed roots
- nearby structures, pavers, fence posts, irrigation, or utility markings
- any leaning, soil lifting, cracks, or open voids near the base

Use one wider photo that shows the distance between the root cut and the trunk. Then take closer images of the damaged roots.

This is not about blaming someone right away. It is about preserving the facts while they are still visible.

## Do Not Cut More Roots to “Clean It Up”

A common mistake is trimming torn roots aggressively after the damage is discovered. That can make the injury larger.

If a root is shredded, split, or exposed, the right response depends on root size, location, tree species, and stability concerns. A qualified arborist may recommend a clean cut in some situations. In other situations, leaving the area alone and protecting the soil is better.

Do not start chopping roots just to make the area look tidy.

A smooth-looking trench can still be a serious injury.

## Watch the Tree, But Do Not Rely Only on the Canopy

After root damage, the canopy may stay green for weeks or months. That can give homeowners false confidence.

Trees often show delayed stress.

A tree may later show:

- thinning canopy
- smaller leaves
- premature leaf drop
- dead branch tips
- sudden water sprouts
- mushrooms or decay near the base
- new lean or worsening lean
- soil cracks or lifting near the trunk
- exposed root movement after rain or wind

The most important warning signs are not always in the leaves. The base of the tree, soil surface, trunk flare, and root plate often tell a more serious story.

## When Root Cutting Becomes a Stability Concern

Root damage becomes more serious when the tree is large and the cut is close to the trunk.

In Florida yards, this matters because storms can turn hidden root weakness into a real hazard. A tree may tolerate damage during calm weather and then fail later during saturated soil or wind.

Be especially cautious if the tree is:

- leaning toward a home, garage, fence, pool cage, driveway, or sidewalk
- a mature oak, pine, large ficus, sweetgum, or other broad-canopy tree
- already hollow, cracked, decayed, or fungus-covered near the base
- recently affected by construction, flooding, trenching, grading, or hardscape work
- standing in wet soil or a drainage area
- located where people, cars, or children regularly pass underneath

The question is not just “Will the tree live?”

A better question is: “Did the damage change the tree’s risk profile?”

## What to Ask the Contractor

Stay calm and factual. Ask questions that help you understand what happened.

Useful questions include:

- Where exactly were roots cut?
- How deep was the trench or excavation?
- Were large woody roots removed or just small feeder roots?
- Was utility locating completed before digging?
- Was the work inside the tree’s root zone?
- Were any roots cut because they were lifting pavers, fence posts, or concrete?
- Will the area be covered with soil, concrete, sod, mulch, or gravel?
- Is there a written scope showing what work was planned?

If the contractor has photos from before or during the work, ask for copies.

## When an Arborist Report Is Worth It

An arborist report is not needed for every scraped root.

It is worth considering when the tree is large, valuable, close to a structure, involved in a permit/HOA issue, part of an insurance discussion, or showing visible risk signs.

A useful evaluation may document:

- species and approximate size
- location of the root damage
- distance from trunk to root cut
- visible decay, cracks, lean, or canopy symptoms
- whether additional inspection is recommended
- whether pruning, monitoring, root-zone protection, or removal should be considered

For high-risk situations, a basic “it looks okay” opinion may not be enough. Written documentation can help homeowners, property managers, HOAs, insurers, and contractors make clearer decisions.

## What Not to Do After Root Damage

Avoid quick fixes that can create more problems.

Do not pile soil over exposed roots to hide the damage. Too much soil over the root flare can trap moisture and create decay problems.

Do not install heavy pavers or concrete directly over the disturbed root area without thinking about drainage and future inspection.

Do not compact the soil with equipment if the root zone is already damaged.

Do not assume fertilizer will solve a cut-root problem. Fertilizer cannot replace structural roots.

Do not remove the tree immediately unless there is a clear safety reason. Some trees can be monitored or protected. Others may need more urgent action. The right answer depends on the tree and the site.

## Better Questions to Ask Before the Next Project

If you are planning fence work, paver repair, irrigation changes, grading, trenching, or driveway work near trees, ask questions before the first cut is made.

For example:

- Which trees are close enough to be affected?
- Where is the root flare?
- Can the trench be moved farther from the trunk?
- Can utilities be routed around major roots?
- Can equipment stay outside the critical root area?
- Should an arborist review the plan before work starts?
- Will the contractor document conditions before digging?

This is especially important for older Florida lots where roots, irrigation, utilities, pavers, and fence lines may all overlap.

## What Homeowners Should Monitor Afterward

After root damage, check the tree after heavy rain, strong wind, and the first few weeks of dry weather.

Look for changes near the base:

- new soil cracks
- lifting ground on one side
- exposed roots that seem to move
- new mushrooms or soft wood near the trunk
- widening trunk cracks
- a lean that appears new or worse

Also watch the canopy over time. A sudden decline is concerning, but gradual thinning can matter too.

Take follow-up photos from the same angles every few weeks. This makes changes easier to notice.

## Final Takeaway

If a contractor cuts tree roots near your Florida home, the safest response is not panic. It is documentation and careful evaluation.

Small root disturbance may only need monitoring and better root-zone protection. Large cuts near the trunk, especially on mature trees near structures or high-use areas, deserve professional attention.

## FAQs

## Can a tree survive after roots are cut?

Yes, some trees survive root cutting, especially when the damage is limited and farther from the trunk. The risk increases when large roots close to the trunk are cut or when multiple roots are damaged on the same side.

## How soon will a tree show stress after root damage?

Sometimes stress appears quickly. In other cases, decline shows months later, especially after heat, drought, heavy rain, or storms. A green canopy right after root damage does not guarantee the tree is stable.

## Should I cover exposed roots with soil?

Do not bury the root flare or pile soil deeply over exposed roots. A light, appropriate mulch layer may help in some situations, but heavy soil or mulch against the trunk can create moisture and decay problems.

## Is a contractor responsible if root cutting makes a tree unsafe?

Responsibility depends on the contract, scope of work, property conditions, local rules, and documentation. Save photos, written estimates, messages, and any arborist evaluation. For disputes, speak with the appropriate professional or legal advisor.

## Should I remove the tree after root damage?

Not always. Some trees can be monitored or protected. Removal becomes more likely when the tree has large root loss near the trunk, visible instability, decay, worsening lean, or a high-risk target such as a home, driveway, pool cage, sidewalk, or power line.
