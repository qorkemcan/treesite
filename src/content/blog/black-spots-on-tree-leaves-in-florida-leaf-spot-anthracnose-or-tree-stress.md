# Black Spots on Tree Leaves in Florida: Leaf Spot, Anthracnose, or Tree Stress?

Black spots are a symptom, not a diagnosis.

In a Florida yard, dark leaf marks can come from fungal or bacterial leaf spots, anthracnose, insects and their residues, sooty growth, algal disease on certain hosts, nutrient or water stress, spray injury, salt, heat, or ordinary aging of older foliage. Several causes can look similar in a phone photo.

The first decision is not whether to spray or remove the tree. It is whether the pattern is limited to leaves, severe enough to justify plant diagnosis, or accompanied by trunk, root, and structural warning signs.

## Use the pattern to choose the next step

| What you observe | Better first interpretation | Next step |
|---|---|---|
| A few older leaves with spots after wet weather | Often a foliar or seasonal issue | Photograph, correct obvious irrigation problems, and monitor. |
| Spots spreading across new growth | Active disease, pest, or site stress may be involved | Identify the tree and seek diagnostic guidance. |
| Sticky residue or black coating that wipes off | Sooty growth associated with insects may be possible | Identify the insect and host first. |
| Dark lesions with twig dieback or cankers | More than a cosmetic leaf issue | Use Extension or plant-diagnostic guidance. |
| Heavy repeated defoliation | Tree vigor may be affected | Review irrigation, roots, and site stress. |
| Spots plus trunk cracks, decay, root movement, or new lean | Plant health and tree risk are separate concerns | Keep people away from targets and request assessment. |

A spotted leaf rarely tells you whether a tree is structurally safe.

## Diagnosis before treatment

Identify the host plant before naming the disease. Anthracnose, leaf spot, sooty mold, nutrient stress, and irrigation problems can overlap visually.
