# Best Trees for Wet Yards in Florida

A wet yard makes a lot of homeowners think they have a drainage problem that needs to be fixed before they can plant anything.

Sometimes that is true.

But sometimes the smarter answer is simpler: choose trees that can actually tolerate the site. A tree that struggles in soggy soil will keep looking stressed no matter how healthy it looked at the nursery. A tree that tolerates seasonal wetness, pond edges, swales, or low areas can turn the same difficult yard into a more successful planting area.

The key question is not just “What tree do I like?” It is: **What tree can handle the kind of wetness this site creates?**

## Not all wet yards are the same

A wet yard can mean:

- standing water after storms,
- seasonally soggy soil,
- a low area that stays damp,
- a slow-draining lawn,
- acidic wet soil,
- a pond, ditch, or swale edge,
- periodic flooding followed by drying,
- irrigation or drainage mistakes.

Trees that handle periodic wetness may still fail in constantly flooded soil. Trees that like moisture may still need oxygen around their roots.

## Match the tree to the wetness pattern

| Site condition | Planting question |
|---|---|
| Short-term storm water | How long does water remain? |
| Seasonal wetness | Does the area dry between events? |
| Pond or swale edge | Is the soil always damp or sometimes flooded? |
| Irrigation problem | Is water being added unnecessarily? |
| Compacted low spot | Can roots get oxygen? |
| Existing tree decline | Is wet soil causing root stress? |

For saturated-soil context, see [Flooding and Tree Health: Managing Saturated Soil](/blog/flooding-and-tree-health-managing-saturated-soil/) and [Why Tree Beds Stay Mushy](/blog/why-tree-beds-stay-mushy-overwatering-drainage-or-mulch-breakdown/).

## Trees often considered for wet Florida sites

Species suitability depends on location, space, soil, salt exposure, utility clearance, and local ecosystem.

Options commonly considered for wetter Florida conditions include:

- bald cypress,
- pond cypress,
- red maple in appropriate sites,
- dahoon holly,
- swamp tupelo where space allows,
- sweetbay magnolia in suitable areas,
- certain wetland-edge native species.

Do not plant a large wet-site tree where mature size, roots, or canopy spread will conflict with a house, driveway, septic area, pool enclosure, or utility line.

## When drainage should come before planting

A wet-site tree is not a drainage-engineering solution.

Address drainage first when:

- water flows toward the foundation,
- the area smells anaerobic,
- septic components are nearby,
- standing water stays for long periods,
- several existing plants are declining,
- soil was recently compacted by construction,
- irrigation is oversaturating the bed.

For construction/root issues, see [Why Trees Decline After Construction Damage](/blog/why-trees-decline-after-construction-damage/).
