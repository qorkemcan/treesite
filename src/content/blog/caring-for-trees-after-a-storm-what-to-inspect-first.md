# Caring for Trees After a Storm: What to Inspect First

After a storm, the safest inspection begins **before anyone walks under the trees**.

The first task is not cleanup. It is identifying places where electricity, fire, floodwater, moving soil, suspended wood, or a loaded structure makes the area unsafe to enter.

## Start with this safety screen

| What you see | Do not do | First action |
|---|---|---|
| Tree or limb touching a power line | Do not approach, touch nearby fences, vehicles, water, or debris | Keep everyone away and contact the utility or 911 as appropriate |
| Smoke, fire, arcing, or damaged electrical equipment | Do not investigate from beneath the tree | Call emergency services and the utility |
| Tree resting on a roof, car, fence, or another tree | Do not cut the piece that appears easiest | Establish an exclusion zone and request controlled assessment |
| Hanging or partly attached major wood | Do not stand below it for photographs | Restrict access and treat it as active failure |
| Lifted soil, root-plate movement, or new severe lean | Do not test the tree by pushing or cutting | Keep people and vehicles outside the likely fall zone |
| Floodwater around electrical or tree damage | Do not enter the water | Wait for electrical and site clearance |
| Stable debris fully on the ground | Do not assume all overhead hazards are gone | Look up and inspect the source tree first |

Use [What to Do If a Tree Is Touching Power Lines](/blog/what-to-do-if-a-tree-is-touching-power-lines-after-a-storm/) when utility contact is possible.

## Inspect in this order

### 1. Targets and access

Start with trees that could reach:

- occupied rooms
- roof and garage
- front entry
- driveway
- public sidewalk
- road
- pool enclosure
- neighbor’s property
- essential access route

A defect in open space may have a different urgency than the same defect above a bedroom or entrance.

### 2. Base and surrounding soil

From a safe distance, look for:

- fresh soil cracks
- mound or lifted soil
- exposed roots
- root plate shifted from its prior position
- gap at the trunk base
- erosion
- new movement after rain or wind

Use [Can an Uprooted Tree Be Saved in Florida?](/blog/can-an-uprooted-tree-be-saved-in-florida/) when the root plate has moved.

### 3. Lean change

A long-standing lean is not the same as a new storm-related change.

Compare with older photos and ask:

- Did the trunk angle change?
- Is the canopy now farther over a target?
- Did soil lift on the opposite side?
- Does the lean worsen after follow-up rain?
- Is the tree moving differently from nearby trees?

### 4. Hanging, broken, or supported wood

Look for:

- partly attached limbs
- cracked unions
- suspended branches
- wood resting in another tree
- sections supported by a roof or fence
- broken tops
- debris hiding loaded branches below

Do not pull, winch, climb, or cut loaded wood yourself.

### 5. Trunk and major unions

Check from the ground for:

- fresh cracks
- widened seams
- bark blowout
- split codominant stems
- impact wounds
- cavity changes
- exposed wood
- fungal structures at the base

Use [Can a Split Tree Be Saved After a Storm?](/blog/can-a-split-tree-be-saved-after-a-storm/) when separation is visible.

### 6. Canopy change

A tree may remain standing but lose enough crown to become:

- heavily one-sided
- newly exposed to wind
- loaded toward a target
- structurally altered
- vulnerable to delayed branch failure

Green leaves do not prove that the remaining structure is reliable.

## Document before conditions change

From outside the hazard area, photograph:

- full tree and target
- root flare and soil
- trunk
- damaged union
- hanging wood
- canopy loss
- utility location
- structure contact
- access route
- debris before removal

Record the date, storm, wind direction if known, and whether the condition changes afterward.

Use [What to Document Before Removing a Hazardous Tree in Florida](/blog/what-to-document-before-removing-a-hazardous-tree-in-florida/) when insurance, ownership, or later review may matter.

## Recheck after follow-up weather

Storm damage may remain incomplete.

Recheck after:

- additional rain
- strong gusts
- saturated-soil conditions
- cleanup that changes support
- removal of a neighboring tree
- visible crack growth
- new branch drop

Use [Can Storm-Damaged Trees Fail Days Later?](/blog/can-storm-damaged-trees-fail-days-later/) for delayed-failure signs.

## Separate inspection from cleanup

The sequence should be:

1. screen electrical and life-safety hazards
2. establish exclusion zones
3. document conditions
4. identify active tree failures
5. decide what can wait
6. define trimming, removal, debris, and stump scopes separately
7. begin cleanup only where the site is stable

A clean yard is not evidence that the standing trees were evaluated.
