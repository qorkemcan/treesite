# Tree Removal Near Power Lines: What Florida Homeowners Should Never Do

If a tree is touching, leaning into, or tangled with a power line, do not cut it, pull it, climb it, or try to move branches away yourself. Treat the situation as dangerous even if the line is not sparking and even if the power appears to be out.

In most cases, the first call should be to the utility company or emergency services, not a standard tree crew. Once the electrical hazard is cleared or the utility confirms the area is safe, a qualified tree service can help with [tree removal services](/services/tree-removal/), cleanup, hauling, [stump grinding services](/services/stump-grinding/), or follow-up pruning.

This is especially important in Florida after thunderstorms, tropical storms, and hurricanes, when wet ground, hidden wires, broken limbs, and unstable trees can all be present at the same time.

## Why trees near power lines are different

A normal tree removal job already requires planning. A tree near power lines adds a completely different level of risk.

The issue is not just the tree. It is the combination of:

- overhead electrical lines,
- wet branches,
- storm-damaged limbs under tension,
- ladders, pole saws, ropes, and chainsaws,
- fences, roofs, driveways, and vehicles nearby,
- homeowners trying to clear access after a storm.

In Florida, this situation can happen quickly. A tree that looked fine before a storm may drop a limb across a service line. A palm frond can hang into a wire. A pine may lean after saturated soil loosens the root zone. An oak limb may split and rest near the line without fully falling.

That half-fallen, half-hanging position is often where people get into trouble.

## What homeowners should never do

Do not:

| Action | Why it is dangerous |
|---|---|
| Cut branches touching wires | The tree, tool, or ground may be energized. |
| Pull limbs with a rope or truck | Movement can bring wires down or shift tension. |
| Climb the tree | You may enter the electrical hazard zone. |
| Use a ladder or pole saw | Tools and ladders can conduct or contact wires. |
| Move fallen wires | Downed lines may still be energized. |
| Stand near wet ground under wires | Electricity can travel through wet surfaces. |
| Assume the line is dead | Power may return unexpectedly. |

If there is any doubt, stay away.

## Who to call first

For active electrical hazards, call the utility company or emergency services first.

A tree company may not be able to work until:

- the utility confirms the line is safe,
- power is de-energized when necessary,
- downed wires are handled,
- the work area is cleared,
- the tree can be approached safely.

After that, a tree service can help with removal, cleanup, trimming, or storm debris. In urgent non-electrical tree situations, [emergency response services](/services/emergency-response/) may be appropriate.

## Service drop versus main line

Some homeowners assume a small line to the house is not dangerous. Do not make that assumption.

Lines can be hard to identify from the ground. Cable, phone, service drop, and electrical lines can be confused, especially after storms or when branches are tangled in several wires.

Do not decide the line is safe based on appearance. Contact the utility or qualified service provider.

## Storm cleanup can make power-line risk worse

After storms, many hazards overlap:

- broken branches under tension,
- leaning trees,
- flooded or saturated ground,
- metal fences,
- wet driveways,
- hidden wires under debris,
- unstable root plates,
- hanging limbs above access paths.

Even if the tree is not touching the line now, a cut branch may swing into it. A leaning trunk may shift. A rope may pull a limb into the wrong place.

For delayed storm risk, see [why some trees fail days after a storm instead of during it](/blog/why-some-trees-fail-days-after-a-storm-instead-of-during-it/).

## When a tree service can help

A tree service can usually help after electrical danger has been addressed.

They may handle:

- removing broken limbs,
- dismantling the tree in sections,
- clearing safe access,
- hauling debris,
- grinding the stump,
- trimming remaining tree parts,
- checking nearby damaged trees.

But the tree service should not be treated as the utility company. Electrical safety comes first.

## Questions to ask after the utility clears the area

Ask the tree service:

- Has the utility cleared the work area?
- Is any line still near the tree?
- Will the tree be removed whole or in sections?
- Are limbs under tension?
- Where is the drop zone?
- How will the crew protect the house, fence, driveway, or pool cage?
- Is cleanup and hauling included?
- Is stump grinding included or separate?

If the answer is “we can just cut it,” slow down and ask for the method.

## What to document from a safe distance

Take photos only from a safe location. Do not walk under wires or damaged limbs.

Photograph:

- the whole tree and nearby line,
- fallen limbs from a distance,
- visible damage to the house, fence, driveway, or vehicle,
- the tree’s lean direction,
- the access path,
- utility work order or communication if available.

These photos can help with utility communication, insurance, and tree-service planning.
