# Best Wind-Resistant Trees for Florida Yards: What Homeowners Should Know

No tree is hurricane-proof.

UF/IFAS hurricane research describes **relative wind resistance**, not a guarantee that a species will survive every storm.

Storm performance reflects the combined effect of:

- species
- root health
- soil
- planting depth
- nursery structure
- pruning history
- canopy size
- tree grouping
- target exposure
- storm wind, rain, and duration

A highly rated species planted badly in restricted soil can still fail.

## Use this wind-performance framework

| Factor | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Species evidence | Some species performed better than others in post-hurricane surveys |
| Regional fit | Tropical, subtropical, and coastal-plain lists are not interchangeable |
| Rooting area | Roots need uncompacted, usable soil |
| Planting quality | Deep planting and root defects can compromise establishment |
| Young-tree structure | A dominant leader and well-spaced branches can improve form |
| Canopy maintenance | Topping and poor cuts can create future weakness |
| Exposure | Open corners, coastlines, and wind corridors increase loading |
| Tree grouping | Research observed better survival for some trees in groups than as isolated individuals |
| Targets | Risk depends on what a failed part could strike |

## Species lists are regional

UF/IFAS maintains different wind-resistance discussions for:

- tropical and subtropical regions
- southeastern coastal plain
- specific palms and trees

Do not copy a South Florida species list into North Florida without checking cold hardiness and local site conditions.

## Trees commonly appearing in stronger-performance groups

Depending on region and source, research has identified higher relative performance for examples such as:

- live oak
- sand live oak
- southern magnolia
- bald cypress
- sabal palm
- some hollies
- dogwood in suitable northern areas
- crape myrtle
- selected small and medium native trees

This does not mean every individual tree of those species is safe or suitable for every yard.

## Root space and anchorage

A tree needs usable soil beyond the nursery root ball.

Poor conditions include:

- narrow cutouts in pavement
- compacted fill
- roots cut for utilities or hardscape
- buried root flare
- chronic saturation for a poorly adapted species
- repeated vehicle traffic
- grade changes

Wind selection without root-space planning is incomplete.

## Planting depth

Planting too deeply can bury the root flare and contribute to poor root development.

At installation:

- locate the first major roots
- place the root flare at the appropriate grade
- avoid deep amended planting holes
- correct circling or defective roots as appropriate
- keep mulch away from the trunk

Follow current UF/IFAS planting guidance.

## Young-tree structural pruning

Early pruning can encourage:

- a dominant leader where appropriate
- well-spaced scaffold branches
- smaller future cuts
- balanced canopy
- reduced codominant-stem development

Topping is not hurricane preparation.

Repeated removal of healthy interior foliage can also reduce canopy function without addressing the real defect.

## Palms

Palms differ structurally from branching trees.

Sabal palm has shown strong relative wind performance in UF/IFAS research, but individual outcome still depends on:

- root condition
- planting depth
- trunk injury
- disease
- site
- species
- crown health

Do not remove healthy green fronds for a “hurricane cut.”

## Pines and oaks

“Pine” and “oak” are broad categories.

Pine performance varies by species, root condition, stand exposure, soil, and damage history.

Oak performance also varies; live oak should not be treated as interchangeable with every oak species.

Use species-level identification and regional evidence.

## Coastal exposure

A tree near direct salt spray must fit both wind and salinity conditions.

Use [Best Trees for Coastal Florida Yards](/blog/best-trees-for-coastal-florida-yards-with-salt-and-wind-exposure/) for the exposure matrix.

## Planting near targets

Even a relatively wind-resistant species should not be placed without considering:

- occupied rooms
- driveway
- road
- pool cage
- neighboring property
- overhead lines
- emergency access

Mature size and target consequence remain part of the planting decision.

## Existing-tree storm preparation

For established trees, useful preparation may include:

- inspection of deadwood and major defects
- structural pruning where appropriate
- removing hanging or broken branches
- protecting roots from construction
- documenting existing condition
- avoiding last-minute topping

Visit [tree trimming services](/services/tree-trimming/) for safe existing-tree pruning.

A tree with active movement, a major opening split, or root-plate failure is not a planting-selection issue. It requires a separate risk response.
