# Questions to Ask Before Hiring a Tree Removal Service in Florida

A useful hiring conversation should tell you three things: **who is responsible, how the tree will be handled, and what the property will look like when the crew leaves**.

You do not need to interview a company for an hour. These ten questions cover the decisions most likely to affect safety, price, cleanup, and disputes.

## 1. Can You Provide Current Insurance and Workers' Compensation Information?

Ask for current general liability documentation and workers' compensation coverage or applicable exemption information. Confirm that the business name on the documents matches the estimate.

Useful follow-up questions include:

- Does the policy cover the type of tree work being proposed?
- Are subcontractors covered?
- Will a crane or separate equipment provider be involved?
- Can the insurance agent or carrier confirm the certificate?

Insurance does not prove skill, but vague or inconsistent documents are a reason to pause. For a deeper verification process, read [Licensed and Insured Tree Service in Florida: What Homeowners Should Check](/blog/licensed-and-insured-tree-service-in-florida-what-homeowners-should-check/).

## 2. Who Will Actually Perform and Supervise the Work?

The estimator, crew, climber, stump-grinding operator, and crane company may be different people or businesses.

Ask:

- Who is the on-site supervisor?
- Are workers employees or subcontractors?
- Who is responsible if the scope changes?
- Will the same company complete hauling and stump work?
- Who should the homeowner speak with during the job?

Subcontracting is not automatically a problem. Unclear responsibility is.

## 3. How Will This Tree Be Removed?

A provider should be able to explain the plan in plain language.

The answer may involve:

- felling into an open drop zone
- climbing and dismantling in sections
- rigging and controlled lowering
- a bucket truck or lift
- a crane
- hand-carrying wood through a narrow yard
- traffic control or utility coordination

The plan should fit the tree and the site. A large oak over a roof is not handled like a small tree in an open lawn.

## 4. Are Power Lines or Other Utilities Involved?

Overhead lines are a potentially fatal tree-care hazard. If branches touch or are close to electrical lines, ask whether the utility must be involved and whether the crew is qualified for the work being proposed.

Do not accept a casual “that wire is fine.” When a tree is touching a power line, stay away and contact the utility or emergency authorities before arranging ordinary tree work.

Underground utilities also matter for stump grinding, equipment setup, and root work.

## 5. Who Checks Permits, HOA Rules, and Property Boundaries?

Florida requirements can vary by jurisdiction, property type, tree condition, species, right-of-way status, and HOA documents.

Ask:

- Is the property inside a city or unincorporated county?
- Does a permit or local approval apply?
- Is the tree in a right-of-way, preserve, wetland, coastal area, or easement?
- Does the HOA require approval?
- Who is responsible for the application, fees, and documentation?
- Is a certified arborist or Florida-licensed landscape architect assessment being used?

Florida Statute 163.045 applies only within the conditions described in the law. It should not be treated as permission to remove any tree without checking the facts.

## 6. How Will You Protect the Property and Access the Tree?

Ask the provider to identify the main exposures:

- roof and gutters
- pool cage
- pavers and driveway
- fence and neighboring property
- irrigation and landscape lighting
- septic components or drain field
- lawn and planting beds
- vehicles and pedestrian access

If equipment will cross pavers or soft soil, ask what will be placed underneath it. If the backyard gate is narrow, confirm that the planned equipment can actually reach the tree.

## 7. What Is Included in the Written Estimate?

The estimate should identify the exact tree and define the work. Confirm:

- full removal or partial work
- removal method
- hauling
- cleanup
- stump grinding
- chip handling
- permit or HOA responsibility
- exclusions
- payment terms
- change-order process
- final site condition

A one-line estimate that says only “remove tree” is not enough. Use [What Should Be in a Written Tree Removal Estimate?](/blog/what-should-be-in-a-written-tree-removal-estimate/) to compare the details.

## 8. What Will Be Hauled Away, and What Will Remain?

Cleanup causes many tree-service disputes because people use the same word to mean different things.

Ask whether the crew will remove or leave:

- branches
- logs
- trunk sections
- palm fronds
- chips
- sawdust
- stump grindings
- unrelated storm debris

Also ask whether the yard will be raked, the driveway blown off, and debris placed at the curb. If municipal pickup is part of the plan, confirm the local rules.

The detailed [tree removal cleanup guide](/blog/tree-removal-cleanup-what-should-be-included-in-the-quote/) provides a fuller checklist.

## 9. Is Stump Grinding Included?

Tree removal often ends at ground level unless stump work is written into the estimate.

Clarify:

- grinding depth
- visible surface roots
- chip removal
- fill and grading
- irrigation or utility concerns
- backyard access
- whether the work happens the same day
- whether the area will be ready for sod or replanting

Compare the removal price plus stump work—not only the removal line.

## 10. What Happens if the Plan or Price Changes?

Hidden decay, unsafe climbing conditions, a blocked setup, unexpected utilities, or additional damaged wood can change a job.

Ask:

- What conditions can change the price?
- Who can approve added work?
- Will the change be documented?
- What if a crane becomes necessary?
- What if only emergency mitigation can be completed on the first visit?
- When is final payment due?

A clear change-order process protects both the homeowner and the provider.

## Three Questions to Ask When Time Is Limited

After a storm, you may not have time for a long comparison. At minimum, ask:

1. **What active hazard are you addressing first?**
2. **What exactly is included in this price, and what will remain?**
3. **Can you provide current business and insurance documentation?**

If power lines, active movement, or danger to people are involved, contact the appropriate utility or emergency authority first.

## Red Flags Before You Agree

Pause or obtain another quote when a provider:

- refuses a written scope
- will not provide insurance information
- says permits never matter
- dismisses power-line hazards
- cannot explain the removal method
- ignores access or property protection
- demands full payment upfront
- gives no answer about cleanup or stump grinding
- pressures you to decide immediately without documentation
- asks for payment to a different unexplained business or person

A low price can be valid. A low-information job is harder to trust.

## Prepare for Better Quotes

Before contacting providers, take photos of the whole tree, trunk, base, crown, access route, and nearby structures. Measure the narrowest gate. Decide whether you want hauling and stump grinding. Gather any HOA or permit information already available.

## FAQ

### What is the most important question before hiring a tree service?

Ask for a clear work plan and current insurance and workers' compensation information. The provider should also identify the exact tree, cleanup scope, and responsibility for permits or HOA approval.

### Should I choose a certified arborist for removal?

An arborist is especially useful when diagnosis, risk assessment, restoration potential, or official documentation is needed. A removal crew still needs the equipment and experience to perform the field work safely.

### Does tree removal include stump grinding?

Not automatically. Ask for stump grinding, depth, roots, chips, fill, and scheduling to be stated in writing.

### How much should I pay upfront?

Payment practices vary, but be cautious with large full-payment demands before work begins, especially after storms. The written estimate should state the deposit and payment schedule.
