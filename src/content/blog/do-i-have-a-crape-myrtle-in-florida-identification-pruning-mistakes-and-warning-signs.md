# Do I Have a Crape Myrtle in Florida? Identification, Pruning Mistakes, and Warning Signs

You may have a crape myrtle if the tree has smooth peeling bark, clusters of summer flowers, small oval leaves, and a multi-stemmed form. In Florida yards, crape myrtles are common near homes because they stay smaller than many shade trees and can handle heat when planted in the right place.

Bad pruning is the main issue, not the tree name. A crape myrtle can look tidy after a hard cut and still be weaker, less attractive, or more problem-prone later.

If the tree is splitting, declining, leaning, rubbing a structure, or has been cut back into thick stubs year after year, it may need better [tree trimming services](/services/tree-trimming/) rather than another aggressive trim.

## How to recognize a crape myrtle

Crape myrtles have several traits that make them stand out from many Florida yard trees.

Look for:

- smooth bark that sheds in patches,
- tan, gray, cinnamon, or light brown exposed bark,
- clusters of crinkled summer flowers,
- small oval leaves,
- multiple trunks or stems,
- seed capsules after flowering,
- vase-shaped or rounded canopy when naturally grown.

A mature crape myrtle can look sculptural in winter because the bark and branching pattern remain visible after leaf drop.

## Why crape myrtles are over-pruned

Many homeowners and landscapers cut crape myrtles back hard because they want a smaller tree, more flowers, or a tidy winter appearance.

The problem is that repeated hard heading cuts can create thick knuckles, weak sprouts, crowded regrowth, and an unnatural form. The tree may bloom, but the structure can become worse over time.

Good pruning should focus on:

| Goal | Better approach |
|---|---|
| Improve clearance | Selective cuts, not topping. |
| Remove deadwood | Cut dead or broken branches cleanly. |
| Reduce rubbing | Remove crossing or crowded stems thoughtfully. |
| Maintain structure | Preserve strong stems and natural form. |
| Control suckers | Remove small base growth without damaging bark. |

## Peeling bark is often normal

Smooth peeling bark can be normal on crape myrtle. It is one of the reasons the tree is valued ornamentally.

Bark changes are more concerning when they appear with:

- soft wood,
- cankers,
- major dieback,
- trunk cracks,
- fungal growth,
- severe decline,
- repeated branch failures.

Do not confuse attractive bark exfoliation with the kind of bark loss that suggests decline.

## When warning signs deserve attention

Look closer when the crape myrtle has:

- large dead sections,
- heavy leaning,
- splitting stems,
- weak attachments,
- repeated storm breakage,
- leaf disease plus overall decline,
- roots or trunk damaged by mowing,
- branches rubbing a roof, fence, or walkway.

A crape myrtle is rarely an emergency tree by default, but poor structure and bad placement can still create problems.
