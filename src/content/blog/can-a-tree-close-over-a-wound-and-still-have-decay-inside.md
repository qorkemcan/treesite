## Short Answer

Yes. A tree can grow new wood around an old wound and still have decay inside.

That does not always mean the tree is about to fail. Trees do not heal like skin. They respond to injury by closing over the wound from the outside and trying to wall off damaged wood on the inside. Sometimes that response works well. Other times, decay continues behind what looks like a sealed wound.

For Florida homeowners, the concern is not simply whether the bark has closed over. The bigger question is whether the old wound is connected to hollow wood, soft tissue, cracks, mushrooms, oozing sap, weak branch unions, or a lean that has changed after storms.

## Why a Closed Wound Can Be Misleading

A smooth roll of wood around an old cut can look reassuring. It may mean the tree has been growing well enough to close the edge of the wound.

But that outer closure does not automatically prove the inside is solid.

When a trunk, branch, or root is wounded, the tree responds by forming barriers around the injured area. Those barriers may slow the spread of decay, but they do not replace the damaged wood with new solid wood in the same way human skin repairs a cut.

That is why an old wound can look quiet on the surface while still hiding internal weakness.

Florida makes this even more important because trees here may deal with heavy rain, saturated soil, hurricane-season wind, high humidity, insect pressure, and repeated pruning or storm damage over the years.

## What Homeowners Often Notice First

A closed wound may deserve a closer look when it comes with other clues.

A homeowner may notice:

- a large old pruning cut that has rolled over at the edges but still has a dark center
- a hollow sound when the trunk is tapped
- mushrooms, conks, or shelf-like fungal growth near the wound
- sawdust, insect holes, or ant activity around the old injury
- bark that is loose, sunken, cracked, or peeling around the wound
- sap, dark staining, or wet-looking streaks
- a branch union that looks swollen, split, or deeply V-shaped
- a trunk cavity that appears larger than it did last year
- a limb over a roof, driveway, pool cage, or fence with an old wound near the attachment

One sign by itself may not answer the question. Several signs together are more important.

## Why Old Pruning Cuts Can Become Trouble Spots

Large pruning cuts are one of the most common places homeowners notice old wound closure.

A clean, properly placed cut near the branch collar gives the tree a better chance to close over the wound. A flush cut, torn cut, or oversized cut can leave the tree with a larger injury and less natural protection.

That does not mean every old pruning wound is dangerous. Many trees live for years with old cuts. The concern grows when the wound is large, low on the trunk, connected to a main scaffold limb, or located on a tree already showing decline.

A wound on a small side branch is one thing. A wound at the base of a large oak, near a codominant stem, or under a heavy limb over the driveway is another.

## Closed Wound vs Cavity: What Is the Difference?

A closed wound means new tissue has grown around the outside edge of the injury.

A cavity means part of the wood inside has already decayed, broken down, or opened up.

A tree can have both. The outer edges may look sealed while the interior has a hollow or decayed section.

That is why homeowners should avoid judging the tree by appearance alone. A cavity may be mostly cosmetic in one tree and structurally important in another. The difference depends on location, size, species, remaining sound wood, canopy weight, lean, root condition, and nearby targets.

## Florida Trees Where Old Wounds Deserve Extra Attention

Old wounds can matter on any tree, but some Florida situations deserve more care.

### Mature oaks

Large live oaks and laurel oaks can carry heavy limbs over homes, streets, driveways, and pool cages. An old wound near a major limb attachment should not be ignored, especially if there is included bark, cracking, decay, or recent storm damage.

### Palms with trunk wounds

Palms are not built like hardwood trees. A trunk wound on a palm does not respond the same way an oak branch wound does. If a palm trunk has soft areas, conks, or dark wet decay near the lower trunk, that deserves attention.

### Pines after storms or construction work

Pines may show delayed decline after root disturbance, lightning, wind stress, or bark beetle activity. A wound that looks old may still matter if the canopy is thinning, the trunk is oozing, or sawdust appears near the base.

### Trees near hardscape or structures

Any old wound becomes more important when failure could affect a roof, fence, sidewalk, driveway, pool cage, power line, or neighboring property.

## What Not to Do

Do not dig into the wound to “see how bad it is.” That can open new tissue and make the problem worse.

Do not fill the cavity with concrete, foam, cement, or expanding material. Old-fashioned cavity filling may make the tree look repaired, but it does not restore structural wood.

Do not paint over the wound just to make it look cleaner. Wound dressing does not fix internal decay.

Do not cut large roots or remove major limbs as a guess. A tree that already has internal decay may respond poorly to additional injury.

## Better Questions to Ask

A better inspection starts with practical questions:

- Is the wound on the trunk, a major limb, or a small branch?
- Does the wound appear to be expanding, cracking, or sinking?
- Are there conks, mushrooms, ants, termites, sawdust, or soft wood nearby?
- Is the tree leaning more than before?
- Are there soil cracks or lifted roots around the base?
- Is the affected limb over a house, driveway, pool cage, or walkway?
- Has the tree been through recent storms, trenching, construction, or heavy equipment traffic?
- Has the canopy changed since the wound was first noticed?

These questions help separate an old cosmetic scar from a wound that may be connected to a larger structural issue.

## When Professional Help Is Worth It

A professional look is worth considering when the wound is large, low on the trunk, near a main branch union, or close to a target area.

It is also worth getting help if the tree has:

- a hollow near the base
- visible conks or bracket fungi
- a spreading crack
- a recent lean
- dead limbs above a driveway or roof
- storm damage around the same area
- insect activity near soft or decayed wood

The goal is not always removal. Sometimes the next step is monitoring, selective pruning, cabling, or reducing weight on a risky limb. Other times, removal is the safer option. The right answer depends on what is still sound, what is exposed, and what the tree could hit if it fails.

## How This Affects Tree Removal or Trimming Quotes

Old wounds and hidden decay can also change the work plan.

A tree that looks solid from the street may need more careful rigging if a major limb has internal decay. A trunk with suspected weakness may not be safe to drop whole. A crew may need a climber, bucket truck, crane, ropes, taglines, or a different drop zone than a homeowner expected.

That is why the cheapest quote is not always the clearest quote. If a tree has old wounds, cavities, conks, or decay signs, the estimate should explain how the crew plans to control the work.

## Final Takeaway

A closed wound is not always a safe wound.

It may mean the tree responded well and sealed the injury from the outside. It may also mean decay has been hidden behind new growth for years. Florida homeowners should pay close attention when old wounds appear near the trunk, root flare, major limbs, or anything the tree could damage if it failed.

If an old wound is paired with hollow wood, conks, cracks, sap, sawdust, insects, leaning, or storm damage, it is worth getting the tree looked at before the next major weather event.
