# Powdery Mildew on Florida Ornamentals: Prevention Tips

Powdery mildew is one of those plant problems that homeowners usually recognize before they really understand it.

A plant starts looking dusty. Leaves seem coated or faded. New growth distorts, buds do not open the way they should, and the shrub or ornamental starts looking tired even though it is still alive.

On many Florida ornamentals, powdery mildew is easier to prevent than to correct once the plant is already heavily covered. It is not always the most destructive disease in the landscape, but it can reduce appearance and vigor if the conditions stay favorable.

The better question is not only: **How do I spray this?**

It is: **Why is this ornamental giving powdery mildew such a good place to start?**

## What powdery mildew usually looks like

Homeowners often notice:

- light-colored dusty coating on leaves,
- gray-white patches on stems or buds,
- distorted foliage,
- stunted new growth,
- buds that do not open properly,
- early leaf drop on susceptible plants,
- repeated problems in shaded, humid, crowded spots.

## Prevention usually starts with the site

| Prevention step | Why it helps |
|---|---|
| Choose resistant cultivars when available | Reduces repeat pressure before planting. |
| Improve sunlight where appropriate | Many mildew-prone plants struggle in too much shade. |
| Increase air movement | Crowded foliage stays humid longer. |
| Avoid over-dense planting | Tight spacing creates disease-friendly pockets. |
| Prune moderately | Light, thoughtful thinning can help; harsh cutting can stress the plant. |
| Use labeled products correctly | Fungicides support management but do not fix a poor site. |
