# Tree Removal for Rental Properties in Florida: Landlord, Tenant, and Storm-Damage Checklist

Tree work at a rental property should begin with **authorization and safety**, not with a tenant or vendor guessing who is responsible.

The practical workflow is:

1. report and secure the hazard
2. confirm who controls the tree and property
3. separate emergency stabilization from planned work
4. coordinate lawful access and tenant notice
5. confirm local approvals and utility issues
6. approve a written tree-service scope
7. document the final condition

This page explains the property-management workflow. Questions about casualty damage, lease rights, rent, or who ultimately pays belong in a separate legal and insurance review.

## Use this rental-property action table

| Situation | First practical action |
|---|---|
| Tenant sees a dead or hanging limb | Report it in writing with safe-distance photos |
| Tree or limb touches a power line | Stay away; contact 911 and the utility as appropriate |
| Tree is on the roof or blocking the only entrance | Secure the area and request emergency stabilization |
| Tree is stable but needs removal | Obtain owner or authorized-manager approval and a written estimate |
| Tree may be HOA, condo, right-of-way, or neighbor controlled | Confirm ownership and authority before authorizing work |
| Crew needs backyard or dwelling-unit access | Coordinate notice and access under the lease and applicable law |
| Storm damage affects habitability or personal property | Separate tree work from lease-rights and insurance questions |
| Stump remains in a tenant-use area | Define grinding, chips, fill, and final restoration in writing |

## Tenant role: report, document, and stay clear

A tenant should promptly report:

- a new lean
- split trunk
- hanging branch
- tree on a structure
- root-plate movement
- deadwood over an entrance or parking area
- blocked driveway
- power-line contact
- storm damage
- stump or root trip hazard

A useful written report includes:

- date and time
- property location
- safe-distance photos
- what is blocked or damaged
- whether the condition is changing
- whether utilities are involved
- whether children, pets, vehicles, or access are exposed

Tenants generally should not hire a crew to remove a large tree or alter the property without authorization unless the lease or a valid emergency instruction clearly permits it.

## Landlord or property-manager role: control the response

The authorized property representative should determine:

- who owns or controls the tree
- whether the condition is routine, planned, or urgent
- whether the insurer should be notified
- whether a local permit or other approval applies
- whether an HOA, condo association, easement holder, neighbor, or utility is involved
- what tenant access or notice is required
- what the tree contractor is authorized to do
- what work belongs to another trade

The landlord or manager should not rely on the tree crew to decide lease rights, insurance coverage, legal responsibility, or whether a damaged dwelling is safe to occupy.

## Separate three work categories

| Work category | Examples | Scope expectation |
|---|---|---|
| Routine maintenance | Limited deadwood, roof clearance, palm fronds | Scheduled trimming with normal access planning |
| Planned removal | Stable dead tree, chronic conflict, documented decline | Written removal, hauling, stump, permit, and restoration scope |
| Emergency stabilization | Tree on roof, blocked essential access, hanging major wood, active split | Immediate hazard reduction; full cleanup may require a later phase |

Emergency work may create safe access without completing the entire removal.

The invoice should distinguish:

- emergency mobilization
- stabilization
- complete removal
- hauling
- stump grinding
- landscape restoration
- return visits

## Access and notice

Florida Statute 83.53 addresses landlord access to a dwelling unit for inspection, repairs, services, and contractors, including notice rules and an emergency exception. The lease and the actual work area still matter.

For planned tree work, coordinate:

- date and work window
- gates and keys
- vehicles to move
- pets and children
- patio and yard items
- pool or amenity closure
- dwelling-unit access, if truly required
- irrigation and private utilities
- noise expectations
- rain-delay plan
- day-of contact

Do not use a tree appointment as a reason to enter parts of the premises that the work does not require.

## HOA, condo, right-of-way, and neighbor trees

Before approving removal, identify the trunk location and controlling documents.

The tree may be:

- on the rental lot
- on HOA common area
- a condominium common element
- in a utility or drainage easement
- within a public right-of-way
- in a preserve, wetland, or buffer
- on neighboring property

Use [HOA Tree Removal in Florida](/blog/hoa-tree-removal-in-florida-what-homeowners-and-boards-should-clarify-first/) or [Condo Association Tree Removal in Florida](/blog/condo-association-tree-removal-in-florida-common-elements-board-approval-and-storm-cleanup/) when association control may apply.

## Local tree rules and Florida Statute 163.045

City and county requirements vary.

Check for:

- tree-removal permits
- protected or specimen trees
- required replacement planting
- right-of-way approval
- wetland, preserve, or coastal rules
- approved landscape-plan requirements
- emergency documentation procedures

Florida Statute 163.045 defines “residential property” as a qualifying single-family detached building on a lot actively used for single-family residential purposes. It creates a specific documentation pathway when the statutory conditions are met.

Do not treat that section as a universal permit exemption for every rental, multifamily, condominium, commercial, or association-controlled property.

## Insurance and casualty questions are a separate track

When a tree damages the rental home:

- photograph the scene before cleanup when safe
- contact the property insurer for claim instructions
- preserve emergency invoices
- document temporary protective work
- separate building damage from tenant personal-property loss
- avoid promising coverage
- avoid deciding lease rights through the tree-service estimate

## What the written estimate should include

| Scope item | What to specify |
|---|---|
| Authorization | Owner or authorized manager approving the work |
| Tree identity | Exact tree and location |
| Urgency | Routine, planned, or emergency |
| Method | Climbing, lift, rigging, crane, or controlled drop |
| Access | Gates, parking, tenant areas, equipment route |
| Utilities | Overhead and underground concerns |
| Approvals | Permit, HOA, condo, right-of-way, or other responsibility |
| Property protection | Roof, pavers, driveway, fence, pool cage, irrigation |
| Debris | Branches, logs, chips, curb staging, hauling |
| Stump | Depth/result, roots, chip removal, fill, return visit |
| Final condition | Clear access, cleanup, photos, restoration exclusions |
| Records | Estimate, change orders, invoice, before/after photos |

## Stump and final-site decisions

A stump in a rental yard can create:

- trip exposure
- mowing conflict
- re-rental appearance issues
- pest concern
- drainage or settling problems
- obstruction near a walkway, driveway, or pool

If grinding is included, define:

- central stump
- surface roots
- target depth or result
- utility locating
- chip handling
- clean fill
- leveling
- sod or mulch
- return after settling
