# Why Tree Removal Over a Roof Needs a Different Plan in Florida

A roof-side removal is not just a tree job performed closer to a house.

The crew must control:

- where each section moves
- whether the roof is already carrying tree weight
- how gutters, soffits, skylights, vents, and pool enclosures are protected
- where workers can stand safely
- how debris leaves the property
- what happens if hidden decay changes the method

Tree removal, temporary weather protection, and roof repair are separate scopes. The written estimate should say who performs each one.

## First determine whether the tree is above the roof or loading the roof

These are different conditions.

### Tree above the roof

The tree is standing and limbs extend over the structure, but no major section is resting on it.

The job may be planned with:

- climbing
- rigging
- aerial lift
- crane assistance
- controlled lowering

### Tree touching or resting on the roof

A limb or trunk is already transferring weight to the structure.

The crew may need to coordinate:

- emergency stabilization
- weight removal
- exclusion zones
- roof access limitations
- temporary weather protection
- insurance documentation
- a roofing or structural professional

Do not climb onto the roof or cut the loaded wood yourself.

## Document the roof and tree before work

From a safe location, photograph:

- full tree and house
- contact point
- roof slope
- gutters and fascia
- visible openings
- damaged pool cage or screen
- tree base
- storm failure
- driveway and equipment route
- condition of accessible surfaces

Do not stand beneath suspended wood or enter a damaged roof area for a better image.

For a storm-claim checklist, use [What Photos Help After a Storm-Damaged Tree Claim?](/blog/what-photos-help-after-a-storm-damaged-tree-claim/).

## The crew needs a section-by-section movement plan

A heavy limb can:

- fall
- swing
- roll
- slide
- bounce
- tear additional wood
- catch another branch
- shift weight on the roof

The plan may involve:

- securing the section before cutting
- reducing it into smaller pieces
- using a lowering device
- lifting it with a crane
- moving it toward a landing zone
- supporting a loaded section
- keeping workers out of the potential movement path

The statement “we will protect the roof” is not enough. Ask how the wood will be controlled.

## Rigging versus crane lifting

### Rigging may fit when

- the tree is structurally reliable
- suitable anchor points exist
- sections can be lowered to a safe zone
- a trained climber can access the work
- the roof can be avoided during movement

### Crane assistance may fit when

- large sections must be lifted away
- the tree may be unsafe to climb
- lowering would put too much wood over the roof
- a legal and stable setup location exists
- reach, capacity, and lift path are appropriate

Neither method is automatically superior. The site and tree decide.

Use [Can a Tree Be Too Close to Remove Safely Without a Crane?](/blog/can-a-tree-be-too-close-to-remove-safely-without-a-crane/) for the broader equipment decision.

## Roof protection is more than laying down plywood

Temporary coverings may help in selected situations, but they do not replace controlled tree movement.

The crew may need to protect or avoid:

- shingles
- metal roofing
- tile
- gutters
- fascia
- soffits
- vents
- skylights
- solar equipment
- screen-enclosure connections
- rooftop HVAC or plumbing components

Some roof surfaces should not be walked on or loaded without appropriate roofing guidance.

The tree provider should not imply that it is performing roof repair unless that service and responsibility are explicitly included.

## Existing roof condition matters

Point out:

- old cracks
- loose tiles
- weak gutters
- prior leaks
- soft decking
- recent roof work
- solar panels
- previous storm damage

Take dated photographs before the crew begins.

A pre-work record helps distinguish:

- existing condition
- tree impact
- controlled removal
- later roof repair

It does not replace an insurance or roofing inspection.

## Keep the landing zone away from the structure

The safest landing zone may be:

- lawn
- driveway
- street-side staging area
- crane landing area
- protected side yard

The crew should explain:

- what can land there
- what surfaces need protection
- how logs are moved
- where brush is staged
- whether a crane or loader enters
- what areas remain closed

A roof-side tree often creates a property-wide work zone.

## Power lines and service equipment

Roof areas may contain:

- overhead service drops
- weather heads
- communications lines
- utility equipment
- solar wiring

OSHA treats energized-line exposure as a potentially fatal tree-care hazard and distinguishes trained line-clearance personnel from unqualified workers.

Do not assume a service drop is harmless because it appears coated.

The provider and utility must determine the safe electrical sequence.

## When the tree is already storm-damaged

The first visit may include only:

- removing unstable weight
- opening an entry
- taking pressure off the roof
- making the tree safe enough for a later phase
- staging debris

Confirm whether the first price includes:

- full tree removal
- all hauling
- stump grinding
- temporary roof protection
- return work
- final cleanup

Use [Should You Call Insurance or a Tree Service First After Storm Damage?](/blog/should-you-call-insurance-or-a-tree-service-first-after-storm-damage/) for the call-order decision.

## What homeowners should move

When safe, move:

- vehicles
- patio furniture
- grills
- planters
- toys
- hoses
- removable decorations
- items near the debris route

Do not move:

- tree-supported objects
- electrical items near damaged lines
- material beneath hanging wood
- anything requiring roof access

Keep pets and people out of the work zone.

## What a roof-side estimate should include

- exact tree and affected roof area
- full removal or emergency phase
- climbing, lift, rigging, or crane assumption
- landing zone
- roof and gutter protection plan
- line or utility coordination
- debris route
- hauling
- stump work
- temporary weather-protection responsibility
- roof-repair exclusion or inclusion
- existing-condition documentation
- change-order process
- final site condition

A vague “remove tree over house” line leaves too many decisions unresolved.

## Questions to ask

- Is the roof carrying tree weight?
- Is the tree safe to climb?
- What will be rigged or lifted?
- Will anyone work from the roof?
- Where will each section land?
- How will gutters and roof edges be protected?
- Are lines or service equipment involved?
- Who documents existing damage?
- Who handles temporary weather protection?
- Is a roofer or structural professional needed?
- Is all debris hauled?
- Is stump grinding separate?
- What could change the method?

The answers should make the division of responsibility clear.
