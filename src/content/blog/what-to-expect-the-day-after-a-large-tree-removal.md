# What to Expect the Day After a Large Tree Removal

The day after a large removal, the yard may be brighter, more open, and visibly disturbed.

Some sawdust, flattened turf, chips, and fresh soil can be consistent with the written scope. Unsafe holes, unfinished hauling, damaged utilities, blocked drains, broken hardscape, or undocumented retained-tree damage should not be dismissed as automatically normal.

The closeout process should begin before the crew leaves and continue the next day in good light.

## Inspect before final payment

When practical, perform a final walkthrough with the provider.

Check:

- exact tree and stems removed,
- hanging or suspended wood,
- logs and brush,
- stump condition,
- holes,
- trip hazards,
- access route,
- roof, fence, and pool cage,
- pavers and driveway,
- irrigation,
- septic or drainage,
- retained trees,
- street and sidewalk,
- gates,
- cleanup.

Take photographs before vehicles leave.

## Compare the result with the contract

| Scope item | Completion question |
|---|---|
| Tree removal | Are all contracted stems removed? |
| Debris | Is brush, wood, and sawdust disposition complete? |
| Logs | Were agreed log sizes removed, stacked, or left? |
| Stump | Was it cut low, left at a stated height, or ground? |
| Grinding | Was the agreed depth and area completed? |
| Chips | Were they removed, spread, or left in a pile? |
| Hole | Was it backfilled as agreed? |
| Final grade | Is the area safely shaped and walkable? |
| Access route | Are ruts and damage handled under the scope? |
| Public space | Is the road, curb, and sidewalk clear? |
| Restoration | Who handles sod, plants, pavers, irrigation, or fence repair? |

“Cleanup included” is too vague unless these items were defined.

## The stump may still be present

Tree removal and stump grinding are separate services unless the quote combines them.

Possible final conditions include:

- trunk cut near grade,
- tall stump left for a reason,
- stump ground below grade,
- surface roots ground,
- root plate removed,
- chips left,
- chips removed and soil added.

Use [the stump-hole guide](/blog/what-happens-to-the-hole-after-stump-grinding/) before assuming the area is ready for construction or planting.

## Chips are not clean soil

Stump grindings can contain:

- wood chips,
- bark,
- soil,
- small roots,
- old mulch,
- stones,
- hidden debris.

A chip-filled depression can settle.

For sod, paving, or a new tree, the finish may require:

- removing excess chips,
- adding clean soil,
- compacting appropriately for the use,
- restoring drainage,
- waiting for settlement,
- offsetting a replacement tree.

Use [the fresh-chip planting guide](/blog/can-fresh-wood-chips-from-stump-grinding-hurt-new-plants/).

## Inspect holes and grade

Look for:

- open stump cavities,
- root voids,
- soft fill,
- sharp stump edges,
- protruding roots,
- low spots,
- blocked drainage,
- soil against structures,
- trip hazards.

Keep children and pets away until the area is stable.

## Follow the equipment route

Walk the approved route and photograph:

- tire or track marks,
- ruts,
- paver movement,
- curb damage,
- fence or gate contact,
- bark scrapes,
- exposed roots,
- compacted soil,
- standing water.

Use [the heavy-equipment guide](/blog/can-heavy-equipment-over-tree-roots-damage-a-florida-tree-even-if-the-yard-looks-fine/) when retained-tree roots were crossed.

## Check irrigation and private facilities

Run irrigation only when safe and appropriate.

Look for:

- broken heads,
- low pressure,
- pooling water,
- damaged valves,
- cut tubing,
- gate-power issues,
- landscape-lighting damage,
- drainage change,
- septic warning signs.

Sunshine 811 does not locate every private facility. Responsibility should be defined in the contract.

## Inspect retained trees

Removal can affect nearby trees through:

- root damage,
- bark impact,
- branch breakage,
- changed wind exposure,
- changed sun exposure,
- compaction,
- rigging contact.

Photograph new damage and compare with the pre-work baseline.

## Check structures and surfaces

Inspect:

- roof,
- gutters,
- soffit,
- pool cage,
- windows,
- fence,
- wall,
- driveway,
- pavers,
- curb,
- mailbox,
- neighboring property.

Do not climb on a damaged roof. Use the responsible building professional when access is unsafe.

## Public-space cleanup

If the job used a road, shoulder, sidewalk, or curb, confirm:

- branches and logs removed,
- chips swept,
- drains clear,
- signs and devices removed when authorized,
- pedestrian path restored,
- permit conditions closed,
- neighbors can access property.

## Insects around fresh wood

Fresh chips, bark, and cut wood can attract or reveal insects.

This does not automatically prove that the house has a structural-pest infestation.

Document the insect and location. Use a pest professional when the concern involves the structure, and a plant or forest-health professional when the concern involves tree material.

## Sunlight and landscape change

Removal can suddenly change:

- shade,
- soil temperature,
- irrigation demand,
- turf exposure,
- privacy,
- wind,
- plant stress.

Do not automatically increase irrigation or fertilizer. Adjust after observing the site and plant needs.

## Create a written punch list

A punch list should identify:

- item,
- photograph,
- contract reference,
- responsible party,
- requested correction,
- deadline,
- access.

Send it promptly and keep communications in writing.

Do not authorize unrelated new work before the original closeout is understood.

## Provider versus landscaper responsibilities

The tree provider may be responsible for:

- wood and brush,
- stump work,
- route cleanup,
- agreed property protection,
- agreed final grade.

A landscaper or other contractor may handle:

- sod,
- replacement tree,
- irrigation redesign,
- paver rebuilding,
- drainage,
- soil amendment.

The contract should say where one scope ends and the next begins.

## Replanting timing

Before replanting, consider:

- remaining roots,
- chips,
- settlement,
- drainage,
- utilities,
- mature size,
- offset from the old stump,
- sunlight,
- species suitability.

Do not plant a replacement tree in unstable chip fill simply because the surface looks level.

## When immediate follow-up is needed

Contact the responsible provider or authority promptly for:

- unstable hanging wood,
- electrical damage,
- gas odor,
- major irrigation or water leak,
- unsafe hole,
- blocked public access,
- new retained-tree lean,
- structure damage,
- uncontrolled settlement.

Call 911 or the utility for immediate life-safety or electrical hazards.
