## Current-source checkpoints that shape Revision Package 4

### Tree placement has no universal house-distance rule

A useful tree-to-house decision should begin with the mature tree and the actual site rather than a universal number.

Species-specific Extension guidance commonly considers:

- mature height and canopy spread,
- available rooting space,
- distance from structures and power lines,
- maintenance access,
- soil and drainage,
- wind exposure,
- root and hardscape conflict.

The page should distinguish an existing mature tree from selecting a new planting location. A spacing recommendation for one species should never become a universal rule for every tree.

### Surface wood chips and incorporated wood are different

Coarse wood chips used on the soil surface can function as mulch.

Fine wood and chips mixed deeply into planting soil can temporarily reduce plant-available nitrogen near the decomposition zone. The practical concern increases with:

- fine particle size,
- high wood-to-soil ratio,
- direct planting into grindings,
- wet or poorly drained pockets,
- unstable chip fill,
- sod or hardscape installation.

The rewrite should avoid saying surface wood mulch broadly “steals nitrogen.”

### Stump drilling is a slow-decay tactic, not site restoration

Increasing exposed wood surface can influence decomposition, but drilling does not:

- remove the stump,
- remove roots,
- restore grade,
- remove a trip hazard,
- prepare a construction base,
- stop every resprouting species,
- produce a predictable timeline.

The current article moves too close to an actionable DIY recipe and gives household-product and burning ideas too much space. The rewrite should de-emphasize drilling instructions and move homeowners toward a goal-based wait, grind, excavate, or professionally managed treatment decision.

### Fire and household chemicals require a stronger stop boundary

Burning a stump is not an ordinary residential cleanup method.

The revision should not teach:

- drilling patterns,
- accelerant use,
- charcoal or fire setup,
- airflow preparation,
- burn duration,
- chemical recipes.

Local fire rules, drought restrictions, smoke, underground roots, utilities, structures, mulch, peat or organic soil, and long-duration smoldering can change the hazard.

Household salt, bleach, fuel, oil, drain cleaner, and improvised mixtures should be rejected.

Any pesticide or stump-control product must follow the exact current EPA-approved label and applicable licensing rules.

### Stump-grinding finish must be tied to the next land use

The correct finish differs for:

- rough mulch bed,
- ordinary lawn,
- replacement tree,
- flower or shrub bed,
- walkway or paver base,
- driveway,
- structure or pool project.

A chip-filled depression may be acceptable for a rough landscape area but is not automatically suitable as planting soil or engineered fill.

The page should separate:

- grinding depth,
- lateral grinding area,
- visible surface roots,
- chip removal,
- clean soil,
- compaction,
- drainage,
- settlement,
- final grade,
- construction preparation.

### Grinding value is a property-use decision

Stump grinding can be worth paying for when the stump blocks a defined use or completion standard.

A stronger value framework should consider:

- trip and mowing conflict,
- public or rental-property exposure,
- regrowth,
- access,
- irrigation and private facilities,
- septic,
- pavers and walls,
- replanting,
- construction,
- species,
- grindable percentage,
- grinding depth and area,
- chips and hauling,
- final grade.

Pest presence alone should not be used as a blanket sales argument. Dead wood can host insects and fungi without proving a structural infestation of the home.

### Black olive identification needs a taxonomy and cultivar reset

The South Florida black olive commonly referenced in landscape literature is currently treated as *Terminalia buceras* in current UF/IFAS material, while older publications and nursery usage may use *Bucida buceras*.

The rewrite should:

- acknowledge the naming variation,
- separate standard black olive from compact or cultivar trade names such as Shady Lady when identification supports it,
- distinguish black olive from edible olive,
- use leaves, branching, bark, flowers, fruit, mature form, and location together,
- avoid identifying from staining alone.

Current UF/IFAS material emphasizes staining fruit as a notable maintenance drawback and does not support presenting root problems as an automatic species trait. Actual hardscape conflict should be documented at the site.
