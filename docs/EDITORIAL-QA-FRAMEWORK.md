# Editorial QA Framework

## 1. Purpose

This framework defines how ProTreeTrim reviews and improves editorial quality across homeowner-facing tree care content.

The goal is not to rewrite existing articles in bulk. The goal is to create a repeatable review system that helps editors evaluate approximately 30 articles per week, identify the most valuable improvements, and keep every article aligned with ProTreeTrim's standards for clarity, trust, Florida relevance, and homeowner decision support.

Each review should help answer three questions:

- Does this article help a homeowner make a better decision?
- Does it sound natural, useful, and human?
- Does it support ProTreeTrim's role as a Florida tree service referral and dispatch network without overstating credentials or services?

## 2. Weekly Review Process

Use this process for each weekly editorial QA batch.

1. Select the weekly article set.
   - Pull approximately 30 articles from the priority order below.
   - Include a mix of high-traffic content, high-impression content, service-focused pages, and Florida-specific topics.

2. Review each article with the editorial checklist.
   - Read the article as a homeowner would.
   - Check the headline, intro, short answer, decision support, language, terminology, links, CTA, image opportunity, schema, and overall consistency.

3. Score each article with the 100-point scorecard.
   - Use the scorecard to create a consistent quality baseline.
   - Do not inflate scores because the article ranks well. Ranking and editorial quality are related, but they are not the same thing.

4. Record the review in the example review template.
   - Capture strengths, weaknesses, recommended edits, internal links to add, and final status.
   - Keep recommendations specific enough for a writer or editor to act on.

5. Assign the final status.
   - Publish: Article is ready as-is or only needs routine maintenance.
   - Minor polish: Article is generally strong but needs light edits.
   - Major revision: Article needs substantial editorial improvement before being treated as a high-quality asset.

6. Batch recommended work.
   - Group similar edits where possible, such as intro rewrites, CTA cleanup, missing internal links, Florida context additions, or AI-pattern reduction.
   - Keep article rewrites separate from the QA process so reviewers do not lose speed or consistency.

7. Track completion.
   - Record the review date, reviewer, score, status, and next action in the editorial tracker.
   - Re-review major revision articles after edits are complete.

## 3. Priority Order

Use this order when deciding which articles to review first.

### Highest Traffic

Prioritize articles with the most organic sessions, engaged sessions, or referral value. These articles shape the most homeowner experiences and should meet the highest quality standard.

### Highest Impressions

Review articles with high search impressions even if clicks are lower. These pages may need sharper headlines, stronger introductions, better search intent alignment, or clearer answers.

### Service Intent

Prioritize articles tied directly to tree trimming, tree removal, emergency tree service, stump grinding, palm trimming, storm cleanup, tree risk, permits, or local service decisions.

### Florida Relevance

Prioritize content where Florida context materially improves usefulness. Look for opportunities to address hurricanes, wind risk, palms, live oaks, mangroves, local permitting, utility clearance, heat, humidity, pests, and regional homeowner concerns.

### Emergency Topics

Review emergency tree service and storm-related topics carefully. These articles must be clear, calm, practical, and careful about safety. They should help homeowners decide what to do next without creating false urgency or unsafe advice.

### High Commercial Intent

Prioritize articles where the reader is likely comparing providers, costs, timing, risks, or next steps. These pieces should include strong decision support, clear CTAs, internal links, and homeowner-first guidance.

## 4. Review Philosophy

### Natural US English

Articles should sound like they were written by a careful American editor for a homeowner audience. Prefer plain, direct sentences. Avoid awkward phrasing, stiff transitions, inflated claims, and unnatural keyword repetition.

### Homeowner-First

Every article should serve the reader before it serves search engines. The content should explain risks, tradeoffs, timing, likely next steps, and when to call a professional.

### Decision Support

Strong ProTreeTrim content helps homeowners make decisions. It should answer what matters, why it matters, what to watch for, and what action makes sense. Useful decision support may include examples, comparisons, warning signs, cost factors, permit considerations, safety notes, and practical next steps.

### Never Keyword Stuffing

Keywords should appear only where they fit naturally. Do not force exact-match phrases into headings, paragraphs, CTAs, image alt text, or internal links. Repetition that makes the article feel mechanical should be removed.

### Never AI Sounding

Avoid generic filler, repetitive sentence patterns, vague summaries, unnatural transitions, and phrases that sound assembled rather than written. Strong articles use specific context, varied rhythm, and useful judgment.

## Operating Standard

The reviewer is not rewriting the article during QA. The reviewer is evaluating quality, identifying gaps, and creating a clear path for improvement. The framework works best when reviewers stay consistent, specific, and homeowner-focused.
