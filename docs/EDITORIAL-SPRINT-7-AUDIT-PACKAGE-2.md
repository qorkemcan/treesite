## Main findings

### These pages explain dangerous work and must stay homeowner-facing

The notch-cut, back-cut, rigging, and loaded-storm-limb pages are useful only when they explain:

- why the operation needs planning,
- what the homeowner should ask,
- when a method is inappropriate,
- where the exclusion zone belongs,
- when utilities or emergency services come first,
- what belongs in the written scope.

They must not become step-by-step cutting, rigging, or felling tutorials.

### Water sprouts need a recovery-pruning role

The water-sprout article correctly warns against removing every shoot, but it needs a better distinction among:

- epicormic shoots,
- water sprouts,
- basal shoots,
- root suckers,
- stump sprouts,
- normal juvenile growth,
- graft-rootstock shoots.

The rewrite should connect sprouts to topping, storm loss, root stress, sun exposure, cultivar or species response, retained leaf area, attachment quality, and multi-year restoration pruning.

### Old support systems need inspection and maintenance boundaries

The cable-and-brace page needs to separate:

- static and dynamic cabling,
- brace rods,
- supplemental support from defect correction,
- hardware inspection from whole-tree assessment,
- system maintenance from removal,
- installation records from undocumented hardware,
- tree-growth change from hardware failure.

A support system does not restore decayed wood or make a tree safe indefinitely. Removing, tensioning, replacing, or adding hardware is not a homeowner task.

### Rigging content needs force and anchor limits without procedural detail

The rope-rigging article should explain that controlled lowering can protect structures and manage movement, but:

- ropes do not eliminate force,
- rigging can increase load on anchors and the tree,
- decayed or cracked wood may be unsuitable for anchoring,
- landing zones and drop zones remain controlled work areas,
- rigging equipment, ratings, inspection, and operator competence matter,
- cranes, lifts, climbing, and ground-based methods solve different problems.

The page should avoid teaching knots, cut sequence, rope angles, or load calculations.

### Storm tension content needs a stronger emergency hierarchy

The storm-limb article is the strongest in the group, but it needs an explicit first-response sequence:

1. injury, fire, active collapse, or blocked emergency access,
2. electrical contact or possible energized equipment,
3. exclusion zone,
4. insurance and property documentation from a safe location,
5. qualified tree-service response.

The rewrite should distinguish tension, compression, torsion, suspended wood, spring poles, root-plate movement, and structure-supported limbs in descriptive—not instructional—language.

### Notch and back-cut pages overlap heavily

The two felling-term pages repeat the same ideas about lean, hinge, drop zone, decay, ropes, cranes, and DIY danger.

Their canonical roles should be separated:

- **Notch-cut page:** explains the directional opening, its limits, and why residential trees may not be felled whole.
- **Back-cut page:** explains release timing, retained control wood, premature movement, barber-chair risk, and why the homeowner should focus on the full felling or dismantling plan.

Neither page should specify angles, depths, heights, sequence, saw positioning, or an escape-path formula.

### Traffic control needs authority and responsibility distinctions

The traffic-control article needs clearer separation among:

- private driveway control,
- sidewalk and pedestrian control,
- HOA or gated-community access,
- local street and curb use,
- county road,
- state-maintained road,
- public right-of-way,
- school, transit, emergency, and delivery access.

Cones and a spotter may be enough on private property, but they are not automatically a valid traffic-control plan for a public road. The responsible road authority, permit, temporary traffic-control requirements, and qualified personnel may apply.

### Mini-loader cleanup needs retained-tree and surface-protection planning

The mini-loader article overlaps with the revised heavy-equipment page but should retain a narrower commercial role:

- cleanup productivity,
- gate and route feasibility,
- machine dimensions and attachments,
- ground pressure and turns,
- retained-tree protection,
- pavers and finished surfaces,
- irrigation, septic, drainage, and private utilities,
- log and brush staging,
- final yard condition,
- change orders.

It should link to, rather than duplicate, the broader heavy-equipment and critical-root-zone hubs.
