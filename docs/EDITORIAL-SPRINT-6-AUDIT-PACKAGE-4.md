## Current-source findings that change the revision plan

- Brazilian pepper, carrotwood, Chinese tallow, melaleuca, and the three common Australian-pine species are currently shown by UF/IFAS Assessment or FDACS as prohibited or noxious in Florida. The articles need exact species and regulatory language rather than a broad “invasive” label.
- Camphor tree is currently **Invasive** in North and Central Florida, while South Florida is listed as **Caution** with reassessment in process. The regional distinction must remain explicit.
- Earleaf acacia is currently **Invasive (No Uses)** in Central and South Florida and **Not a problem species (un-documented)** in North Florida. The article must not generalize the southern status statewide.
- “Prohibited,” “noxious weed,” “invasive,” “caution,” and “not recommended” are different terms. None of them, by itself, proves that a homeowner may remove a mature tree without checking parcel control, local government, HOA, easement, wetland, coastal, right-of-way, utility, and wildlife requirements.
- Invasive-tree removal is not complete when the trunk is cut. Identification, contact exposure, seed and propagule containment, stump or root-crown response, labeled herbicide use, equipment access, disposal, monitoring, and replacement all belong in the written scope.

## Shared editorial weaknesses

### Identification is too dependent on one memorable clue

The articles often rely on red berries, camphor odor, popcorn-like seeds, papery bark, needle-like branchlets, or curved phyllodes. Each rewrite needs a multiple-feature identification workflow and named lookalikes. One photograph, smell, fruit, or phone-app result should be treated as a probable identification, not proof.

### Regulatory status is simplified

Several pages say the species is “invasive” but do not distinguish current UF/IFAS regional assessment from FDACS noxious-weed or prohibited-plant status. The revised pages need the exact current status, date-sensitive verification, and a clear statement that plant-status law and tree-removal authority are separate questions.

### Mechanical work, pesticide work, and restoration are blended

Cutting, stump grinding, cut-stump treatment, basal-bark treatment, seedling removal, debris hauling, monitoring, and replanting are different scopes. A tree crew is not automatically a licensed pesticide applicator, wetland contractor, plant identifier, permitting authority, or restoration professional.

### Pesticide and sensitive-site boundaries are too light

Every article mentions herbicide cautiously, but none provides a complete label-first framework. The rewrite must cover target species, application method, timing, treated tissue, product label, water and wetland restrictions, desirable vegetation, children, pets, PPE, commercial applicator licensing, and follow-up. Household chemicals and unlabeled mixtures must be rejected.

### Seed, fruit, capsules, and viable debris need species-specific handling

Fruit-bearing or capsule-bearing material should not be dragged through clean beds, dumped into natural areas, or moved in a way that spreads propagules. Disposal instructions must be tied to current local rules and the species’ viable material. Grinding wood is not the same as controlling seed, seedlings, roots, or stump sprouts.

### Structural risk must remain tree-specific

“Invasive” does not mean “immediate failure risk.” Wind, lean, deadwood, included bark, decay, root-plate movement, targets, saturated soil, and storm change determine urgency. Electrical contact and active failure need the 911 and utility hierarchy before private tree work.
