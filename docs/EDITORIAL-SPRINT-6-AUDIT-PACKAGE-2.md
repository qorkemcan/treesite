## Main findings

### Vines and pruning

- Vine guidance needs species identification before cutting or chemical treatment. Native, ornamental, invasive, woody, poison-ivy, and canopy-smothering vines do not share one response.
- Summer pruning should be organized by objective, tree type, live-crown impact, wildlife, site condition, and weather—not a universal calendar answer.

### Property authority

- The private-property removal page needs a precise address-first workflow and current statutory language.
- Florida Statute 163.045 uses a narrow definition of covered residential property and specifies an onsite assessment and signed documentation for the unacceptable-risk pathway.
- Ownership, local authority, HOA, easement, right-of-way, development-plan, mangrove, wetland, and utility layers must remain distinct.

### Living-tree roots

- The surface-root article has useful decision content but treats one-inch diameter too much like a universal stop line.
- Root size, distance, tree size/species, prior root loss, lean, targets, soil, hardscape objective, and redesign alternatives need to be evaluated together.

### Stump and post-removal decisions

- The stump cluster is comprehensive but repetitive and weakly sourced.
- Every page needs an end-use-first scope: lawn, bed, replanting, pavers, construction, natural decay, regrowth control, or visible-obstruction removal.
- Chemical pages must distinguish registered herbicides, decay products, and unsafe household shortcuts.
- Root persistence should be split into living resprouting tissue, dead woody roots, soil voids, hardscape effects, and site restoration rather than one universal timeline.
