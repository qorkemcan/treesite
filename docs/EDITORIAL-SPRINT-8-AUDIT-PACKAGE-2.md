## Current-source checkpoints

### West exposure needs right-plant, right-place selection—not a generic sun-tolerance list

UF/IFAS landscape-siting guidance treats the south and west sides of buildings as high solar-exposure locations and emphasizes mature size, available soil volume, hardscape, utilities, drainage, salt, and regional climate.

A useful revision should:

- separate North, Central, and South Florida,
- define the purpose of the planting,
- distinguish open lawn from reflected-wall and pavement heat,
- include a limited species matrix with mature size and site caveats,
- address establishment irrigation separately from mature drought tolerance,
- screen invasive or prohibited species,
- protect utility and structure clearance.

The current article promises “best trees” but names no trees.

### Pet-friendly selection must begin with positive identification and veterinary toxicity review

ASPCA’s plant database warns that its toxicity list is not exhaustive and that even material from plants listed as non-toxic may cause gastrointestinal upset if eaten.

The revision should not reduce pet safety to litter and traffic patterns. It needs to separate:

- toxic plant parts,
- fruit and seed ingestion,
- thorns or sharp material,
- pesticide and fertilizer exposure,
- choking and obstruction hazards,
- digging and root-zone damage,
- shade and heat safety,
- emergency veterinary response.

Common names are insufficient. Scientific-name verification is essential because unrelated plants can share similar common names.

### A shrinking palm crown is a symptom cluster, not one diagnosis

UF/IFAS palm morphology guidance identifies the apical meristem, or bud, as the single growing point of a palm stem. Damage to that growing point can be fatal to a single-stemmed palm.

The revision should distinguish:

- deliberate overpruning,
- naturally retained versus self-cleaning fronds,
- potassium, magnesium, manganese, and boron symptom patterns,
- small new leaves versus loss of older leaves,
- spear and bud abnormalities,
- trunk narrowing that records earlier stress,
- planting depth,
- root-zone saturation or drought,
- cold or storm recovery,
- lethal and nonlethal disease possibilities.

“Underfed” is not a diagnosis, and fertilizer should not be recommended without identifying the likely disorder and site limitation.

### Oak pruning cannot repair root, trunk, or attachment failure

The existing oak article correctly separates canopy work from root and trunk defects, but it still needs a more disciplined tree-risk framework.

The revision should remove homeowner “hollow-sound” testing and separate:

- defect,
- likelihood of failure,
- likelihood of impact,
- consequence,
- time frame,
- mitigation options.

Species can inform the assessment, but live oak, laurel oak, and water oak should not be ranked from common reputation alone. Proximity to a roof is a target condition, not proof of unacceptable risk.

Any Florida Statute 163.045 discussion must use the current onsite-assessment, signed-document, qualified-professional, defined-property, and unacceptable-risk language.

### No universal palm-inspection interval was verified

The current palm-inspection article contains literal internal citation artifacts and publishes an annual baseline plus a two-to-three-year interval for some older palms.

The UF/IFAS materials reviewed support:

- prompt review after storm change,
- monitoring the bud and new growth,
- allowing some storm-damaged palms up to six months to produce new leaves before final recovery conclusions,
- species- and symptom-specific disease monitoring.

Those points do not establish one universal professional-inspection interval for every Florida palm.

The revision should separate:

- routine homeowner observation,
- documented professional condition review,
- post-storm recheck,
- disease-exposure monitoring,
- newly planted palm establishment review,
- immediate problem-driven assessment.

The public citation artifacts are a publication blocker and must be removed completely.

### Orange paint on a tree is not the same as orange ground-locate paint

Leon County provides a valid local example: trees marked for removal in its canopy-road program are numbered and receive an orange dot and orange flag.

Sunshine 811’s APWA color system uses orange for underground communications, alarm, and signal facilities. That standard applies to excavation markings; it does not create a statewide rule that an orange dot on tree bark is a utility designation.

The revision should identify:

- the person or entity that placed the mark,
- the project and jurisdiction,
- tree location and ownership,
- number, flag, tag, nearby ground paint, or survey stake,
- whether the marking is inventory, pruning, preservation, review, or removal,
- the correct city, county, utility, surveyor, contractor, or property-manager contact.

A paint mark is not a permit and does not grant the homeowner authority to remove the tree.

### Palm water symptoms overlap with nutrition, disease, planting depth, and root injury

UF/IFAS palm-disorder and transplanting guidance shows that excessive water, poor drainage, planting depth, root-zone conditions, nutrient disorders, disease, and environmental injury can produce overlapping symptoms.

The revision should not imply that wet-looking or dry-looking soil alone resolves the diagnosis. It should include:

- newly planted versus established palms,
- root ball versus surrounding soil,
- rainfall and irrigation records,
- emitter coverage and uniformity,
- drainage and perched-water conditions,
- planting depth,
- soil moisture at more than the surface,
- spear and newest-leaf condition,
- nutrient and disease mimics,
- a stop point before fertilizer, fungicide, or irrigation escalation.

### Stump difficulty is a written-scope issue as much as a machine issue

The current stump article has useful internal links and recognizes access, root flare, hidden material, utilities, hardscape, wet soil, and final land use.

The revision should become a stronger estimate and completion page by defining:

- measured stump diameter and flare area,
- target grinding depth,
- lateral grinding limits,
- included surface roots,
- accessible percentage,
- machine and gate limits,
- hidden metal, rock, and concrete assumptions,
- public utility locate and private-facility responsibility,
- chip volume and hauling,
- clean soil and final grade,
- settlement,
- second-visit triggers,
- construction exclusions.

Grinding does not remove every root or create engineered fill.
