## Main findings

### The removal hub needs a unique canonical job

The broad “does this tree need removal?” page overlaps with already revised pages about trees likely to fall and removal versus pruning. Its rewrite must become a structured intake hub:

- immediate emergency and utility screen,
- observable defect inventory,
- target and occupancy,
- tree health versus structural condition,
- recent change,
- preservation alternatives,
- qualified assessment,
- authorization,
- written physical-work scope.

It should not remotely determine that a tree is safe, unsafe, or legally removable.

### Root pages need a consistent architecture

The critical-root-zone, girdling-root, equipment-damage, and root-killing pages currently use related language without a shared distinction among:

- root flare and root collar,
- absorbing roots,
- woody transport roots,
- structural or buttress roots,
- stem-girdling roots,
- circling container roots,
- critical root zone,
- tree-protection zone,
- project-specific structural root zone,
- roots of a standing tree,
- roots remaining after removal.

Revision should reduce overlap and link each page into one root-management cluster.

### Cavity advice must reject cosmetic “repair” without overdiagnosing decay

The cavity page correctly rejects concrete, but it needs stronger boundaries:

- no drilling, scraping, burning, probing, tapping, or filling as a structural test,
- cavity opening versus internal decay,
- wildlife occupancy,
- old woundwood versus active cracking,
- location at base, trunk, union, or limb,
- remaining shell and load path cannot be judged from one photo,
- inspection tools and tree-risk assessment are professional decisions,
- hidden concrete, metal, foam, or debris must be disclosed before cutting.

### Root-killing content has the highest chemical and destabilization exposure

The page must clearly separate:

1. a standing tree whose roots support the tree,
2. a removed tree with a living stump or resprouting species,
3. dead woody roots that remain physically present,
4. invasive-plant control,
5. hardscape or utility conflicts.

No recommendation should be made before species, site, label, water, desired vegetation, children, pets, utilities, and applicator role are known.

### Deadwooding and severe pruning need accepted-scope language

The two pruning pages need to distinguish:

- deadwood removal,
- broken or hanging branches,
- crown cleaning,
- reduction,
- restoration pruning,
- topping,
- lion-tailing,
- pollarding when historically established,
- palm pruning,
- fruit-tree pruning,
- removal preparation.

They also need wildlife, electrical, work-zone, branch-size, rigging, and final-cleanup boundaries.
