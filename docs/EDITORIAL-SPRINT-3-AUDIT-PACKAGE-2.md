## Main findings

### Intent overlap

- The same mature-size, roots, pavers, pool cage, power line, septic, storm, pruning, stump, and removal warnings repeat across most pages.
- The general shade, fast-growing shade, wind-resistant, coastal, near-house, and poolside pages need distinct decision roles instead of parallel species lists.
- The post-stump article should focus on site readiness, not function as another complete Florida tree-selection guide.

### Accuracy and trust

- No tree can be promised to cause no root problems.
- Growth speed does not by itself establish weak wood or poor storm performance.
- Wind-resistant does not mean hurricane-proof, and species performance depends on site, structure, roots, planting, and maintenance.
- A single generic setback should not replace mature-size and site-specific planning.
- Coastal suitability needs direct-spray, sheltered coastal, canal, inland, salinity, drainage, and hardiness distinctions.

### Publishing residue

- Six June planting pages expose `Internal Links to Add` notes and raw source URLs.
- `best-shade-trees-for-florida-backyards.md` contains unreplaced `turn...` citation markers.
- The backyard shade and poolside pages begin without H1 headings.
- The poolside page has no sources, internal links, CTA, or service path.

### Commercial boundaries

- These are planting and landscape-decision pages, not removal landing pages.
- Extension, nursery, landscape-design, utility, HOA, and local-rule research should be primary.
- Tree-service links should appear only for stump preparation, existing root/hardscape conflicts, unsafe clearance, removal, or cleanup.

## Recommended revision order

1. Best Shade Trees for Florida Backyards — citation artifacts and missing publishing structure.
2. Best Trees for Poolside Landscaping — no sources, H1, links, or CTA.
3. Best Small Trees Near a Florida House — absolute title and generic setback claim.

5. Trees to Avoid Near Hardscape — replace blacklist framing with fit/conflict matrix.
6. Fast-Growing Shade Trees — distinguish speed from structural quality.
7. Coastal Florida Trees — add exposure-based selection matrix.
8. Wind-Resistant Trees — strongest article; compress while preserving evidence.
