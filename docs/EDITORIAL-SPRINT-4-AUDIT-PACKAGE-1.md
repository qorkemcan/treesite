## Main findings

### Preservation versus removal

- All eight articles understand that a living tree may still be mechanically unreliable, but several turn uncertainty into a removal-oriented conclusion without defining the evidence or qualified assessment required.
- Cabling, bracing, pruning, monitoring, site correction, emergency stabilization, and removal need distinct roles.
- Dispatch should not be presented as diagnosing decay, quantifying remaining strength, prescribing support systems, or determining root recovery.

### Emergency boundaries

- Split and uprooted-tree pages need immediate exclusion, power-line, structure, loaded-wood, and active-movement instructions before preservation discussion.
- Emergency stabilization must be separated from complete removal, hauling, stump work, and later restoration.

### Root-zone evidence

- Root-damage and compaction pages need project records, affected sides, distance, root size, grade, drainage, traffic, fill, pavers, utilities, and timing.
- Timing after construction may be evidence but is not proof of causation by itself.
- Overmulching must separate loose mulch correction from buried soil, girdling roots, decay, and root-collar excavation.

### Pruning and support standards

- Overpruning needs distinct topping, lion-tailing, crown reduction, thinning, raising, palm, and corrective-pruning paths.
- Cabling and bracing need installation, pruning, inspection, maintenance, and replacement expectations.

### Publishing quality

- Every page lacks an H1, sources, contextual links, current update date, and the established decision table.
- Repeated rhetorical contrasts and conclusion sections create a recognizable AI-style pattern.
