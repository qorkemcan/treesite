## Current-source checkpoints

### Brown palm fronds should be pruned by function and leaf condition, not appearance

UF/IFAS palm-pruning guidance supports avoiding overpruning and retaining healthy green fronds. The existing article is directionally useful, but it needs a cleaner H1, authoritative sources, direct service routes, and a sharper distinction among:

- fully dead fronds,
- yellowing or partly green fronds,
- fruit stalks,
- self-cleaning palms,
- hurricane cuts,
- nutrient deficiency,
- newest-spear and bud problems,
- tall-palm access and utility hazards.

The revision should avoid a simple “cut brown, leave green” model and should explain why the newest growth matters more than older cosmetic fronds.

### Gate access must become a measurable access-scope page

The stump-grinder gate article has solid practical detail, but it lacks an H1, source section, service routes, measured access table, private-facility boundary, and final scope rules.

The revision should define:

- clear opening,
- narrowest route,
- turn radius,
- thresholds,
- slope,
- working clearance around the stump,
- machine class,
- paver and turf protection,
- public utility locate versus private irrigation and lighting,
- fence-panel responsibility,
- chip and final-grade expectations,
- what happens if access is not safe.

### Old topping risk should avoid homeowner testing and become a defect-time-frame page

The old topped tree article correctly warns that topping can create weak regrowth and decay risk, but it contains homeowner “hollow sound” testing and lacks sources, H1, internal links, service routes, and a formal risk framework.

The revision should separate:

- old heading or topping cuts,
- decay behind old cuts,
- epicormic or stress regrowth,
- weak attachments,
- end weight,
- target exposure,
- storm timing,
- corrective pruning,
- cabling or bracing limitations,
- removal decision,
- emergency utility boundaries.

Do not provide cutting instructions.

### Brown palm leaves need a pattern-based plant-health page

The palm-browning article is useful but still broad and unsourced. The revision should build a decision matrix by affected leaf age and location:

- oldest lower fronds,
- brown tips on otherwise green leaves,
- newest leaves,
- spear,
- one-sided browning,
- rapid crown collapse,
- multiple palms.

It should also separate nutrient deficiency, overpruning, irrigation, salt, cold, disease, root disturbance, and bud problems. It should avoid “nutrient deficiency is common” as a stand-alone diagnosis.

### Stumps left in the ground need risk, scope, and chemical/fire boundaries

The stump article is helpful but template-heavy and lacks sources, H1, internal routes, direct service routes, and a written decision matrix. The revision should distinguish:

- natural area versus active yard,
- trip and mowing hazards,
- sprouts and root suckers,
- insects as habitat versus structural infestation risk,
- mushrooms and decay,
- settlement,
- replanting,
- hardscape conflict,
- chemical label limits,
- burning and local rules,
- stump grinding scope and final condition.

### Mulch mushrooms should be separated from tree-decay fruiting bodies

The mulch-mushroom article has the right basic message: mushrooms in mulch are not automatically a tree danger. It needs stronger diagnostic boundaries:

- mushrooms in loose mulch,
- mushrooms from buried wood,
- mushrooms on roots or trunk flare,
- shelf fungi or conks,
- old stump debris,
- overmulching and wet beds,
- pet and child exposure,
- tree decline symptoms,
- risk assessment triggers.

It should not overstate harmlessness when fungus is attached to woody structural tissue.

### Heat reduction needs a science-backed placement and mature-fit framework

The heat-reduction article is conceptually useful but lacks sources, H1, internal links, service routes, species or placement specificity, and measurable decision criteria.

Revision should include:

- shade over west and south exposures,
- hardscape surface heat,
- evapotranspiration and canopy effect in plain language,
- mature height and spread,
- utility and structure clearance,
- species and regional fit,
- cooling goals by patio, driveway, wall, and walk,
- storm and maintenance tradeoffs,
- tree preservation versus planting.

This should connect to the newly revised west-facing-yard selection article.

### Girdling roots need root-flare exposure boundaries and correction limits

The girdling-roots article has good base-level education but needs authoritative sources, H1, internal routes, service routes, and more precise action boundaries.

Revision should address:

- visible root flare,
- buried flare,
- circling roots,
- crossing roots,
- stem-girdling roots,
- container defects,
- deep planting,
- mulch volcanoes,
- root-collar excavation,
- when correction may be possible,
- when root cutting is unsafe,
- structural and health implications,
- documentation before pruning or removal.

The revision should avoid suggesting homeowners cut roots without a qualified diagnosis.
