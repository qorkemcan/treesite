## Main findings

### Palm symptom content

- Scale content has strong pest boundaries but is too long and exposes editorial notes and raw sources.
- Black-spot content is readable but lacks an H1, sources, links, CTA, and a defensible symptom-comparison matrix.
- Palm pages must distinguish cosmetic frond symptoms from spear, crown, trunk, basal, structural, and rapid-progression warning signs.
- Pest identification and treatment should remain with qualified plant-health or licensed pest professionals; tree services should handle access, dead fronds, structural decline, removal, or stump work.

### Mulch and transplant content

- White growth on mulch is usually a low-anxiety decomposition query; the page should avoid turning it into removal intent unless the tree itself has structural warning signs.
- Transplant shock needs a check-correct-monitor-escalate workflow and clearer boundaries between installer warranty, irrigation, planting defects, plant-health diagnosis, and tree removal.
- Neither page currently has a usable source section or contextual internal-link network.

### Planting hubs

- The foundation page correctly rejects the idea that roots deliberately attack structures, but still overstates root causation and lacks drainage, soil, construction, utilities, septic, and foundation context.
- The right-tree/right-place article should become the master planting hub and route readers into the focused coastal, wind, pool, shade, hardscape, near-house, stump, and utility guides.
- Universal distance rules should be replaced with mature-zone planning, scaled site inventory, rootable soil, and future-use review.

## Recommended final revision order

1. Black Spot on Palm Fronds — missing H1, sources, links, CTA, and diagnostic matrix.
2. Planting Near Your House — foundation-risk overstatement and missing site variables.
