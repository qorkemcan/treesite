## Current-source checkpoints that change the revision plan

- UF/IFAS identifies ball moss as a harmless epiphyte that attaches to a support but does not feed on the host tree. It may be present on declining trees, but it should not be used as a stand-alone diagnosis of decline.
- Florida mistletoe is a hemiparasitic flowering plant: it photosynthesizes while taking water and mineral resources from host tissue. Host stress and infestation intensity matter, but mistletoe presence alone does not establish structural risk.
- Oozing material can be resin, gum, latex, sap, honeydew, wetwood or slime-flux exudate, fermentation residue, or fluid associated with wounds, insects, disease, or decay. Color and smell alone are not a structural diagnosis.
- Patterned trunk holes may be caused by sapsuckers, bark or ambrosia beetles, other borers, mechanical injury, old hardware, or dead branch stubs. Fresh boring dust, frass tubes, host species, canopy change, and hole geometry matter.
- The current Florida residential-tree statute requires qualifying onsite documentation from an ISA Certified Arborist or Florida licensed landscape architect and defines an unacceptable-risk threshold. It applies to a specific single-family residential-property definition and expressly does not displace delegated mangrove authority.
- Florida DEP treats red, black, and white mangroves as regulated specimens, including dead mangroves and rooted seedlings. Homeowner exemptions, professional mangrove trimmer requirements, permits, and removal rules are activity- and site-specific.
- Active bird nests containing eggs, chicks, or dependent young require federal wildlife caution. There is no single statewide “nesting season” that makes every tree unavailable for the same fixed months.
- There is no authoritative statewide cheapest month for tree removal. Local workload, storm events, tree condition, access, equipment, utilities, permits, cleanup, and contractor capacity determine the quote.
- The day-after and cleanup pages need contract-completion standards, not a rule that visible disturbance is automatically normal or automatically defective.

## Main findings

### Ball moss must be de-escalated without hiding real tree defects

The current page correctly says ball moss is not a parasite, but it sometimes treats visible abundance as a stronger decline clue than the evidence supports.

The rewrite should:

- identify ball moss as a bromeliad and harmless epiphyte,
- distinguish it from Spanish moss, lichens, mistletoe, and nests,
- avoid claiming that heavy ball moss diagnoses canopy decline,
- separate cosmetic removal from deadwood or structural pruning,
- reject aggressive canopy stripping,
- add exact pesticide-label and applicator limits if treatment is discussed,
- route whole-tree defects independently from the epiphyte.

### Mistletoe needs plant biology, host condition, and pruning consequences separated

The current page calls mistletoe parasitic but does not explain that it is a green hemiparasite connected to host tissue through haustoria.

The rewrite should separate:

- positive identification,
- infestation distribution,
- host vigor,
- affected branch importance,
- pruning impact,
- regrowth from embedded tissue,
- wildlife value,
- toxic ingestion,
- active nests,
- tree-risk assessment.

Mistletoe should not become a shortcut to tree removal, and a green clump should not be called merely cosmetic.

### Oozing-fluid content is too diagnostically broad

The current article places pruning wounds, wetwood, borers, decay, palms, fungi, and structural risk into one “sap” bucket.

The rewrite needs a symptom-classification workflow:

- clear sap,
- conifer resin,
- stone-fruit gum,
- latex,
- sticky honeydew from insects above,
- dark wetwood or slime-flux exudate,
- foamy fermentation,
- wound fluid,
- frass mixed with fluid,
- palm trunk exudate.

The article should prevent touching, tasting, smelling closely, drilling, scraping, sealing, or spraying before species and cause are known.

### Patterned holes require a visual-identification matrix

The existing article correctly warns against random insecticide, but it underdevelops the strongest homeowner clue: pattern.

The rewrite should distinguish:

- neat horizontal or rectangular sapsucker rows,
- isolated old exit holes,
- fresh round entry holes,
- D-shaped or oval holes where species-relevant,
- toothpick-like ambrosia-beetle frass,
- powder or boring dust,
- sap or resin flow,
- old nails, straps, or mechanical injury,
- branch-stub scars.

The “hollow sound” homeowner test should be removed. Diagnosis and pesticide decisions must be host-, pest-, timing-, and label-specific.

### The protected-tree page needs exact legal gates

This is the package’s highest legal-risk article.

The rewrite must accurately separate:

1. private ownership and boundary,
2. local tree ordinance,
3. Florida Statute 163.045,
4. qualifying onsite documentation,
5. the statutory unacceptable-risk threshold,
6. single-family residential-property definition,
7. mangrove authority,
8. wetlands, coastal areas, conservation easements, and development conditions,
9. right-of-way and street trees,
10. HOA or private covenants,
11. utilities,
12. wildlife,
13. emergency documentation.

It must not imply that a species name alone makes removal legal or illegal statewide.

### Seasonal pricing needs a quote strategy, not a calendar promise

The current page is useful but repetitive.

The rewrite should make these distinctions:

- price versus scheduling availability,
- planned versus emergency scope,
- local storm event versus official hurricane-season dates,
- dry access versus a fixed “dry season,”
- active nest inspection versus a universal nesting calendar,
- normal-hours work versus after-hours or holiday response,
- permit and utility lead time,
- apples-to-apples quote comparison,
- tree deterioration while waiting,
- contractor insurance and equipment availability.

“No guaranteed cheapest month” should remain the central answer.

### Day-after inspection should begin before the crew leaves

The current page frames the next morning as the first inspection. A stronger workflow begins with a final walkthrough before payment and continues the next day in better light.

The rewrite should define:

- wood and brush disposition,
- stump height or grinding depth,
- chips and backfill,
- final grade,
- holes and trip hazards,
- access route,
- pavers and structures,
- irrigation and private facilities,
- street and sidewalk cleanup,
- retained-tree damage,
- photographs,
- written punch list,
- provider versus landscaper responsibilities,
- settlement and replanting timing.

It should avoid suggesting that visible insects around old wood automatically indicate a structural-pest problem at the house.

### Cleanup duration needs a phase-and-scope model

The current article repeats that cleanup takes time but does not define the work phases tightly enough.

The rewrite should separate:

- cutting and controlled lowering,
- brush staging,
- chipping,
- log processing,
- loader or hand transport,
- hauling,
- municipal or storm-debris rules,
- stump grinding,
- chip handling,
- road and sidewalk cleanup,
- lawn or hardscape restoration,
- final inspection.

“Cleanup included” must be replaced by measurable completion terms.
