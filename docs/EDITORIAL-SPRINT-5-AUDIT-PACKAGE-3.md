## Main findings

### Surveys, assessments, and local preservation

- The survey/assessment article has the correct central distinction but needs a formal document matrix and professional-role boundaries.
- The city-preservation article repeats that rules vary without showing homeowners how to identify jurisdiction, current code, protected status, permit path, mitigation, and written determinations.
- The unauthorized-removal article contains visible public citation artifacts and precise fine examples without a stable, dated source framework.

### Seasonal cutting and wildlife

- The month article should not position June as a broadly problematic month without species- and work-specific evidence.
- Planned pruning, palm care, active nesting, disease-sensitive species, named-storm conditions, saturated soil, and emergency work require separate pathways.

### DIY and emergency safety

- The DIY article has strong homeowner appeal but needs a factual stop-work matrix rather than repeated danger language.
- The emergency-before-help page should become a concise first-ten-minutes protocol with 911, utility, evacuation, exclusion-zone, insurer, and no-manipulation steps.
- The kids-and-pets page has the package’s strongest visual framework but needs electrical, floodwater, structural, heat, wildlife, chemical, medical, and veterinary boundaries.

### Power lines

- The power-line article carries the highest immediate life-safety risk.
- It must explicitly instruct users to assume lines are energized, avoid touching trees or ground near downed conductors, call the utility first, and use appropriately qualified line-clearance professionals.
- ProTreeTrim must not route ordinary tree crews into an energized-line hazard.

### Publishing quality

- All eight require H1 headings, current dates, authoritative sources, decision tables, contextual links, direct service or authority routes, and clearer professional boundaries.
- Formulaic structures remain visible across the package.
