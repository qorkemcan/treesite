## Main findings

### Insurance and claim evidence

- The general insurance article is the weakest decision page in the package because it describes common coverage patterns without authoritative sources, a claim workflow, or policy-specific limits.
- The two evidence articles are useful but overlap heavily. One should own the complete emergency financial record; the other should own the technical photo protocol.
- Descriptive tree invoices and photographs can support a claim file but must not be presented as proof of coverage, causation, negligence, or prior tree health.

### Liability and appraisal

- The branch-on-car page should become a safety, insurer, evidence, and attorney workflow rather than a notice-and-foreseeability explanation.
- The appraisal page correctly recognizes multiple valuation contexts but needs assignment purpose, qualifications, effective date, method, evidence, and legal/tax/insurance boundaries.

### Property transactions

- Buyer and seller articles have strong search and commercial value but need transaction-stage organization, written scope definitions, and distinctions among home inspection, tree inventory, tree-risk assessment, survey, permit research, disclosure, and contractor estimating.
- Tree work should reduce real defects or provide documentation, not cosmetically hide material conditions before a sale.

### Seasonal and absentee ownership

- The vacation-home page identifies the right problem but needs a real monitoring system: named local contact, access method, photo deliverables, storm trigger, escalation levels, spending authority, insurer/HOA notice, and completion reporting.

### Publishing quality

- All eight pages need H1 headings, current dates, authoritative sources, decision tables, contextual links, service boundaries, and more varied prose.
- Repeated Short Answer, common mistake, better questions, professional guidance, and final takeaway structures remain visible.
