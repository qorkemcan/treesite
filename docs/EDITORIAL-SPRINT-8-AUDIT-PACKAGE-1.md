## Current-source checkpoints

### Active nests are a condition, not a statewide calendar ban

The U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service states that destroying a nest containing eggs or chicks, or one still used by dependent young, can violate the Migratory Bird Treaty Act.

The article should distinguish:

- active nest,
- inactive nest,
- protected migratory bird,
- eagle-specific rules,
- state-listed species,
- immediate life-safety conditions,
- local tree authority.

It should not suggest that every nest, every bird, or every season creates the same legal result.

### The 5-15-90 statistic is not supported by the official sources reviewed

Current OSHA and NIOSH felling materials support:

- hazard assessment,
- work-zone control,
- retreat planning,
- falling-object awareness,
- electrical separation,
- keeping bystanders away.

The audit did not locate an official OSHA or NIOSH source for the article’s exact claim that:

- 5 feet,
- 15 seconds,
- 90 percent of severe incidents,

form a recognized verified rule.

The numerical claim should not remain as a factual anchor without a reliable primary source. The page should be reframed around verified felling danger-zone principles or retired if the rule cannot be sourced.

### One-sided dieback is a clue, not a deterministic root map

UF/IFAS notes that root damage on one side may produce dieback on the same side in some species, but in other species branch death may appear elsewhere in the crown.

The revision should avoid implying that each canopy side is predictably supplied by roots directly below that side.

### Stump grinding and biological regrowth are separate outcomes

UF/IFAS notes that invasive and strongly sprouting woody species vary in their ability to regrow from remaining stump or lateral-root tissue.

The article must distinguish:

- new root growth,
- stump sprouts,
- root suckers,
- seedlings,
- nearby connected stems,
- physical stump completion,
- species-specific herbicide control.

Any herbicide discussion must follow the exact current EPA-approved label and protect desirable connected roots.

### Florida tree-removal law needs exact current language

Florida Statute 163.045 uses:

- a qualifying onsite assessment,
- an ISA Certified Arborist or Florida licensed landscape architect,
- a signed document,
- a defined single-family detached residential property,
- the unacceptable-risk threshold,
- removal as the only practical means of reducing risk below moderate.

The statute does not erase ownership, HOA, mangrove, wetland, right-of-way, utility, wildlife, or other legal gates.

### Pruning sealer is generally not the default

UF/IFAS states that ordinary pruning sealants are not supported by science and can interfere with natural wound closure.

The rewrite should remove or heavily qualify:

- cosmetic wound coating,
- broad moisture-trapping claims presented as universal,
- a homeowner-level three-cut tutorial inside a sealer article,
- hollow-sound testing.

The page should focus on proper pruning objective, branch collar protection, large-wound limitations, storm tears, palms, disease-specific exceptions, and monitoring.

### Staking belongs primarily to establishment, not mature-tree rescue

UF/IFAS guidance supports staking or guying when a newly planted root ball needs temporary stabilization, with attachments that do not damage bark and removal after establishment.

A mature tree that suddenly leans, lifts soil, or moves at the root plate is a stability and target-risk problem, not a homeowner staking project.

### A quote page should define a measurable contract scope

The quote article has strong commercial value but should define:

- exact tree and work objective,
- pruning specification,
- equipment and access,
- utilities,
- traffic,
- permits and authority,
- debris and log disposition,
- stump depth and area,
- chips and final grade,
- property protection,
- change orders,
- insurance evidence,
- payment and cancellation terms,
- exclusions.

Avoid implying that every Florida tree provider holds one universal state contractor license.
