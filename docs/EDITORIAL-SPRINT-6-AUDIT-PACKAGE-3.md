## Main findings

### Visual symptoms need an observation-to-diagnosis boundary

- Leaf spots, bark growth, cracks, and needle loss can be described from the ground, but the current pages sometimes move too quickly from appearance to a probable cause.
- Each rewrite needs a structured observation workflow: species or tree group, affected plant part, distribution, timing, recent weather or site change, progression, and whole-tree condition.
- Remote visual guidance must not substitute for plant-diagnostic testing, qualified tree-risk assessment, utility control, or emergency response.

### Plant health and tree risk are different questions

- A cosmetic or foliar symptom does not automatically make a tree hazardous.
- A tree-risk concern can exist even when leaves look normal if the trunk, root plate, major unions, or targets create a serious failure consequence.
- Every page needs separate lanes for plant diagnosis, routine care, pruning, tree-risk assessment, emergency response, and removal.

### Water guidance needs tree-age, root-zone, soil, and drainage distinctions

- Newly planted root balls, establishing trees, and mature trees do not share one watering method.
- Watering bags should be treated as temporary delivery tools, not as universal prescriptions.
- The overwatered-versus-underwatered page needs a better moisture-check workflow, rainfall and irrigation review, planting-depth and drainage diagnosis, and clear limits on homeowner interpretation.

### Identification must become a real hub

- The current identification page is a broad overview, not a usable identification system.
- It needs Florida region, leaf arrangement, leaf type, bark, reproductive structures, growth habit, habitat, and multiple-photo guidance.
- Native, nonnative, naturalized, invasive, palm, conifer, broadleaf, fruit, and ornamental categories must remain distinct.
- A probable name should lead to a species-specific source and condition review, not an automatic pruning or removal recommendation.
